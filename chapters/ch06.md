## Chapter 6: Slinkies and Loop-the-Loop

The listening logs were Priya's idea, which meant they had columns.

"Station, frequency, mode, time," she said, holding up her graph-paper notebook at the first meeting of February, "and a one-word summary. The summary is the important column. It builds the habit of having an opinion."

"Mine says *loud*," said Theo.

"Your entire column says *loud*," said Priya. "You can't write *loud* for everything you hear."

"I can when it's loud."

Outside the storeroom windows it was full dark at five o'clock, which was February's whole personality — the flagpole halyard ticking against the pole — and inside Room 114-B the radiator clanked its one-note Morse, and the club compared what they'd heard that week.

Jun went first, because hers was short. From her bedroom window above the shop, with Abuelo Rudy's little Yaesu, she could hear the Tuesday-night net on the Maple Run repeater like a phone call. She'd logged nine check-ins, a net-control operator with a cold, and one mobile who gave his location as "the overpass" and then drove out of the conversation mid-sentence.

Priya's father had helped her hang forty feet of enameled wire from her bedroom window to the maple — "temporarily," her mother said — and Priya had logged a week of twenty meters after homework: a contest station in Puerto Rico working the world like an auctioneer, two Australians fading in and out like breathing. Her summary column said *inadequate* beside her own antenna, which Jun knew meant the wire was staying.

Theo had gotten a handheld for Christmas — "lightly used, the guy said" — and now heard the repeater from the Marsh basement, where the furnace made the squelch flutter and his little brothers kept barging in to ask if he was talking to spies. "I tell them yes," Theo said. "It builds character."

"Bea," said Priya, turning a page. "Your turn."

Bea sat in the second row, as always, with her steno pad — spiral at the top, small precise letters, almost nothing ever crossed out. She opened it, looked at the page for a moment, and turned it around so they could see.

It was blank.

"It's not blank at school," Bea said. "At school I hear what you hear. At home—" she closed the pad "—at home it's static. The handheld hears nothing in the apartment. The radio Mr. O lent me hears nothing in the apartment. The building eats it." She said it the way she'd say *the bus was late*, and somehow that was worse. "I've never heard forty meters," she said. "Or eighty. The night bands. To me they're a rumor."

The radiator clanked. Nobody wrote anything.

"Tell them the rest," said Mr. O, from the desk.

Bea took a small breath. "The lease at Pinewall says no antennas. Page four. No exterior antenna, aerial, wire, mast, flagpole, or satellite dish of any size. No cables through exterior walls or windows. Nothing attached to or hung over a balcony railing." She recited it flatly, like a phone number. "My grandmother reads leases. She reads them twice. The deposit was two months, and we are not people with two months to lose."

Theo was outraged in the theatrical way of a man who had found his episode. "That's censorship. The airwaves belong to the people. It's in the spirit of the Constitution, probably."

"The spirit of the Constitution has no standing in landlord-tenant court," said Priya, but her pencil was out, tapping her teeth — her tell for thinking hard. "No *exterior* antenna," she said. "That's the interesting word. Legally interesting."

"That's the word I heard," said Bea.

"Hold on," Jun said.

Because there it was — the feeling she got at the thrift shop when somebody set a box on the counter and apologized for it. *Interior.* An attic was interior. A plant stand was interior, give or take a pane of glass. She looked at Bea. "Does Pinewall have an attic?"

"A crawlspace." Bea's eyes narrowed the way they did when she was following a thread. "There's a hatch in my bedroom closet ceiling. A light bulb on a pull chain." A pause. "Nails."

"Jun," said Priya, "a forty-meter dipole is sixty-five and a half feet. You taught me that."

"So we don't build a forty-meter dipole," Jun said. "We build a forty-meter *compromise*."

That was when Mr. O took the little black portable out of the desk drawer — the same radio that had pulled Brazil out of the air in September on twelve feet of hookup wire. He set it down in front of Bea with the care of a man laying down a tool.

"Receiving is free," he said. "It has always been free. Any person may listen to anything they can pull out of the sky — no license, no call sign, no permission slip. The license is for *transmitting*, and transmitting is something you four do only with a licensed control operator in the room — Mr. Webb or me, at a station belonging to one of us. That rule does not bend for weather, leases, or plant stands. Understood?"

Four nods, in four different tempos.

"Then as to what you build for *receiving*—" Mr. O capped his pen "—I am a physics teacher. I know nothing about plant stands."

Theo was already up, marker in hand. "Then it's settled," he announced, and wrote it on the whiteboard in letters four inches high:

OPERATION HOUSEPLANT.

"Don't name it," said Priya.

"It's named," said Theo.

In the hallway afterward, Jun texted her mother: *do we have any Slinkies in the toy bin. the metal kind. it's important*

The reply came before she reached the stairs. *Why.*

Then, a moment later: *yes. two. $1.50 and you pay like everyone else.*

Jun put the phone away smiling. Seventy-five cents of antenna was still antenna.

Saturday meant Si's truck, which smelled of pipe tobacco and wet dog, and coffee from the thermos that Resistor regarded, from his half of the bench seat, with open pity.

"So the girl's in a concrete box," Si said, by way of hello, when they'd stamped the snow off in the barn doorway. He had clearly gotten the whole situation from Mr. O. "Building eats VHF. Landlord eats leases. What do you want from me?"

"Stealth," said Theo. "You're the most stealth-adjacent person we know."

"I put antennas on forest-service lookouts," Si said, "where the rangers wanted them invisible so tourists would photograph the view instead. I put one on a lighthouse you would not believe. Invisible was my entire career." He poured coffee into a mug that said ASK ME ABOUT MY ANTENNAS, which was a lie, because he hated being asked. The little kerosene heater ticked away in the corner, losing its argument with the barn. "Show me what you brought."

Jun produced the Slinkies — two, metal, from the bottom of the Second Wind toy bin, seventy-five cents each plus her mother's lecture about paying retail for her own merchandise. One had a kink halfway down, like a broken finger. Si turned them over in his big hands and something in his face went soft and professional.

"Slinky," he said. "Steel, plated, decent conductor, and it stretches. People think these are toys. These are *helical resonators* with a marketing department." He handed one to Bea. "How tall's the crawlspace?"

"You can't stand," Bea said. "You can kneel."

"Good," said Si. "Then nobody will be tempted to stand up through Babcia's kitchen ceiling."

"Si." Priya had located a sheet of drywall leaning by the workbench and a marker, which meant class was in session whether Si liked it or not. "Explain why a short antenna works *at all*. Sixty-five and a half feet is resonant on forty meters. Twenty feet of toy is not. The radiation resistance has to fall off a cliff."

"It does," Si said.

"Then where does the power go?"

"Into the tuner, mostly. Into the wire. A little bit—" he held up two fingers close together "—into the sky."

"That's terrible."

"That's February at a no-antenna apartment," Si said. "But here's what your math hasn't told you yet, because you keep thinking about transmitting. On receive, the sky is *loud* — static from storms a thousand miles away, every power line in Maple Run. A receiver doesn't need an efficient antenna. It needs an antenna that hears louder than the room it's sitting in. The girl's concrete box is the problem. Get twenty feet of wire up off the floor and away from the wiring in the walls, and the sky will outshout the building. It'll still be a bad antenna. But a bad antenna up in the clear beats a good antenna in a drawer. Write it down: *any antenna beats no antenna.* That's the whole law of attics."

Priya wrote it down. Then she underlined it, which for Priya was a standing ovation.

The tuner came off a shelf — an old manual one the size of a breadbox, two big knobs and a cross-needle meter, with a dent Si said was a long story involving a mule. "Rule one," he said, setting it on the bench. "This box does not fix the antenna. Nothing fixes the antenna short of sixty-five feet of wire in the clear. What this box does is *persuade* — it turns whatever strange impedance the toys present into something the radio will accept. The radio is persuaded. The sky is not persuaded. The sky takes what it gets." He tapped the dented case. "People buy these thinking they make antennas resonant. They make *radios* happy. Different thing. Write that down too."

"Phase one," said Jun. "Attic. This week."

"Phase two," said Si, and reached under the workbench.

What he dragged out was a length of three-quarter-inch copper pipe, ten feet of it, green at the joints and kinked in two places, the ghost of some plumber's abandoned project. "Copper's four dollars a pound," Si said. "So this pipe is the most valuable thing in this barn that isn't the dog." Resistor thumped his tail on the rag rug. "We are going to bend it into a circle three feet across, tune it with the variable capacitor out of that dead clock radio Priya's been glaring at, and feed it with a gamma match — which you will please pronounce *gamma* and not *grandma*."

"Why a circle?" Bea asked.

It was the first thing she'd said since the truck, and Si pointed at her like she'd won a prize. "Because a small loop is a *current machine*. A dipole's a voltage machine — all the action's at the ends. A loop runs the same current all the way around, and current is what radiates. Three feet across — call it nine point four feet of pipe. Not even a tenth of a wavelength on forty meters. Shouldn't work at all. Works like a champ, *if* the conductor's fat and the joints are clean, because whatever resistance you leave in there eats your signal for lunch. Every ohm is a thief." He rolled the pipe with his boot. "It'll be narrow. Sharp as a razor. Tunes forty through twenty on the capacitor and hears like something ten times its size. And—" he looked at Bea "—it can sit on a balcony holding a flowerpot, and no lease ever written says one word about it."

Bea looked at the pipe for a long moment.

"Loop-the-loop," she said.

Theo gasped — actually gasped, one hand to his chest. "She named it. She never names anything. Operation Houseplant has a *flagship*."

---

They did the attic on Wednesday afternoon, because Wednesday was when Babcia had her circle at St. Casimir's, and because Bea had told her grandmother, with complete precision, that the radio club was coming over to help with a physics project.

It was, Jun reflected, technically true. Physics was extremely involved.

Pinewall was three stories of brick and balconies on the north side of town, the kind of building that had been cheap and sturdy in 1974 and was now just cheap. Bea's apartment was on the top floor, which was the whole reason the plan was possible: above her bedroom closet, past a square of painted plywood, lay the crawlspace — a low, cold, faintly dusty darkness that ran the length of the building under the roof.

They went up one at a time off Babcia's step stool: Jun first, with her headlamp, then Priya, then Bea with the sack of parts. Theo waited below with the coax and the expression of a man whose talents were being wasted. "I'm the ground crew," he said, to no one.

The crawlspace was everything Bea had advertised. The pull-chain bulb threw shadows between the joists; underfoot was blown insulation the color of old dryer lint, joist tops showing like rails; and the underside of the roof deck bristled with shingle nails, hundreds of little iron teeth.

"Step only on the joists," Jun said. "The stuff between the joists is ceiling. Step between the joists and you are in Babcia's kitchen, and that is a conversation I am not having on your behalf. And keep your head down unless you want a nail in your hat."

"Noted," said Priya, who had already bumped hers once and was holding still with the offended dignity of a cat.

The plan was simple, which didn't mean it was easy. A center insulator — half a plastic coat hanger, drilled on the barn drill press — went up first, screwed to a rafter in the middle of the run. From it, one Slinky ran east along the rafters, stretched to about ten feet, its far end tied off with nylon twine. The second Slinky ran west the same way, kink and all. Each end of the salvaged RG-58 went to one toy: center conductor east, braid west, wrapped twice and clamped with alligator clips for now, solder later if it proved itself. At the insulator, Jun coiled the coax into eight turns, six inches across, and zip-tied the loop — the choke, Si's standing order, so the feedline itself wouldn't become part of the antenna and drag the apartment's wiring into the act.

"That's it?" Priya said, surveying twenty feet of stretched toy in a cold dark space. "That's the whole antenna?"

"That's the whole antenna."

"Radios used to be furniture," Priya said. "People had *consoles* for this."

They dropped the coax down through the hatch, connected it through Si's breadbox tuner to Mr. O's portable, and Theo — ground crew, promoted — switched it on with due ceremony.

Static. Thin, dead, second-hand static — the exact same nothing as the whip antenna leaning in the corner.

"Well," said Theo. "We have built the world's largest earplug."

Up in the attic, Jun and Priya and Bea crawled the run with the headlamp, looking for sin. It was Bea who found it — she had gone quiet and still at the far end, the way she did in front of knots. "Here," she said.

The kinked Slinky, sagging under its own uneven weight, had laid two of its coils across the galvanized furnace duct that ran along the west side. Steel on steel, in three places.

"It's touching metal," Bea said.

"It's *shorted* to metal," said Priya, arriving on her knees. "The duct is part of the antenna now, and the duct goes into the ground."

They re-tied the west end two rafters higher, a hand's width of air between the coils and every piece of metal in the place. Jun took the sag out of the middle with a third tie. Down below, still connected, Theo tuned the portable across forty meters out of habit, and the static *swelled*.

It did not become music. It became *weather*. The dead hiss came up like a tide, thick and granular, and inside it, barely, a slow ditter of Morse — faint as a heartbeat — and then, at the low end of the band, the edge of a voice, wavering in and out of the noise like a man talking in his sleep one county over.

"It's alive," Theo said up through the hatch, in the hush people use for baby animals. "It's not loud. But it's *alive*."

They took turns with the tuner knobs, learning the breadbox's moods — peak the noise, peak it again, smaller knob, smaller — and the faint voice gained maybe an S-unit and a half, the difference between *a rumor* and *a rumor with words in it*. It was, Jun thought, exactly what Si had promised, to the inch: a bad antenna, up in the clear, beating the good radio that sat deaf on the floor.

She called Si from the hallway while Bea coiled the slack coax along the closet baseboard with museum neatness.

"It works," Jun said. "Barely. Two S-units of barely. There's a voice down at the bottom of forty that sounds like it's being transmitted from inside a sock."

"That's the deal," Si said, and she could hear the coffee in his voice. "Barely is the deal. The toys load up on forty through the tuner — that's what the box is *for*. The toys are a keyhole, not a window. She wants the window, you finish my circle."

Bea said it differently, later, when the three of them were pulling coats on in the flat gray-blue light of a February evening. She stood in the closet doorway and looked up at the dark square of the hatch, behind which twenty feet of stolen radio now ran the length of the building.

"It's a keyhole," she said — not disappointed; precise, like she'd found the word it had always been. "You can't see much through a keyhole. But you can tell there's a room."

The Slinky dipole stayed. The attic was interior — Priya had checked the lease twice, with the solemnity of a clerk stamping a passport — and the coax ran down inside the closet, behind the winter coats, emerging only at floor level, humble as a phone charger. Babcia, arriving home with a net bag of groceries, found four teenagers drinking cocoa at her kitchen table, doing their physics homework in a silence so studious it was practically visible.

She looked at them for a long moment over her glasses.

"Good," she said, and put on the kettle, and Jun did not breathe out until her back was turned.

---

The loop took two Saturdays, which was about average for Operation Houseplant.

The parts list, as Priya recorded it on the drywall in her clerk's hand:

- *10 ft of 3/4-inch copper pipe (barn, free, green)*
- *one variable capacitor, ex–clock radio, $3, as-is bin*
- *one PVC junction box, gray (hardware store, 90 cents — the only new part)*
- *one SO-239 chassis connector (Si's coffee-can of connectors)*
- *copper strap for the gamma arm (flattened pipe offcut)*
- *coax, twine, zip ties, paint — the usual suspects*

Bending the circle was Jun's job, because Jun had done conduit for the theater and knew about going slow. Si had produced a form: the rim off an old manure spreader, almost exactly three feet across, bolted to the bench. "I will not ask," Priya said. "Good," said Si. Jun worked the pipe around the rim an inch at a time, and it came along sweet and true until the last foot, where the old kink decided its moment had come and folded with a sound like a soda can giving up.

Everyone stood there looking at it. The kink was bad — creased nearly shut.

"Well," Jun said, stomach somewhere around her boots. "I killed the most valuable thing in the barn that isn't the dog."

Si came over and looked at the crease for a long time, the way men of his profession look at things they are deciding not to call mistakes. Then he picked up the pipe cutter.

"A loop doesn't close," he said. "Everybody pictures a ring. It's not a ring — it's a circle with a *gap*, and the capacitor lives in the gap. That's where the tuning happens." He cut the kinked foot cleanly out of the circle. "You didn't kill it. You marked where the box goes. Nine point four feet of pipe, three feet across — right on the number." He handed her the cutter. "Cut the other end square and stop apologizing to plumbing."

The capacitor came out of the clock radio in twenty minutes of happy destruction — Jun with the screwdriver, Bea with the parts tray, keeping the screws in rows like a surgical nurse. It was a beautiful little machine: two gangs of interleaved aluminum plates, like a pair of metal combs that had been persuaded to mate, turning on a shaft with the original tuning knob and its slow reduction drive. Turn the knob and the plates meshed and unmeshed, and with them, the capacitance rose and fell.

"Three hundred sixty-five picofarads," Priya said, reading the numbers she'd coaxed out of the internet at lunch. "That's the big gang, fully meshed. On a good day. Some of these plates are bent, so it's a good-day number."

"Mount it," said Si.

They mounted it in the gray PVC box, knob out the back, the sawn ends of the circle bolting to the capacitor's terminals with copper straps and every bit of slack trimmed away. The gamma match was last: coax braid to the bottom of the loop, center conductor to a foot of flattened copper strap that ran parallel to the pipe an inch off of it, tapped partway around — Si refusing to say exactly where, on the grounds that they'd learn more finding it. The whole ring stood in the bench vise like the world's most athletic hula hoop, and Theo circled it taking photographs from angles.

"Test it," said Si. "Ears first. Hook it to the little radio and turn the knob slow. You're listening for the band to come *up* — maximum noise. That's resonance. That's the whole secret handshake of loops."

Jun clipped the coax to Mr. O's portable, set it on twenty meters, and rolled the knob.

It was uncanny, the first time — the noise floor rising out of nothing as the plates meshed, swelling to a peak, then falling away past it, like a flashlight beam sweeping a wall. Priya demanded three repetitions, logging knob positions. They found the peak on twenty, crisp and narrow. They found it on fifteen. Bea, saying nothing, turned the band switch to forty and began rolling the knob toward full mesh, slower and slower, and the noise climbed — and stopped climbing.

The plates ran fully home. The peak wasn't there. The knob was done, and forty meters was still a low, flat murmur.

"It's not coming down far enough," Bea said. "It's stuck."

She was right. They swept it four times, and four times the noise peak stalled out high — somewhere above ten megahertz, an empty no-man's-land of beeps and time ticks, nowhere near the 7.15 they wanted. The loop tuned twenty beautifully and then ran out of capacitor like a car running out of road.

Nobody said anything for a moment. The heater ticked. Resistor sighed in his sleep.

"Priya," said Si, mildly, nodding at the drywall. It was as close as he ever came to explaining.

Priya was already moving, marker in hand. "Resonance," she said. "Frequency goes as one over the square root of the capacitance. We peak at—" she checked her log "—about ten point five megahertz with everything the capacitor has. We want seven point one five. That's a ratio of about one point four in frequency—" the marker squeaked "—and you square it for capacitance. One point four squared is about two. We need roughly *twice* the capacitance we've got. Seven hundred picofarads, give or take. The variable gives three-sixty-five." She turned around. "We're short by the whole capacitor. Twice."

"Can we take plates out of the other gang and—" Theo started.

"You add capacitance in parallel," Priya said, "not by surgery."

"So we add capacitance in parallel," said Si, and tipped the coffee-can of parts onto the bench with a crash. "Junk box rule: the fix is in the can. You just have to believe hard enough and sort."

They sorted. It took twenty minutes and Priya's meter and one argument about whether a mysterious orange disk was a capacitor or a coin, but they came up with them: two silver-mica capacitors, little stamped dominoes from the inside of some dead transmitter, one marked 270 and one 62, soldered side by side on a scrap of perfboard.

"Three hundred thirty-two picofarads," she announced. "A *padding capacitor*. It sits across the variable, pads it up. Three-sixty-five plus three-thirty-two — call it seven hundred at full mesh, and the variable swings us around the band from there."

It went in across the loop terminals, on a short clip lead, because Priya had seen one step further than the rest of them: "We can't leave it in permanently," she said. "With the padding in, we'll never get back *up* to twenty — too much capacitance. So it's a jumper. Clip it on for forty. Unclip for twenty. The world's smallest band switch."

Bea clipped it on. She rolled the knob back from full mesh, slowly, and this time — two-thirds of the way in, plates breathing easy — the forty-meter band came up out of the floor like a sunrise: a wall of noise, and in it, unmistakably, voices, stacked on top of each other like tenants in an apartment building.

"There," Bea said.

"There," Si agreed.

They unclipped the jumper and found twenty again, right where they'd left it. Clipped it: forty. Unclipped: twenty. Theo applauded the capacitor, which accepted with a small scratchy sound.

The razor showed itself that same afternoon, and it was Priya who measured it, who could not leave a peak unquantified. Tuned dead-on a forty-meter signal, she rocked the portable's dial and watched the noise fall off a cliff: tune away and the peak was simply gone. "Call it fifteen kilohertz wide," she said. "Total. On a band that's three hundred wide. Jun. This thing is a *razor*." She did the second half of the math out loud, for the room: low loss and a tiny radiation resistance meant a sky-high Q — the quality factor, how sharply the thing rang — and a sky-high Q meant a bandwidth you could shave with. "Every time we move the radio more than a few kilohertz, we re-peak the loop. That's the tax. Efficiency and bandwidth, pick one."

"Pick efficiency," said Si. "Always pick efficiency. Knobs are free."

The gamma tap took an hour of sliding the copper strap an eighth of an inch at a time under the analyzer's little screen — Si leaning on the bench, offering no opinions whatsoever — until the SWR dipped to one point four to one at the bottom of forty and they all pretended they hadn't been holding their breath. "On transmit, a loop this size puts real voltage across that little capacitor," Si said, while they tidied. "At a hundred watts it would arc and singe your eyebrows. On receive it's a kitten. Ears only at the apartment — because of the *law*, not the arcs. The arcs are just free motivation."

At dusk, because Si was Si, they carried the ring out to the barn doorway and clipped it to his IC-7300, and forty meters rolled in out of the darkening sky thick as a brick — nets, contests, ragchews — the loop doing, on nine point four feet of pipe, a shameless impression of an antenna ten times its size.

"Down an S-unit or two from the big wires," Si admitted. "For a hula hoop, that's a miracle—" he caught himself, coughed "—that's *physics.*" He looked at the ring, then at Bea. "Hearing's believing. Get it on her balcony."

The disguise took one more evening in Jun's garage, and it was, Theo declared, the podcast-worthy capper of the whole operation: a plant stand.

Not a fake plant stand. A real one — PVC pipe and fittings from the scrap bin, glued into a three-legged stand the exact height to hold a three-foot ring at railing level, painted flat black with the half-can of spray paint Jun's mom used for picture frames. The copper circle seated into it like it had been born there. Theo contributed a plastic ivy garland from the dollar bin and wound it around the ring's lower half with the concentration of a set dresser; Bea contributed a terra-cotta pot, empty, into which Jun set a paver from the salvage yard — twenty pounds of ballast against the February wind that came down the alley like it had been sent for.

"It's sculpture," Theo said, stepping back. "It's giving *modern trellis*. It's extremely HOA."

"It's not a homeowners' association," said Bea. "It's a lease."

"It's giving lease," Theo said.

They installed it on a Thursday after school, with Babcia at her sister's and the sky going dark at five. The stand went on the balcony *inside* the railing — nothing over the edge, nothing hung, nothing drilled; Bea read the lease clause aloud while they worked, the way other people read instructions. The coax came in at the corner of the sliding door, through a gap in the weatherstripping that Priya closed with a foam draft block she'd carved to fit with a steak knife. The door still closed. Almost.

"'Almost' is doing a lot of work in that sentence," Priya said, and folded a towel along the track.

The roundtable had been Si's idea, delivered by phone like a lumber order: "Seven point one seven five, Thursday, eight o'clock. The Owl and I will be on. Gus from Boise, if the band's kind. Bring your ears." So at eight o'clock the four of them were sitting on Bea's bedroom floor, coats still on because the balcony door leaked February around its foam gasket, Mr. O's little portable in the middle of the circle like a campfire. Through the glass, the copper ring stood silhouetted against the parking-lot lights, ivy and all.

Bea did the honors, because it was her antenna. She rolled the capacitor knob, hunting the peak by ear the way Si had taught them — and for a bad thirty seconds there was nothing, just the thin apartment static, and Jun's heart sank, until Priya said "*razor*," and Bea remembered: she rocked the portable's dial a hair, re-peaked, rocked it again — and the band opened like a door.

The first thing they got was the buzz — some Pinewall fixture, the parking-lot floods or the elevator motor — a low growl smeared across the band. Bea got up, slid the door open on its towel, rotated the ring ten degrees, and the growl dropped into the loop's null and *died*, and underneath it, clear as church, three men were talking about snow tires.

"...mounted them in October this year, I learned my lesson. This is W7JUNK, Whiskey Seven Juliett Uniform November Kilo — ten minutes, Dave, you're up."

"November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima," said Mr. O's voice, dry as toast, from forty miles and one tall mast away. "You're five nine, Si, as if you didn't know. Gus is still in there, I heard him between the fades."

"Kilo Seven Golf Tango Bravo," said a third voice, cheerful and gravelly, swimming up out of the noise and sinking again. "Boise. Gus. You're both five seven tonight — you're *breathing*, boys."

"That's the sky breathing, not us," said Si. "Forty meters in February. She gives, she takes."

They talked about snow tires, and about Gus's dog, who was afraid of the vacuum and not afraid of thunder, and about the price of copper pipe — "don't get me started," Si said, "I know a pipe that's practically a retirement account" — and once Si said, in a voice that pretended to be addressed to the band and wasn't: "We understand there's a new listener or two out there in Maple Run tonight. Somebody with excellent taste in garden sculpture. Welcome to forty meters. It only gets better from here."

And Bea grinned.

Jun saw it happen and would remember it exactly: not the small, polite, quarter-inch smile Bea deployed at teachers, but the real thing, bright as a struck bell, her whole face coming undone with it, sitting cross-legged on her own bedroom floor in her winter coat, listening to three old men she'd mostly met argue about snow tires on an antenna built out of a plumber's mistake and a dead clock radio.

"She grinned," Theo said afterward, in the awed tone of a man reporting a whale sighting.

"I heard a joke," Bea said, which would remain her position.

The razor kept them honest. Twice the conversation drifted — once when Gus said "let's slide up five, there's a net creeping in below us," and the roundtable simply *vanished* until Bea, grinning still, reached over and re-peaked the loop and pulled them back out of the dark. "Every few kilohertz," Priya whispered, delighted. "Forever. It's the tax." Bea did not look like a person paying a tax. She looked like a person who had been given a very small, very sharp knife and intended to whittle the whole sky.

They had planned to be gone by nine; Babcia's Thursday circle ended at nine-thirty, twelve minutes' walk from St. Casimir's. They were, of course, not gone by nine, because Theo laughed — at Si's lighthouse joke, the one with the foghorn — laughed in his full podcast baritone at 9:07, and at 9:09 they heard the apartment door.

Every head came up. Bea killed the volume, which only made it worse, because a bedroom going silent is the loudest sound in the world.

The bedroom door opened. Babcia stood in it in her black coat and her church scarf, and she took the whole scene in without hurry: four teenagers on the floor in a ring around a little black radio. The coax, white as a lie, running across the rug to the balcony door. The towel in the door track. And through the glass, clear in the parking-lot light, a three-foot copper circle with ivy wound on it, standing in a plant stand, doing a poor impression of decor.

The silence lasted about a year.

"Shortwave," Babcia said. It was not a question, and it was not the word Jun had braced for.

"This is the physics project," Bea said. Her voice was steady. Her hands, Jun saw, were not.

"And the plant stand?"

"Also the physics project."

Babcia looked at the ring for another long moment, and something moved through her face that none of them could read — it was not anger; it went the other direction, somewhere older. She set her church handbag down on the dresser with terrible calm.

"Your friends go home now," she said. "It is a school night. We will talk, you and I."

"Yes, Babcia."

They put on their coats in a silence so careful it hurt. In the hallway, Theo waited until the apartment door closed, then whispered, with genuine reverence: "Your grandmother is *terrifying*."

"Yes," Bea said, and held the stairwell door for them, and if Jun's headlamp caught, for half a second, the corner of her mouth — well. The hall light was bad. It proved nothing.

---

Bea told them nothing all week. "She said *we will talk*," was the whole report, delivered Tuesday with the finality of a vault door. "We talked."

The following Wednesday, at four o'clock exactly — Mr. O noted the time twice — there was a knock on the door of Room 114-B, and Babcia came in carrying a foil-covered roasting pan and sour cream, in the black coat, with Bea behind her wearing the expression of a defendant.

"Mrs. Kowalczyk," said Mr. O, rising. "You're very welcome here."

"You may call me Babcia," she said. "Everyone does eventually." She set the pan on the desk and peeled back the foil, and the smell went through the storeroom like a benevolent occupying army: pierogi, dozens of them, rows of little crimped half-moons shining with butter, onion glistening on top.

"These are for the club," she said. "There are rules."

She gave the room the inspection first. She walked the QSL corkboard slowly, reading the call signs like names on a memorial. She stood a long time in front of the old photographs — the boys in skinny ties, the tower on the roof. She put one finger, briefly, on the dented corner of the Kenwood TS-520S, glowing its aquarium green, and Jun thought of her mother doing the exact same thing, and had to look at the floor.

Then Babcia turned around, folded her hands, and told them.

"My husband — Bea's dziadek — was a radio officer. Merchant marine. Polish Ocean Lines, out of Gdynia, thirty-one years." She said it to the room, but she was watching Bea. "Your grandfather talked to *ships*," she said. "All night he sat in the radio room with the headphones, and the ships talked back. The dits and dahs — he read the code like the newspaper. Faster than the newspaper. The newspaper was always late." A pause, precise as a seam. "Thursday night I opened her door and I heard it through the static — the band, the voices, the way they say the letters. I have not heard that sound in that house in nineteen years. I stood in the hall, and I was twenty-three years old, listening at the radio-room door."

Nobody moved. Even the radiator had the decency to hold its tongue.

"So. The rules." Babcia counted them off on fingers that had crimped ten thousand pierogi. "Nothing over the railing. Nothing drilled. Nothing transmits from my home until the government and your Mr. Okafor both say so — *receiving*, he explained to me on the telephone, is free, so receiving we have. Grades stay where they are. And the sculpture—" she looked at Bea "—if it is a planter, it will have a plant. A real one. The plastic ivy is a lie, and in spring you will put in real ivy, and you will water it."

"Yes, Babcia," said Bea.

"The attic," Babcia added, "I read the lease again. The attic is *interior*." She said it like a woman who had found the loophole forty-eight hours before Priya did and kept it in her apron pocket. "The wires may stay. They are tidy. I looked."

She ate one pierogi, standing, pronounced the room acceptable, and refused, with Polish finality, to sit down. At the door she paused and looked back at the four of them around the desk with paper plates and shining faces.

"He would like this," she said. "The junk. The—" a gesture at everything "—the *making do*. Ships' radios broke at sea, and there is no store at sea." And then, to Bea, softer, in the doorway: "Kochanie. The pan comes home. With reviews."

She left. The room exhaled in four parts.

"Reviews," Theo said reverently, through a pierogi. "We're being *reviewed*. By a professional."

Mr. O waited until the footsteps had faded — he was a careful man — and then took a second pierogi and said, as if to the whiteboard: "March is six weeks of weather in a trench coat. The county runs Skywarn spotter training the first Saturday, and this club will attend, because a club that can hear should know what it's hearing." He looked up, in the direction of the roof, where the pop-can dipole rode out the winter on its broomstick. "And before the storms come, this room needs a better ear on the repeater. Something permanent. Something," he said, "with a permit."

"Operation Rooftop," said Theo, with his mouth full.

"Don't name it," said Priya.

Jun was watching Bea. Bea was wrapping the foil back over the pan — two pierogi left, an engineering problem for later — with her small, exact hands, and she said, quietly, to nobody in particular: "She kept his headphones. They're in her sewing drawer, in the good tin. I used to put them on when I was little, before I knew what they were for."

She closed the foil. She picked up the pan like a trophy. And on the desk beside her, the little black portable murmured away on forty meters, band noise rising and falling like surf under a pier, all the voices of the evening coming home.
