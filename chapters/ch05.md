## Chapter 5: The Night the Sky Opened

Si Webb's truck had a heater that sounded like a coffee percolator arguing with a hammer, and on the second Saturday of January, Jun Alvarez sat wedged in the middle of the crew cab's back seat with Priya's notebook corner digging into her thigh and her breath fogging the window, and she could not remember being happier about being cold.

"Let the record show," Theo said from the far window, in the voice he used for the *Marsh Hour* cold opens, "that we are driving twenty minutes out of town, in January, to listen to a radio band that Mr. Webb himself described as — quote — 'probably dead until five o'clock.'"

"Probably," Si said, from behind the wheel, without turning around. Resistor, his ancient brown mutt, occupied the passenger seat like paid security and did not move. "Dead's doing a lot of work in that sentence. It's not dead. It's *asleep*. There's a difference, and the difference is the whole trip."

In the truck bed, wrapped in two moving blankets and cinched down with ratchet straps, rode the Kenwood TS-520S — the club's radio, the dumpster radio, the radio that had spent the fall being scrubbed, coaxed, and prayed over in Room 114-B until it glowed green and pulled voices out of the air like it was 1978 and nothing had ever gone wrong. Behind them on Larkspur Road, Mr. O's wagon followed with the coax, the analyzer, and — Jun had been promised — coffee that was not Si's, which everyone agreed was the single most important piece of equipment in the caravan.

"Okay," Priya said. She had her graph-paper notebook open on her knees and her good pencil out, the one she didn't lend. "We've been driving for ten minutes. That's long enough that I'm allowed to ask the question without sounding impatient. Why five o'clock? Why does the band *care* what time it is? It's the same sky at three as at five."

"It is not the same sky at all," Si said. "It just looks like it, because you can't see the machinery. Priya, you like layers. How many layers is the sky?"

"Trick question," Priya said. "It's continuous."

"It's a trick *universe*," Si said, "but for radio purposes, three layers matter, and you can write this down, because I'm only saying it once and I know you'll check it." He held up three fingers off the steering wheel. "Closest in, you've got the D layer. Sixty, seventy, eighty kilometers up. It only exists while the sun's up — sunlight makes it, and it *eats* radio waves. Doesn't bounce them, doesn't bend them, absorbs them. It's why the low bands go deaf at noon. You want to think of it as fog, except the fog eats sound."

Priya's pencil was moving. "That's why the repeater doesn't care," she said slowly. "Two meters punches straight through everything, it's not going to the sky at all. But HF—"

"HF has to get past the D layer twice," Si said. "Once going up, once coming down per bounce. Above it you've got the E layer, call it a hundred kilometers, does some useful work, not the star of the show. And up top —" he lifted his hand off the wheel to the height of the visor "— the F2 layer. Three hundred kilometers. That's your trampoline. That's the layer that takes a signal from a wire in my meadow and hands it to the Pacific Ocean. Charged by the sun, same as the D. The difference is what it's made of and how long it takes to go flat."

"Go flat," Jun said. "The layers go flat at night?"

"The D layer goes flat *fast*," Si said. "Sun drops, the air down low is thick, the charged bits find each other and un-charge in minutes. The fog burns off. But the F2, three hundred kilometers up, where there's almost no air at all — that charge bleeds off slow. Hours slow. So every evening, in every place on Earth, there's a window where the fog is gone but the trampoline's still up." He glanced at Priya in the mirror. "And here's the part you'll like, because it's geometry. The places sitting right on the edge — right on the line between day and night, the sunrise line, the sunset line, the *terminator*, to use your word — those places get the best of it. Signal running along that line gets a charged sky above it and no fog below it, on both ends at once. Hams call it the gray line. And it is the cheapest, most reliable miracle in this entire hobby, and nobody under forty believes in it, because you can't buy it."

Priya's pencil had stopped. "The enhancement runs *along* the terminator," she said. "Not across it. So the stations you can work are the ones whose—" she drew a line down her page "—whose sunset is at the same time as ours, give or take. North and south. Or — wait." Jun watched her flip the globe in her head, watched her do the thing where her eyes tracked something invisible. "Or it's our sunset and *their sunrise*. The same line, on opposite sides of the planet. Somebody sitting on the other side of the terminator — the line goes through both of us."

"Now she's got it," Si said to the windshield.

"Who's on the other side of the Pacific's sunset line?" Theo asked.

"Our sunset," Priya said, "is somebody's sunrise, about—" she did the timezone math with her lips pressed together "—Japan. Japan's tomorrow morning. Australia just after." She looked up. "That's who we're going to listen for. That's the whole plan. We're going to aim a wire at the sunset and fish the edge of the world."

"I'm going to name an episode after that," Theo said.

"You're going to name an episode after *geometry?*"

"I'm going to name an episode 'Fishing the Edge of the World,'" Theo said, "and you're going to be in it, and you're going to pretend to hate it."

Jun let the argument wash past and pressed her forehead to the cold glass. Outside, the January fields went by in shades of straw and iron, the ditches ridged with old snow that had survived the week in the shade. She thought about the sky as machinery — three invisible layers stacked over the bare winter fields, charging and draining, on a schedule older than the road they were driving on. "Si," she said. "Is it always this good? The window. Every day?"

"Depends on the sun," Si said. "The sun's got moods, eleven years long, give or take. Solar cycle. We're near the top of cycle twenty-five right now — sunspots like freckles on a kid in August. More spots, more charge in the F2, higher the MUF goes."

"MUF," Priya said, pencil ready.

"Maximum usable frequency. The highest frequency the sky will still bend back down instead of letting it shoot off into space and bother the astronauts. Low-sun years, ten meters is a daytime local band and a whole lot of nothing. Peak years —" he took the last curve of Larkspur Road, and the fence line of his own place came up out of the fields, the six masts standing in the meadow like a small bare forest "—peak years, the MUF climbs past twenty-eight megahertz every decent afternoon, and ten meters turns into a worldwide party line that anybody can walk into with a wire shorter than a canoe. You four picked a good year to be born curious. I'll say that once, too."

The truck tires crunched onto frozen gravel. Resistor sat up for the first time in twenty miles, because he was home, which was the only trick he had left.

---

The Webb farm in January was all edges. The oaks at the meadow's end had dropped every leaf and stood in black calligraphy against the sky, and the barn threw a shadow that was already longer than the barn. Mr. O pulled in behind them and got out holding his travel mug up first, like a torch. "I bring offerings," he announced. "Real coffee. From a machine that was cleaned this decade."

"You brought that man here," Si said to Jun, accusatory, climbing down. "I want that on the record."

"He was invited separately," Jun said. "I checked."

The dipole itself had been born on Wednesday, in Room 114-B, in forty minutes between the bell and the late bus. It was almost embarrassing how small it was. Priya had written the arithmetic on the whiteboard the way she always did, like she was daring the universe to disagree: total length in feet is four sixty-eight divided by the frequency in megahertz; four sixty-eight over twenty-eight point four is sixteen point five feet; each leg is half of that, eight and a quarter. Sixteen and a half feet, total, for an antenna intended to reach Japan.

"That's it?" Theo had said, holding the two measured legs of copper wire — stripped from a spool of stiff #14 that had once, according to Si, been the ground wire of a fence charger — across his palms like a noodle. "The extension cord was twice this. The flagpole wire was longer than this."

"Short wavelength, short antenna," Priya had said. "Twenty-eight megahertz, the wave is ten meters long. That's why they call it ten meters. The math is almost insulting."

"It's an antenna you could lose in a laundry basket," Jun had said, coiling it, and the phrase had stuck. They'd soldered the legs to the coax center and braid through holes in a center insulator cut from a thrift-shop cutting board (Marisol had donated it; it had a crack and a picture of a rooster), crimped dog-bone end insulators from the same cutting board, and wound the choke right at the feedpoint: eight turns of the RG-58, six inches across, taped into a fat donut, so the RF went up the wire instead of surfing back down the outside of the coax to make mischief in the barn. Then Jun had coiled the whole thing — wire, insulators, choke and all — into a grocery bag, and it had ridden to school in her backpack for two days like a very unimpressive secret.

Now, in Si's meadow, in air that bit, they dumped it out of the grocery bag onto a tarp, and it looked like even less.

"Sixteen and a half feet," Si said, standing over it with his hands in his Carhartt pockets, "to talk to the other side of the planet. Humbling, ain't it. Get it wrong by six inches and it won't care — these things are forgiving, that's the other lesson — but you'll still sweat the six inches, because you're builders now, and sweating the six inches is the job." He nodded at the two oaks. "Those two. East leg and west leg. Wire'll sit broadside to the Pacific, more or less, which is where your sunset line runs. Before anybody throws anything —" and here he pointed, not at the trees, but up and over, at the power company's service drop running down the far side of the barn "—where's the wire you never go near?"

"Service drop," four voices said, in unison, with the weary music of a litany they had learned in November on the flagpole and would apparently be reciting until graduation.

"And the rule?"

"Twice the antenna's length, minimum," Priya said. "Thirty-three feet for this one. That drop is a hundred feet away."

"Spotter?"

"Me," said Bea, who had already picked up the throw line and was reading the oak limbs the way she read everything — quietly, and to the end.

The hanging was, by prior club standards, almost elegant, which should have been their first warning. Bea had spent December walking nulls through deep snow with the grim precision of a person who had decided physics owed her money, and she put a water bottle with paracord tied to it over a high east limb on the third throw — the first two sailed through the gap she'd aimed at and came down on the wrong side, which she pronounced "acceptable data." Theo threw west and put the bottle directly into the one fork in the entire tree that would swallow it, and spent ten minutes performing his anguish for an audience of crows while Bea, without comment, threw his line for him.

They hauled the dipole up between the two oaks, center peaked a little higher than the ends — not a strict inverted-V, just the natural sag of a wire with a coax tail hanging off the middle — ends fifteen feet apart, thirty feet up, the choke donut turning slowly in the January air like a wind chime that had given up on sound. The coax tail came down the west oak, across the grass, and in through the barn's people-door, where the Kenwood sat on Si's workbench, plugged into the bench outlet, glowing green. It looked at home. That was the thing Jun kept noticing. The barn was a cathedral of scavenged aluminum, masts and booms and coils of coax on pegs, and the dumpster radio on the bench looked less like a guest than like something the barn had been expecting.

"Analyzer," Si said, and handed it to Jun — the little box from October, the one that had judged the pop cans. "You're closest to the radio. Sweep it. Twenty-eight to twenty-nine. Tell me the worst news first."

Jun clipped the analyzer in where the rig's coax ended and ran the sweep, watching the little numbers walk. "SWR is… two point six at twenty-eight four," she said, and her stomach dropped the way it had in October, in exactly the same direction. "Two point four up high. It's bad everywhere. It's not a resonance thing, it's just bad."

"Bad everywhere isn't the antenna being the wrong length," Priya said, immediately, crowding in. "If we'd cut it wrong it would be bad on one side and better on the other. Flat-bad is something else. Flat-bad is the wire talking to something it shouldn't." She turned around, slowly, the way she turned a problem over. "The choke is right, we taped it ourselves. The solder joints held the halyard pull. What's near the wire? Metal. What's metal out here?"

"The east leg," Bea said, from the door. She'd been outside the whole time, hands in her jacket, watching the antenna the way she'd watched the oak limbs. "It droops. The east end comes down past the corner of the —" she searched for the word, and gave up on politeness instead of accuracy "—the tower thing."

"Mast three," Si said. "Unused since the ice took its antenna in '19. Aluminum. Twenty-two feet of it."

"The east leg passes within about four feet of it," Bea said. "I watched it while you were all watching the bottle."

There was a silence in which everyone mentally awarded Bea the point.

"A big hunk of vertical metal that close to one leg is a parasitic element you didn't invite," Si said. "It soaks up your signal and re-sings it out of tune, and your SWR goes flat-bad everywhere, exactly like the lady said. Now — you've all got hands and there's another oak twenty feet north. What's the fix?"

"Move the east end to the north oak," Jun said. "Keep the west end. Rotate the whole antenna. It's broadside to the Pacific either way — it just points a little more southwest-northeast, which—" she looked at Priya.

"Which is *fine*," Priya said. "The terminator in January runs more southwest-northeast anyway. Honestly it's arguably better." She caught herself. "Arguably. The sample size will be one evening."

The re-hang took fifteen minutes, most of which was Theo explaining to the crows that this had been his plan all along. Bea walked the east halyard to the north oak, put the bottle over a clean limb on the second throw — "acceptable data," Jun said, and Bea's mouth did the thing that on Bea counted as a grin — and they hauled the leg up clear of mast three, clear of the barn roof, a clean sweep of wire between two bare trees with nothing near it but sky.

Jun ran the sweep again. "One point four," she said. "At twenty-eight point four, one point four to one. It dips right on the frequency we cut it for." She set the analyzer down on the bench and found she was grinning so hard her cold cheeks hurt. "The wire knows what it is."

"The wire always knew," Si said. "You just had to stop asking it to sing next to a thirty-foot piece of somebody else's antenna." He pulled his watch out on its chain, looked at it, and then looked at the sky over the west fence, where the sun had gone thick and low and the light on the meadow was the color of weak tea. "Four o'clock. Radio's on. Antenna's up. SWR's happy." He lowered himself onto the barn stool with the gravity of a man docking a ship, and nodded at the Kenwood's glowing dial, which hissed back at them, thin and empty.

"Now," Si said, "we do the hard part. Nothing."

---
The nothing went on for forty minutes, and it was the loudest nothing Jun had ever heard.

The Kenwood's dial sat on 28.400. The speaker hissed — a clean, thin, watery hiss, different from 20 meters' busy surf, different from the repeater's disciplined silence — and underneath the hiss there was, as far as anyone could tell, not one single human being on the entire planet Earth.

Mr. O tuned slowly, up to 28.600, down to 28.300, across the beacon segment and back, the way you'd walk a beach. Once, far down, something fluttered — a Morse beep, a single syllable of somebody's voice — and everyone leaned in, and it sank and did not come up again, like a fish refusing to be caught on principle.

"It's four oh six," Theo said. "For the podcast notes, I want to record that we've heard the following: static. Hiss. One (1) beep, disputed. And Mr. Webb's dog sighing." Resistor, asleep on a feed sack, sighed again on cue. "Should I call CQ? Give them something to hear?"

"You'd be calling CQ into an empty room," Mr. O said. "The band isn't ignoring you. It isn't *there* yet. The F2 over the Pacific is still getting hammered by full daylight sun and the D layer under the path is still eating. Listen first — that's the rule of every band, but ten meters teaches it like a monk." He settled onto the second stool. "Patience is a piece of equipment. It's on the parts list."

"Priya," Si said, "tell them what your math says."

Priya had been very quiet, which for Priya was a form of shouting. She'd filled half a page. "The math says this is fine," she said, and her voice had the tight sound of a person re-checking their own parachute. "The MUF along the path should cross twenty-eight megahertz as the terminator approaches. The D layer on our side collapses at local sunset, which today is five oh four. The D layer at the other end, their sunrise, is just forming — barely any absorption. The F2 at both ends is still holding charge from the day. Everything I've got says the window opens in the next hour." She closed the notebook. "The math also says," she added, "that math has never once actually talked to Japan."

"Then it's got the same record as the rest of us," Si said. "Sit down and have terrible coffee."

They sat. The barn had a kerosene heater that ticked, and the light through the one west window climbed the pegboard wall an inch at a time, picking out the coils of coax, the pegged booms, the oiled jaws of the vise. Jun watched the dust turn in it. Bea sat on an upturned bucket near the open door, watching the actual sky do its actual work. Nobody said much. It occurred to Jun that this was a thing she had never once done with three friends and two adults: sit in a room with a machine on, and not talk, and wait.

At four forty-one, Jun got up to stretch and walked to the door, and Bea said, without turning around, "The sun's touching the hills."

It was. The whole west edge of the world had gone the color of the inside of a pumpkin, and the oaks were black lace in front of it, and between the oaks, faintly, the wire caught it — a long, low line of light strung on nothing, glowing like a struck match that refused to burn down. The terminator was out there now, somewhere overhead and racing west, the actual edge of the actual night, and every one of them was watching a copper wire watch it.

"Five oh four," Priya said softly, behind them. "Local sunset. The D layer is —" she stopped herself, and what came out instead was not math at all. "The fog is burning off *right now*."

The hiss changed.

It was not dramatic. That was the thing Jun would try to explain later and fail. Nothing beeped, nothing boomed. The hiss simply *stood up* — rose, thickened, developed grain and texture, the way a field of gray water develops visible waves when wind arrives — and then, out of the grain of it, sliding up out of static like a shape out of fog: a voice.

"...CQ, CQ, this is..." — gone — "...listening..."

Mr. O's hand was already on the dial, gentle, two fingers, and the band came up around them like a house filling with guests. There — a fast, brisk Morse, clear as a tapped glass. There — two voices on top of each other, Spanish, laughing. There — a strong, slow, deliberate station repeating its call into the din, and underneath everything a surf of beeps and voices and pure distance that hadn't been there four minutes ago and now could not be stopped, signals arriving from three time zones as though they'd been standing in the hall the whole time, waiting for the door.

"Oh," Theo said, very quietly, all the podcast gone out of him. "It's *full*."

"It's full," Mr. O said. He let it play. He had a teacher's instinct for when to let a thing play. "Understand what you're hearing. Twenty minutes ago this band could not have reached Spokane. Right now the maximum usable frequency along the sunset path is above us, the D layer on our end is gone, the far end of the path is waking up into its sunrise with the sky still charged from yesterday — and for the next hour or so, that wire —" he pointed through the open barn door at the glowing line between the oaks "—is the length of the Pacific Ocean."

"South America first," Si said, nodding at a loud, relaxed voice rolling up out of the noise, unhurried vowels in Portuguese. "PY-land. Brazil. His sunset's about an hour behind ours, close enough to share the line. He'll fade as the line moves west. By the time he's gone —" and he said it the way a man says the tide is coming in "—the far side'll be up. Japan. Australia. The sunrise people." He looked around at them, four kids standing in a row in the barn door with the sunset on their faces. "You built the fishing rod. The tide's here. Get to the radio."

For twenty minutes they only listened, because Mr. O made them, and it was the right call and everybody knew it by minute three. The band was a city at dinner hour. A station in Chile worked a station in Utah, rapid-fire, contest style. The Brazilians held court lower down, laughing. Somewhere a beacon sent its call in slow Morse over and over, patient as a lighthouse, and Bea — who had been quietly teaching herself the letters off a chart since the fox hunt — tilted her head and said, "That's a K," to no one, and went back to listening with the expression of a person memorizing a new alphabet.

Then, at five twenty-three, up the band, a new voice came up out of the static like a lamp being lit in a window across a valley.

*"CQ, CQ, CQ, this is Juliett Alfa One Yankee Mike Kilo, JA1YMK, calling CQ and standing by."*

The voice was thin and wavering, riding the ripple Mr. O had promised them months ago, but it was *clear* — a real person, a real throat, and the accent of the English was one Jun had only ever heard in videos. Nobody moved. Then the whole barn moved at once, and Si's hand landed on Mr. O's shoulder, and Mr. O was already reaching for the mic.

"Rules," Mr. O said, fast and flat, teacher voice, while the voice from Japan called CQ again. "I am the control operator of this station. That means every transmission is legally mine — my hands on the switch, my license on the line, and I can cut you off mid-word if I have to, which I won't enjoy and will do anyway. Under my supervision you may speak on the air as my guest. That's called a third-party contact, it's as old as the hobby, and it is exactly how Rudy Alvarez's granddaughter is going to talk to Japan. We identify with my call at the start and at least every ten minutes. Speak plain English, give the report he gives you, log everything. Jun." He held out the microphone. "He's calling CQ. Answer him exactly like he expects: his call, then mine. Slowly. He wants to hear you. Go."

Jun's hand was shaking when she took the mic, and she let it shake, because there was no time to fix it and also because some part of her understood that the shaking was the correct instrument reading. She pressed the PTT — *push to talk*, the manual diagram from her desk, the whole fall of learning compressed into one black switch — and said, into the barn, into the wire, into the sunset:

"Juliett Alfa One Yankee Mike Kilo, this is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima."

She let go. The static came back. Two seconds passed like two hours.

*"November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, JA1YMK — good evening! Good evening, or — " a laugh, bright and young-sounding, and absolutely delighted, "— good morning here! Your signal is five and seven, five seven into Osaka. My name is Takeshi, T-A-K-E-S-H-I, and you are my first contact of the morning, the sun is just coming up over the ocean. November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, JA1YMK, over."*

The barn made a sound — Theo actually punched the air, Priya said "oh my *god*" in a voice nobody had heard from her before, and Si closed his eyes like a man at the symphony. Osaka. The wire was sixteen and a half feet of fence-charger copper, and it was in *Osaka*.

Mr. O leaned close to Jun's ear. "Give him his report. Tell him who you are. Then I'll ID properly. Breathe first."

Jun breathed. She keyed the mic. "JA1YMK, this is N8OWL — Takeshi, good morning to you, and it is good evening here, the sun just set, that is — " her voice wobbled and she didn't stop it "— that's the whole reason we can hear you, we're working the gray line. You are five and five, five five, into the western United States. My name is June — Juliett Uniform November Echo — and I am a student operator here with my club, under our sponsor's supervision, and Takeshi, this is my first HF contact ever, on an antenna we built this week out of salvaged wire. It is sixteen and a half feet long and it is hanging between two oak trees. Over."

The static hissed. Then Takeshi came back, and he was laughing — not at her, she could tell, the specific warm laugh of a man whose entire day had just been made before breakfast.

*"June! First contact, congratulations! Five five, thank you. Your antenna is sixteen feet of wire and it is crossing the whole Pacific Ocean — this is the best thing about radio, it never stops being true! Here in Osaka the sky is orange, I can see it from my window. My antenna is also wire — twenty meters of it, from my balcony to a pole, my wife calls it the clothesline for talking. We are the same, June." A burst of static washed through, and his voice rode over it. "Please give my greetings to your club and your sponsor. I must go to work soon, so I will say seventy-three — seventy-three, June, and good luck with the license. JA1YMK, November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, over."*

Jun pressed the switch one last time, and her voice came out steadier than it had any right to be. "Seventy-three, Takeshi. Work safe. This is N8OWL — November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima — clear."

The mic hung there in her hand. The barn was very quiet except for the band, which was now a full choir. Mr. O reached over and gently took the microphone and hung it on its clip, because Jun's hands had apparently decided their work was done for the evening.

"Logged," Priya said, and Jun heard her pencil already going — date, time, frequency, call, name, RST both ways, and then, in the remarks column, in Priya's small furious handwriting, which Jun read upside down: *osaka. sunrise there. wire held.*

"Ms. Alvarez," Mr. O said, "you have a shaky hand and a steady voice. That is the correct order of priorities." And then, because he was Mr. O, he added, "Class dismissed," and got up so she could have the stool, and Jun sat in front of the glowing green dial with her ears ringing and the entire Pacific Ocean folded into sixteen and a half feet of copper above the frozen grass outside.

---

Takeshi faded within ten minutes — his sunrise line marching west with his morning — but the band had finished opening now, and it was a tide coming in, and the club worked the edge of the world until seven o'clock.

Theo got the next one, because of course he did, because the universe had made him a baritone and then dared a band to exist. A station in Wellington came up answering somebody else's CQ, and when the frequency cleared Theo gave his call in the voice he used for the *Marsh Hour* sign-offs and was so smooth about it that Mr. O made him log his own contact as penance for showing off. Priya worked a station in Hawaii — "that barely counts as DX," she said, and then logged it with a hand that was not entirely steady either, and allowed herself exactly one (1) private fist pump, which Jun witnessed and promised, under interrogation, to deny.

And then, at six fifty-one, sliding up out of the noise with the slow roll of a man in no hurry whatsoever: *"CQ, CQ, this is Victor Kilo Two Kilo Delta Papa, VK2KDP, calling and listening."*

"Australia," Si said, from the stool he'd reclaimed, eyes closed. "Sydney, by the prefix. Morning there. Summer."

Jun answered him — her hands had stopped shaking by then, which felt like a small grief and a large one at the same time. VK2KDP came back with a five-and-six for her signal, and took his five-five like a compliment, said his name was Doug and that it was thirty degrees and his feet were up, and when Jun told him she was sixteen and it was below freezing and the antenna was a clothesline between two trees, he laughed so hard he had to unkey, and said, "Love it. Love it. That's the whole game, kid, wire and weather," and gave her a seventy-three that sounded like a wave from a porch, and was gone down into the static as the line slid on.

Bea worked no one, because Bea declined the mic with one shake of her head, but she took the log from Priya at seven and wrote the last two entries herself, in her small exact hand, and nobody missed that she'd stayed the whole evening, on the bucket by the door, between the sky and the radio, equally watching both.

---

They shut it down the way Si made them shut everything down: in order. Radio off, analyzer capped, coax unplugged from the bench feedthrough, and — Si's rule, stated once, never repeated — the antenna end of the feedline grounded to the pegboard bus before anyone left the barn. "Wire up in the sky's a lightning invitation even in January," he said. "Don't have to be a storm. The sky's always charging. Respect the sky and it'll mostly respect you."

Outside, the temperature had fallen off a cliff, and that was when Jun looked up.

The stars were out — all of them, the full winter country set, the Milky Way a chalk swipe from one oak to the other, and the cold had scrubbed the sky so clean that the stars had depth, near ones and far ones, a whole architecture of light. And there, strung between the two black oaks, lit by the barn's floodlight from below and the starlight from above, was the wire: sixteen and a half feet of dead fence charger, glowing faintly at the edges, a single copper line drawn across the dark, with a hundred feet of halyard shadow above it and Japan somewhere on the other end of it, eating breakfast.

Jun stood there with her chin up and her hands going numb, and thought about a creased photograph in a shoebox on the top shelf of her closet: a roof in Tucson at dusk, a TV mast and a palm tree, and one bare copper wire catching the last violet light like it was lit from inside. She had named that picture in her head two years ago and never once said the name out loud.

Copper wire and starlight.

It wasn't a metaphor, she understood now, standing under both. It was an inventory. It was the complete list of what you needed: one wire, one sky, and the patience to wait for the hour when they touched. Her grandfather had known. He'd stood under a wire like this one, in this exact color of cold, and talked to the sunrise on the far side of the world, and written in the back of his logbook that the sky was patient and it was waiting.

It had waited. She'd come.

"You all right over there?" Mr. O asked, loading the Kenwood — wrapped again in its moving blankets, a sleeping legend — into the wagon.

"Yeah," Jun said. "I'm going to remember this exact minute when I'm eighty."

"That's the correct use of it," Mr. O said.

Si came out last and killed the barn lights, all but the floodlight, and stood with them in the gravel, Resistor leaning against his boot. He looked at the wire for a while, and then at the sky, with the unimpressed affection of a man checking on old coworkers.

"That's the sky opening," he said. "You saw it tonight, so now you'll believe it forever — a band can be stone dead at four and worldwide at five, and the only thing that changed was where the night was. Remember the other half, too: it closes. It closes every single night, and some seasons it barely opens at all, and one of these days a storm is going to come through here and remind you that your antenna is a kite string in a lightning rod's neighborhood. Everything about this hobby is weather." He pulled his collar up. "Solar weather, sky weather, regular wet weather. The hams that last are the ones who learn to read all three."

Nobody had anything to add to that, so nobody did. They loaded up. Theo fell asleep against the truck window before the end of Larkspur Road. Priya wrote in her notebook by penlight — Jun glanced over once and saw, at the top of a fresh page: *MUF confirmed. math 1, universe 0. tiebreaker: osaka.* Bea sat very still with the logbook on her knees, and at the first stoplight in town she said, to nobody in particular, in the tone of a person filing something where it would keep:

"Could an antenna be small enough that nobody could see it?"

Priya's penlight came up. "Define *nobody*."

"A landlord," Bea said, and the light changed, and Si drove on, and in the middle seat Jun grinned into the dark all the way back, because she knew the sound of a new project the way she knew the sound of a good dumpster.

That night, in her room, with the house gone quiet and the thrift shop's radiators ticking below, she opened the salvage log to a fresh page and wrote:

*Entry 244. One (1) evening, gray line, Si's farm. Source: built Wednesday, Room 114-B, $0. Condition: 1.4:1 and holding. Potential: confirmed.* And then, because the salvage log had room for one more column than her mother knew about, she wrote the thing that wasn't salvage at all: *Takeshi saw the sunrise over the ocean and heard our wire. The sky opens. It closes too. Si says everything about this hobby is weather, so I guess we're learning to read the sky now, one wire at a time.*

She closed the notebook, turned out the lamp, and lay there in the dark, listening to the winter radiator hiss like a band waiting for sunset.
