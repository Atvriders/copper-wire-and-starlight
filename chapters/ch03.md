## Chapter 3: Wire in the Walls

The soldering iron hissed against a fresh dab of solder, and Jun Alvarez leaned into the open chassis of the Kenwood with the concentration of a surgeon who had personally known the patient.

"Steady," said Si Webb, at her elbow, touching nothing. Si never touched anything. Across a month of Fridays coming to Room 114-B to resurrect the TS-520S, he had not once picked up a tool. He stood behind whoever was holding one and asked questions until the tool drifted to the right place on its own. "Heat the joint, not the solder. Let the solder fall in love with both sides."

"It's in love," Jun said. "It's basically married."

She pulled the iron away and squinted through Mr. O's magnifying visor — which Si had refused to wear and Jun wore with pride — at a joint like a tiny chrome gumdrop holding the spade lug of a brand-new fuse holder to the radio's back panel. The old holder lay in the parts tray, green-furred and sprung, its clip tension gone to wherever old elastic goes to die. It had survived two months of Jun's vinegar cleaning before giving up completely on Thursday, when the Kenwood went dark mid-hiss and Jun revived it by wiggling the fuse — the electronic equivalent of kicking a vending machine.

"Fuses blow for a reason," Si had said, watching her wiggle it. "Holders die for a reason too. Yours died of being older than your vice principal."

The replacement holder had spent its previous life inside a dead oscilloscope at Si's barn, which meant it was a *good* fuse holder, from the era when things were built by people who assumed other people would fix them someday.

"Okay," Jun said, setting the iron back in its stand. "Holder's in. Fuse is in. Now the part I actually like."

"The part where you spray expensive juice into a switch," Theo said. He was documenting the resurrection for *Marsh Hour* posterity, one close-up at a time.

It was the first Friday of November, three-fifty in the afternoon. Outside the storeroom window the practice field was going iron-gray the way it did now before five o'clock, and Room 114-B had taken on its winter smell: damp coats and hot forty-year-old electronics. The Kenwood sat in the middle of the long desk under the window, case off, lake-green boards exposed, the two big final tubes standing in their metal hats like small silos.

"Band switch," said Si. "You know the drill. Straw in, short bursts, then work it."

Jun picked up the can of contact cleaner — DeoxIT, the label mostly worn off, Si's own can, which he had handed over with the ceremony of a man passing down a sword — and aimed the little red straw into the stacked wafers of the band switch. The spray came out cold and sharp-smelling, a hospital for robots, and the forty years of oxide hiding in the switch did not visibly do anything, which was the frustrating thing about oxidation: it surrendered without a sound.

"Now work it," Si said. "Stop to stop. You're not tuning. You're scrubbing. Forty times."

Jun worked it. The big knob went 3.5, 7, 14, 21, 28 and back with a heavy, precise clunk she could feel in her wrist bones — a switch from before plastic. On the tenth pass it got smoother. On the twentieth it got quieter. By the fortieth it felt like a safe being opened by its rightful owner.

"That's the sound of money you didn't spend," Si said. "Now the controls. Every pot on the front. Shot of juice, spin the knob twenty times. A pot you don't clean is a pot that'll scratch at you for the next ten years."

Bea, who had cleaned the front panel all afternoon with a soft toothbrush, held the radio steady while Jun sprayed and spun, sprayed and spun. Priya read aloud from the service manual — scanned by a saint in Ohio, printed and spiral-bound by Priya, who had added a table of contents because she was Priya. "Band switch, wafers one through four," she read. "Driver tube, twelve-B-Y-seven-A. Final amplifier, two six-one-four-six-B. Input power, two hundred watts PEP on single-sideband. Output, about a hundred and twenty." She looked up. "A hundred and twenty watts is a lot of watts to push into a wire out a window."

"That," said Si, "is the next conversation. Finish the patient first."

They finished the patient. Case on — real screws, from the era before plastic tabs. Power cord in. Mr. O's twelve-foot random wire, the sad heroic hookup wire from the first meeting, clipped to the antenna socket and draped out the window. Si folded his arms, and Jun noticed that Mr. O had stopped grading quizzes and come to stand at the end of the desk, hands in his pockets, tie — Orion, for November — hanging perfectly still.

"Your switch, Ms. Alvarez," Mr. O said. "You found it in a dumpster. You get to wake it up."

Jun said "please," out loud, the way she had the first night in her bedroom, and flipped the power switch.

The dial lamp came up out of nothing, aquarium green. The hiss came with it — and then, as the tubes warmed through their first minute, the hiss *thickened*. It developed texture, depth, a low oceanic body that the little black portable had never had, and underneath it, faint and then not faint, a rapid chatter of beeps slid past as she turned the big dial with two fingers.

"Morse," said Mr. O. "Bottom of the band. Keep going up."

She kept going. The numbers slid under the hairline — 14.0, 14.1, 14.2 — and the band opened like a series of doors. A burst of demented teletype ("RTTY," said Mr. O; "machines talking to machines; they're happy, don't worry about them"). Then a voice, deep and unhurried, saying *"...so I told him, you don't tune a linear with a lightbulb in your teeth, Vern, you just don't..."* — and it was *loud*. Not the thin, fog-bound waver of the little portable. This voice was in the room, close enough to touch, with another voice waiting under it, and under that the soft surf of a hundred more spread out across the band like lights on a hillside.

Nobody said anything for a moment. Theo had lowered his phone.

"That's twenty meters at four in the afternoon," Mr. O said quietly. "It's been doing that all day, every day, the whole time you were in math class."

"So the radio hears now," Jun said. "It really hears."

"The radio always heard," said Si. "You just fixed its ears. Let it run. Tubes want a half hour to find their footing — the dial will drift a little while it warms, that's not a fault, that's a personality." He sniffed, and Jun caught it too: rising off the vents, sweet and faint and unmistakable, the smell of old dust on warm tubes, and for one second she was seven years old on a stool in Tucson with her grandfather's coffee steaming next to a glowing green dial, saying *Mija, this one talks to the whole world.*

She didn't say anything. She didn't have to. Mr. O caught her eye and gave her the smallest possible nod, which from him was a hug.

---

Within a week, the Kenwood had become what Mr. O called, in his faculty-meeting voice, the club's hearth.

It lived on the desk under the window with its case scarred and its dial glowing, and people drifted in to be near it the way people drift toward a fire. Theo came at lunch to record podcast intros with the band murmuring underneath, until Mr. O made a rule that the radio was not a sound effect. Priya read the service manual in the corner like a novel, occasionally muttering "that's *elegant*" at a schematic. Bea simply listened, hands folded, head tilted, and once — Jun saw it — wrote down a frequency where she'd heard something interesting. Kids who were not in the club started appearing at the door to ask what the green thing was, and Mr. O answered every one of them the same way: "It's a radio. It talks to the whole world. The club meets Wednesdays."

But the random wire out the window had begun to bother everybody, the way a leaky faucet bothers everybody — quietly, then constantly.

"It's the mouth," Si said, on Friday, watching the wire sway. "You've built yourselves a magnificent ear. One hundred and twenty watts of mouth, sitting right there in those tubes, and nowhere to go. You can't transmit into that." He nodded at the strand of hookup wire. "Not won't. *Can't.* Feed power into an antenna that isn't tuned for it and the power comes back home and cooks your finals. You want to talk, you need an antenna that's *resonant* — cut to the frequency, so it takes the power like a swing takes a push. The simplest one ever built is a dipole. Two legs of wire, fed in the middle, a half wavelength end to end."

"How long is a half wavelength," Priya asked, already uncapping a whiteboard marker, "on twenty meters?"

"You tell me," said Si, which was his whole teaching method in four words.

Priya wrote on the whiteboard: *length (feet) = 468 / f (MHz).* Under it: *468 / 14.1 = 33.2.* She stared at the result. "Thirty-three feet. Each leg sixteen point six. That's—" she measured the room with her eyes "—longer than this room. That's a *lot* of wire."

"That's nothing," Si said. "Eighty meters wants a hundred and twenty-five. Be grateful we're starting in the suburbs." He looked around at them, the four of them and the glowing radio, with the expression of a man arriving at the part of the afternoon he'd been steering toward since Halloween. "Thirty-three feet of insulated copper. Stranded, so it flexes. Cheap, because you're kids and I'm a cheapskate. Where does wire like that grow?"

Jun was already flipping open her salvage log.

She'd written the entry three weeks earlier, on a rainy Saturday at the shop, and knew it by heart. She turned the log around so they could all see: *Entry 231. Extension cord, 100 ft, orange, 16-gauge, 3-wire. Source: Second Wind as-is bin, $2. Condition: dead as a cord — female end crushed flat, ground pin long gone. Potential: ?* and then, in her own handwriting, the note she'd added without knowing why: *it's basically a spool of wire pretending to have a job.*

"The whole world," said Mr. O, reading it, "is a parts bin."

"Si," Jun said. "A hundred feet of cord. Three conductors inside. That's three hundred feet of wire."

"Three hundred feet," Si agreed. "You'll need thirty-three. The rest is for mistakes." He picked up his jacket, which meant the lesson was over, and paused at the door the way he did when he was about to say the thing he'd come to say. "Wednesday, bring the cord. Build your dipole. And then you'd better talk to Ms. Delgado, because a thirty-three-foot antenna doesn't fit out a window — it goes up in the air, outdoors, where the sky can see it." He looked out at the flagpole on the lawn by the practice field, its rope clinking in the November wind.

"I know a pole," he said, and left.
---

Wednesday afternoon, on two pushed-together desks in Room 114-B, the autopsy of the extension cord began, with the Kenwood glowing nearby like a chaperone.

The cord was magnificent in its uselessness: a hundred feet of bright orange vinyl, thick as a garden hose, with a crushed female end that some previous owner had clearly run over with something heavy. Jun cut the ends off with a flush cutter — "say goodbye to its old job" — then scored the jacket lengthwise with a utility knife, shallow and careful, and peeled it back.

Inside, twisted in a gentle helix, lay three separate insulated wires: black, white, and green.

"Three hundred feet," Theo said, with the hush of a man looking at treasure. "Two dollars. You people throw away everything good."

"That's what I've been *saying*," Jun said.

Priya had commandeered the whiteboard. *Dipole, 20 m.* Under it: *total = 468 / f. Each leg = 234 / f.* And under that, a box around the question that mattered: *f = ?*

"Fourteen point one," she said. "Middle of the band. We did this Friday."

"Except—" Theo raised a finger, in the manner of a man who had done Research, and produced a folded printout. "The most famous frequency on twenty meters is fourteen point three. There's a net there — a scheduled on-air meeting — that's been running all day, every day, since before any of us were born. Ships, weather watchers, people passing messages. It sounds like Grand Central Station. That's where the *people* are." He spread his hands. "We should build for where the people are."

There was a short silence. Si, at the back of the room with his arms folded, said: "You're building an antenna for one frequency, then. Not for the band."

"For where the people are," Theo repeated, with slightly less certainty.

"Huh," said Si, in the tone of a man hanging a picture he knows is crooked. "We'll see. Cut your wire."

Priya shrugged and did the math, marker squeaking. "Two thirty-four over fourteen point three. Sixteen point three six feet per leg."

"Now add a foot," said Si.

"The math says sixteen point three six."

"The math says what the math says. The wire says you're going to wrap an end through an insulator, and fold it back, and someday want to trim it, and you cannot cut wire *longer*." Si nodded at the orange coils. "Wire's cheap. Regret's expensive. Seventeen feet a leg."

So they measured out seventeen feet of the black conductor and seventeen feet of the white — "the green one stays in the log," Jun said, coiling them; "ground wire, someday; I can feel it" — and Priya vetoed Jun's first measuring method (pacing) in favor of two yardsticks taped nose-to-tail along the desk edge.

The parts list, as it finally stood, tacked to the corkboard under Iceland, 1963:

- Two legs, 17 ft each, stranded 16-gauge copper, salvaged (one black, one white, for science and for style)
- One center insulator: half a cutting board, Second Wind, 50 cents, engraved KEV & LINDA — 25 YEARS
- Two end insulators: the other half of the cutting board (Kev and Linda's marriage had not survived, but their board would hold up an antenna)
- About 75 feet of RG-58 coax, from a spool a tower crew left in a truck, per Si
- One choke balun, to be coiled at the feedpoint: eight turns of the coax itself, six inches across
- Rope: two end lines, plus whatever Mr. Abernathy said went up the pole

The center insulator was the heart of the build. Jun drilled it — three holes in a triangle, big one at the top for the support rope, two at the bottom corners for the legs — and Bea took over the soldering, because Bea's joints came out like something from a factory brochure, small and bright and exact. The white leg went to the coax's center conductor; the black leg went to the braid. Each leg got a strain-relief knot through its hole, because, as Si put it, "solder is a fair-weather friend; a knot shows up every day."

"Why a braid and a center?" Theo asked, watching Bea work. "It's one antenna. Why does the wire care which half gets which leg?"

"Ask Si," said Jun.

"Ask Priya," said Si.

Priya capped the marker, looked cornered, and then — Jun watched it happen — visibly started to enjoy herself. "Okay. The signal leaves the radio as a wave traveling up the coax, in the space *between* the center wire and the braid. When it hits the middle of the antenna, the wave splits: half of it surges out the white leg, half out the black, millions of times a second. A dipole is two conductors doing a wave's worth of dance. The *care* is this: the current is only balanced — equal in both legs — if the braid's current stays on the *inside* of the braid. Coax is supposed to be a pipe with the signal sealed inside it."

"Supposed to be," said Si.

"But the feedpoint is where the seal breaks," Priya continued. "Some of the current climbs out and starts traveling back down the *outside* of the braid — like water sloshing out of a bottle and running down the glass. Then your feedline is secretly part of your antenna, radiating all the way down the pole and into the shack, and your SWR readings lie to you, and your transmit signal follows you home." She pointed at the coffee can sitting on the desk — the big red family-size one from the teachers' lounge, donated by Mr. O, six inches across. "Which is what the choke is for. Right at the feedpoint, you coil the coax itself into eight turns. To the signal sealed *inside*, the coil is nothing, a straight path. To the current sloshing around on the *outside*, the coil is a wall. It chokes it off. One-to-one choke balun."

"Balun," Theo said. "That word sounds made up."

"Balanced to unbalanced," Priya said. "The dipole is balanced — two equal legs. The coax is unbalanced — a wire inside a pipe. The choke marries them."

Theo wound the choke himself, eight careful turns around the coffee can, and zip-tied the coil under Jun's supervision ("tight enough to hold, loose enough to forgive"), and Si watched the whole operation and said only, "A balun costs forty dollars in a catalog. That one cost a zip tie and knew Kev and Linda."

"Do we multiply anything by the velocity factor?" Priya asked, as the last tie snugged down. "Point six six, for the RG-58. If the wave slows down in the coax, shouldn't the wire be shorter?"

"The point six six is the coax's private life," Si said. "It matters inside the cable, for matching games you'll play another year. Out in the air, the wave runs at full speed — air's velocity factor is one. And the four-sixty-eight in your formula already carries the wire's little lies: the insulation, the sag, the end effect. Four-sixty-eight isn't physics' first guess. It's a hundred years of hams writing down what actually worked." He hefted the finished center insulator, with its two bright legs and its coil of coax, and set it down like a judge delivering a verdict. "Now all it needs is thirty feet of altitude and a permission slip."

---

The permission slip, in the end, was Jun's, and it took her two drafts.

Ms. Delgado's office had a letter tray labeled in typed caps REQUESTS and another labeled, less encouragingly, PENDING. Jun put the single page in the first tray on Thursday morning and was called back at lunch. Ms. Delgado read the request — *REQUEST FOR TEMPORARY INSTALLATION: one (1) wire antenna, 33 ft, on flagpole halyard, after school hours only, Nov–Jun, Physics Club (Radio), Mr. Okafor sponsor* — with the total neutrality of a person who had read ten thousand requests and approved forty.

"Mr. Abernathy supervises anything that touches the pole," she said. "Nothing interferes with the flag. The installation comes down when the school asks, without negotiation, and before any event on that lawn. Lightning: at the first thunder, it is disconnected and abandoned. Those are my conditions." She turned the page over. "And the power lines."

"The service drop to the building is on the far side, eighty feet from the pole," Jun said, because she had paced it twice with Priya. "The rule is at least twice the antenna's length from any power line. Thirty-three feet of antenna needs sixty-six feet of clearance; we have eighty. And if the pole itself fell — which it won't — it would land on grass."

Something moved in Ms. Delgado's face and was gone.

"You paced it," she said.

"Priya measured. I paced."

Ms. Delgado signed the bottom of the page, initialed the conditions, and made a copy for Jun with the office copier, which was, Jun understood, the vice-principal equivalent of a blessing. "After school only," she repeated. "And Ms. Alvarez." She handed back the copy. "When this wire of yours talks to somewhere far away — and I am given to understand that is the entire point — I want to hear about it."

"You'll hear about it," Jun said. "Probably from Theo. On the podcast."

"I know," said Ms. Delgado, in the tone of a woman who knew everything in the building eleven minutes early. "I've subscribed."

Mr. Abernathy arrived at the flagpole at three-forty-five on Friday with a canvas tool roll, a flashlight on his belt in broad daylight, and a ring of keys that hung off his hip like a chandelier. He was a broad, deliberate man with the unhurried walk of somebody who had never once been impressed by a deadline, and he had hung every stage light and curtain in the school for twenty-two years, which he mentioned exactly once, when Si introduced himself.

"Webb," said Si.

"Abernathy," said Mr. Abernathy. He looked at the coil of rope under Si's arm, then at the center insulator in Jun's hands, and gave a single nod, which Jun would later learn was the custodial version of a twenty-minute speech. "You know rope."

"I know rope," said Si.

"Then you know the pole's not in a hurry," said Mr. Abernathy. "Neither are we."

He walked the site first, before anything came out of any bag, and made the four of them walk it with him. "Rule one," he said. "Before a wire goes up, everybody tells me where the power lines are. Not points at. *Tells.*"

"The service drop comes off the pole at the street," Jun said, "down the far side of the building to the transformer by the gym. Nearest line to this pole is eighty feet."

"And the rule?"

"Twice the antenna's length, minimum, from any line," Priya said. "We're inside the rule. And if the whole flagpole fell toward the lines, which it won't, it couldn't reach them."

"Hm," said Mr. Abernathy, which was his version of the nod. "Rule two. Nobody stands under anything that's going up. Eyes up when it's moving. Theo — you're walkway. Anybody comes out those doors, you stop them, politely, like you own the lawn."

"I have experience owning lawns," said Theo, and took up his post.

Rule three was the flag.

Mr. Abernathy lowered it himself, hand over hand on the halyard, and Bea and Priya took the corners as it came down — nobody had asked them to; Mr. Abernathy simply held their eyes until they stepped in — and the flag never came nearer the ground than a folded triangle. He carried it inside himself. "She sleeps inside on weekends," he said. "Now. The flag's line is the flag's line. Your wire gets its own."

That was the part Jun would remember: the new line went up without the flag ever giving up its rope. Mr. Abernathy tied a light messenger line to the flag halyard, ran the loop up through the pulley at the top — "the truck," he called it, "the little wheelhouse up there" — and used the messenger to pull a brand-new loop of braided rope through the truck, until the pole carried two independent loops side by side. He spliced the new loop's ends with a figure-eight knot, tested it with his whole weight, and cleated it off.

"Round turn and two half hitches holds a boat," he said, tying off. "A figure eight holds a life. Remember which one you tied."

"Why a V?" Theo asked from the walkway, as Jun tied the center insulator into the new line. "Why not flat — besides the part where we'd need two poles and we have one pole and a custodian. Why does the upside-down V *work*?"

"Ask Priya," said Jun and Si, in unison.

"The droop bends the current's geometry," Priya said. "A flat dipole presents about seventy-three ohms at its feedpoint. Droop the legs into an inverted-V and the impedance slides down toward fifty — which is exactly what our coax and our radio are built to feed. It's the one antenna that *wants* to match, and it only needs one tall thing. Every ham with one tree learns the inverted-V."

"And the tall thing matters why?" said Si, gently, because he never let a lesson stop one question early.

Priya rolled her eyes but she was smiling. "Height. A low antenna throws its energy up at the clouds and warms the sky. Get it up in the air and the signal leaves low and flat, toward the horizon — toward Denver, not toward the sun. The ground is a sponge. You rise above the sponge."

"Twenty-eight feet of rise," Si said, and looked up the pole. "Not a tower. Enough. It is always, at the start, enough."

They hoisted it at four-oh-two. Mr. Abernathy hauled, slow and even, hand over hand; Jun walked the center insulator out from the pole so it rose clean; Bea and Priya paid out the two end lines so the legs came up without snagging; and the antenna — two dollars of dead extension cord and fifty cents of cutting board — rose up the flagpole into the iron-blue November sky like it had been waiting all its life to be useful.

The apex stopped just below the truck, twenty-eight feet up, and Mr. Abernathy made the line fast on the cleat with a proper cleat hitch, figure-eights and a final locking turn. "That's the bit of hardware," he said, pointing at the cleat with his flashlight, "that this whole school hangs its flag on. Now it hangs a little more."

The ends came out at forty-five degrees, white leg toward the fence, black leg toward the walkway lawn. Bea's end line tied off at the chain-link corner post; Priya's went to a screw-in ground anchor Mr. Abernathy set with a ratchet bar, twenty paces out. The wire ends hung at exactly twelve feet, measured with a twelve-foot piece of conduit he pulled from the tool roll.

"Twelve feet," he said. "Nobody clotheslines themselves walking under it, nobody touches it on a dare, and during transmissions nobody comes within ten feet of any part of the wire. A hundred watts is not lightning, but it is not a pillow. That's rule four. Rule five." He pointed at the sky, where the first cold front in a week was combing thin clouds eastward. "Thunder — anywhere, even arguing with itself over the county line — the coax comes off at the window, the wire comes down, and nobody operates until it's passed. Not a discussion. Antennas are replaceable. Students are not orderable from a catalog."

"Yes sir," said four voices, and meant it.

The coax came down the pole in loose spirals, velcro-wrapped every two feet — Mr. Abernathy produced the velcro from the tool roll without comment, the way a stagehand produces sandbags — crossed the lawn under a rubber cable ramp he had liberated from the theater ("so nobody sues me over a trip hazard, and so the mower lives"), and came in through the window feedthrough of Room 114-B.

At four-twenty, the club stood in a ragged line on the lawn and looked at what they'd made: a pale orange thread rising twenty-eight feet to a point just under the wheelhouse, two legs sweeping down and out, still as a held breath in the cold air, with the last thin sunlight running along the copper under the vinyl like the ghost of a filament.

"Huh," said Si, which from Si was a standing ovation.

The judgment came at dusk, in the shack, from a box the size of a paperback.

Si's antenna analyzer — the same little instrument that had passed sentence on the pop-can dipole in October — hung off the shack end of the coax, and the whole club crowded behind it while Si turned its dial with one thick finger. "Fourteen point three," he said. "Your famous frequency. Where the people are."

The needle said 3.4:1.

"That," said Si, "is not an antenna. That's a space heater. A third of your hundred watts would come back down the coax and warm the finals."

"But the math—" Theo started.

"The math was fine. The *target* was wrong, and something else is wrong too." Si swept the dial slowly downward, and the club watched the numbers walk: 2.9, 2.3, 1.8, and then, down around thirteen-six, the bottom of the curve at 1.5:1 — and rising again below that. "It tunes best low. Your antenna thinks it's a thirteen-six antenna. Which means it's electrically *long*. What did you do with the extra foot I made you cut?"

Jun closed her eyes. "The tails. I left a foot of free wire hanging past each end insulator. For trimming. You said you can't cut wire longer, so I left it dangling instead of folding it back."

"And a dangling tail is still antenna," Si said. "The wire doesn't know your intentions. It only knows its length."

"There's a second problem," said Mr. O, from his desk, pulling over a legal pad. "And it's the one I let you keep, because the analyzer teaches it better than I can. You built for one frequency. But nobody lives on one frequency — you tune around, you follow the openings, you answer a CQ wherever you find it. An antenna isn't a key cut for one lock. It's a bathtub." He drew a U on the pad. "The SWR curve. Low in the middle, rising at both ends. The whole twenty-meter band runs fourteen-oh to fourteen-three-fifty. If you want the whole tub usable — everything under two-to-one — you put the bottom of the bathtub in the middle of the band. Fourteen point one. Not parked on fourteen-three, which is somebody's front porch — that net has met there every day for decades, and you do not build a house on a neighbor's porch."

Theo looked at his folded printout, then folded it again, smaller. "So we built a porch antenna."

"You built a *low* antenna," said Mr. O, "which is the one kind you can fix with scissors. You cannot trim a wire longer."

They fixed it with scissors. The end lines had enough slack — Abernathy's doing — that Bea and Priya could walk each wire end down to chest height in the cold dark, Bea holding the flashlight in her teeth, Priya reading numbers off a scrap of envelope. Si made the first trim deliberately stingy: one inch off the white tail, one off the black; walk the ends back up; sweep.

"Thirteen point six seven," said Theo, reading the analyzer over Priya's shoulder. "It moved a *sneeze*. We'll be out here all night."

"Wire teaches patience," said Si. "Now you may cut two."

Two inches a side; sweep. The dip walked up the band: thirteen-eight, at 1.5:1. Two more; thirteen-nine-five, and Priya's breath was coming in little clouds now, and nobody suggested going inside. Si made them stop there and guess the next trim before cutting — "guessing first is how the math gets into your fingers" — and Priya's guess, two inches, landed the bottom of the bathtub at 14.100 on the dot, at 1.6:1.

Si swept the band edges. "Fourteen-oh-fifty: one-point-eight. Fourteen-three-fifty: one-point-nine. Under two, wall to wall." He switched off the analyzer and put it in his coat. "Write it in the notebook, Priya. Sixteen point six feet a leg, plus what's left of your tails. Date it. That's a tuned antenna, and tuned antennas get their names written down."

Priya wrote it down. Jun watched her best friend print, in small precise letters, *CLUB DIPOLE #1 — 20 m inverted-V — 2 × 16.6 ft — apex 28 ft, ends 12 ft — SWR 1.6:1 @ 14.100 MHz*, and then, after a pause, underline it twice, which Jun had only ever seen her do to correct answers.

"Tomorrow morning," said Mr. O, "the band wakes up early. So do I." He looked around at them. "Donuts are on the sponsor."

---

Saturday, nine-thirty in the morning, and Room 114-B was full of sugar, sunlight, and radio.

Mr. O brought the donuts. Si brought a thermos of his own coffee, which Theo accepted with enthusiasm, sipped once, and set down with the careful neutrality of a man who has just met a new element. "It's an acquired taste," Si said. "I acquired it in 1979." Outside the window, their antenna stood against a scrubbed blue November sky, the orange jacket bright in the cold light, and every time the wind moved it, Jun felt the movement somewhere in her own chest, like the building had grown a nerve.

The Kenwood had been warming since nine. The shack smelled of warm dust and frosting. Mr. O sat in the operating chair, and the club stood in a crescent behind him, and nobody pretended they were doing anything but waiting for history.

"Procedure," said Mr. O, "is boring right up until it's the only thing between you and chaos. We listen first." He tuned, slowly, past a dozen voices — two stations in Oregon arguing cheerfully about snow tires, a man reading the weather off his porch like a psalm — and found a quiet pool at 14.262. "We ask if the frequency is in use, twice, because some stations are too weak to hear and too proud to interrupt." He pressed the PTT. "Is this frequency in use? This is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima." He listened. Asked again. Listened. The band hissed its patient hiss.

"And now," said Mr. O, "we ask the sky a question. CQ, CQ, CQ, this is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, calling CQ and standing by."

The first CQ went out into the world and the world kept its counsel. Theo started to say something and Priya shushed him. Mr. O called again, unhurried, the way you skip a stone you've skipped before — and halfway through the second call, there was a voice.

"November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, this is Kilo Zero Oscar Lima Tango. K0OLT, Al, in Denver, Colorado. Good morning to you."

The room did not cheer. It was, Jun would think later, stranger and better than cheering: four teenagers going absolutely still at once, like deer in a clearing, while a man five hundred miles away said *good morning* through a wire they'd built out of trash.

Mr. O gave him a report — "you're five-nine, Al, five-nine into Maple Run" — and Al gave one back, five-seven, "with a little QSB," and Bea, watching the S-meter rise and fall like a slow breath, said softly, "The pond is rippling." Al asked about the station, and Mr. O told him: a club, four students, a Kenwood TS-520S rescued from a dumpster, and the antenna was a hundred feet of dead extension cord on the school flagpole. There was a pause of pure delight from Colorado.

"Well, I'll be," said Al. "I started on a wire off my mother's clothesline in 1962. You're doing it the right way, OM. Sunny here, thirty-eight degrees, snow on the peaks. Tell those kids they're welcome on my frequency any time."

In the doorway, unnoticed, stood Mr. Abernathy, in Saturday coveralls, come to check his rope on his weekend rounds. He stayed for the whole contact without saying a word, and when Al signed — "seven-three from Denver, K0OLT clear" — the custodian said, to nobody in particular, "My wife's sister is out in Littleton," in the tone of a man filing something away.

Mr. O re-identified the station — "every ten minutes, the name, that's the law and the manners" — and then spun the dial and found, of all things, another CQ: "Whiskey Five Juliett Echo Delta, W5JED, Jed, in Austin, Texas, calling CQ and listening."

He answered, and Jed came back strong, and when the reports were traded — five-nine both ways — Mr. O did the thing he'd been saving. "Jed, this is a school club station, and I have a guest here I'd like to let say hello. The control operator remains November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima. Stand by for Theo."

Theo took the mic. For one heartbeat — Jun saw it, and loved him for it — the voice of *Marsh Hour*, the talent-show baritone, the man who owned lawns, had absolutely nothing to say. Then he found it.

"W5JED, this is Theo. In Maple Run. I'm—" he glanced at the antenna out the window "—I'm talking to you on an extension cord we cut with a utility knife on Wednesday."

"Well, Theo," said Jed, warm as toast through a thousand miles of sky, "you're five-nine into Austin. Five-nine plus. And that's a radio voice if I ever heard one, son. You keep at it."

Afterward, nobody could remember who laughed first.

Mr. O offered the mic around — "the sky is taking callers" — but Priya shook her head ("I will transmit when I have something worth five-nine to say"), and Bea shook hers, smiling, and Jun found that she wanted to wait. She had said her name on the air once already, with Si's call wrapped around her like a borrowed coat. For this, she wanted her own call sign in her pocket first. The sky would keep. The sky was patient — somebody she loved had promised her that, in pencil.

They logged it, because a contact isn't real until it's written down. Mr. O opened the club logbook — WESTRIDGE HIGH ARC, its last entry more than a year old, its pages still smelling faintly of 1963 — and Jun wrote, in her best block letters, the log running on Greenwich time because, as Mr. O said, the sky doesn't do time zones:

*22 NOV 2025 — 1831 UTC — 14.262 MHz SSB — K0OLT, Al, Denver CO — RST 59/57 — first club HF contact. Antenna: extension cord. 1839 UTC — W5JED, Jed, Austin TX — RST 59/59 — third-party guest: Theo (59 into Austin).*

The book was awake again. Jun closed it gently, the way you close a book you're coming back to, and found that Mr. Abernathy had drifted to the window and was looking up at his pole with his arms folded, and that Theo was very quietly holding his phone up and recording nothing, like a man at a wedding.

Si was the last to speak. He stood at the window with his terrible coffee, looking out at the orange wire against the cold blue sky, and he said it the way he said everything that mattered — flat, and short, and gone before you could thank him for it.

"You put a wire in the sky," he said, "and the sky answered. Remember that feeling. It doesn't get old; I've checked for fifty years." He turned around. "Now — who wants to hunt a hidden transmitter?"

Four hands went up. Bea's was first again, straight and unhesitating, like a flag.

"Thought so," said Si. "Here's the game. Somewhere out there, a little transmitter is hiding, saying its name over and over, and you find it with your ears and an antenna that *points*. We build the pointer when the snow flies." He put on his jacket. "Bring mittens. December's coming, and December is when the real fun starts."

"Define *points*," said Priya, but she was already reaching for the whiteboard marker.

"Like hide and seek," said Bea, quietly, testing the shape of it.

"Hide and seek with physics," said Si, and was gone, leaving the smell of his coffee and the glow of the green dial and four kids already talking over each other about snow.

Jun stayed to coil things. Through the window the antenna held still in the late light, and she opened the salvage log and wrote:

*Entry 233. Remainder of the orange cord: 66 ft, plus 100 ft of green (the green one stayed in the log). Condition: excellent. Potential: whatever the sky asks for next.*
