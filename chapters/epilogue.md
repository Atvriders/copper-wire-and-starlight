## Epilogue: Call Signs

Question twenty-one was trying to trick her, and Jun was almost sure she respected it for that.

She leaned over the answer sheet, eraser hovering, in Meeting Room B of the Maple Run Public Library, where the carpet smelled of lemon cleaner and somebody's civic optimism. Outside the windows it was the second Saturday of August 2026, bright and hot, the last Saturday that counted as summer. Inside, at four pushed-together folding tables, sat the entire membership of the Westridge High Amateur Radio Club, taking the same thirty-five-question exam with four very different relationships to panic.

Priya had finished in nine minutes and was going through her booklet a second time, on principle: a first pass was a draft. Theo had treated the walk to his table like a red carpet and was narrating his own thought process in a whisper until an examiner said, "Mr. Marsh. This is not a broadcast," without looking up.

And Bea — Bea had put her pencil down, aligned it exactly parallel to the table's edge, and sat with her hands folded, watching the clock with her judge's face. She had been done for six minutes. She was simply waiting for the room to catch up.

Jun went back to twenty-one: where a Technician could and could not transmit, dressed up in a trench coat so you'd misread it. She knew this. She had known it for a year without knowing she knew it — a year of Si asking her questions from behind terrible coffee instead of giving answers, a year of the actual sky teaching the actual math. She changed her answer, changed it back, and put the pencil down before she could talk herself into a third opinion.

"Time," said Mr. O, from the examiners' table, with the satisfaction of a man who had waited a whole school year to say it. "Pencils down, candidates. The government thanks you."

---

There were three examiners, because the rules said three, and they formed what Si called the parole board. Mr. O, N8OWL, in a Perseus tie even though it was August and nobody was making him. Si, W7JUNK, who had taken the examiner training online and complained about the website for a month ("Designed by a committee of fax machines") and then, Jun suspected, secretly loved being trusted with the rubber stamps. And a retired mail carrier named Gerry from the county club, un-rattleable after forty winters of other people's business.

They gathered up the booklets and went out to the hallway to grade, and Meeting Room B went silent in the particular way of rooms where your future is being decided twenty feet away.

"So," Theo said, pushing his chair back onto two legs. "While we wait for the government to finish deciding whether we're geniuses, I'd like to go on record—"

"You were on record for the whole exam," Priya said. "You narrated."

"I provide commentary. It's a service."

Jun let them wash over her. She was watching Bea, who had produced, from somewhere in her denim jacket, a second folded form.

"Bea," Jun said. "What is that."

"The next one," Bea said.

"There are two exams," Priya said slowly. "You filled out paperwork for both."

"The General is thirty-five more questions," Bea said, in the tone of a person describing a small appetizer. "If you pass the first one, they let you try. I brought the form." She paused, then spent three of her rationed sentences: "I want the extra bands. For Morse. The whole point is the Morse."

"You are insatiable," Theo said, with enormous respect.

"I'm thorough," Bea said.

The door opened. Gerry came back in with the face of a man who had graded ten thousand of these. "Miss Kowalczyk, Element 2: pass," he said. "I hear we're keeping you a while longer." Bea followed him out to a side table and a fresh booklet, nodding once to the room like a surgeon going in.

Mr. O and Si came in with the grading overlays — plastic templates full of holes, the world's most consequential punch cards. Si dropped into the chair across from Jun and folded his arms.

"Well," Si said.

"Silas," said Mr. O.

"I'm building suspense. It's my only hobby you people haven't ruined."

Mr. O set three small white slips on the table, face down, one at a time, like a man dealing cards in a movie. "Certificate of Successful Completion of Examination," he said. "The world's longest name for a permission slip. Ms. Nair. Thirty-five of thirty-five." He slid her the slip. "There was a question she wishes to discuss with the question pool committee, but she got it right anyway."

"It was ambiguously worded," Priya said, already reading her slip. "I got it right *despite* them."

"Mr. Marsh. Thirty-four." The second slip. "The one you missed, you would have gotten right if you had stopped performing."

"Thirty-four is a ninety-seven," Theo said. "That's an A. That's an A in government."

"Ms. Alvarez." Mr. O held the third slip for half a second longer than the others, and something in his face went — not soft, exactly; precise, the way it went when a demonstration worked. "Thirty-three. Including question twenty-one, which you changed twice. You changed it *from* the right answer and then *back* to it, which the eraser marks testify to. The committee salutes your journey."

Jun took the slip. CERTIFICATE OF SUCCESSFUL COMPLETION OF EXAMINATION, it said, and her name, and Element 2, and three signatures. It was just a piece of paper. It weighed nothing. She held it with both hands anyway, the way you hold something that wasn't supposed to survive.

"Now the fine print," Mr. O said, and folded his hands. "You are not on the air yet. That certificate is a receipt, not a license. The examiners send your results to the FCC, and someday — five days, ten, whenever the machinery finishes — a call sign appears in the database next to your name. The day it appears, you may transmit. Until that day—" he looked at each of them "—you listen."

"Listening is chapter one," Jun said.

Mr. O looked at her for a moment, and didn't smile, exactly. "It is," he said. "The test is the easy part. The hobby is the hard part, and the hobby is the fun part." He stood and straightened the Perseus tie. "Congratulations, candidates. You have passed the easy part."

Si waited until Mr. O was out of earshot, refilling his coffee from the library's courtesy urn.

"Thirty-three, thirty-four, thirty-five," Si said. "A year ago you three thought SWR was a boy band." He drank, grimaced, and looked at Jun over the rim. "Your grandfather started with a piece of paper just like that. They all did. Everybody on the air was somebody's kid with a receipt, once." He set the cup down. "Now you're legal. God help the ionosphere."

From the side table came the scratch of Bea's pencil, already deep into the next mountain.

---

Waiting for the FCC's database was like waiting for a comet. You knew it was coming. You knew, roughly, when. You had charts and confidence and other people's eyewitness accounts, and none of it — none of it — made it come any faster. The sky did not care that you had brought a lawn chair.

The group chat, still named *Room 114-B* after a year, developed rituals.

Priya checked the license search every morning at six and reported the null result with scientific detachment: *day 2: nothing. day 3: nothing. government systems update overnight, so mornings are statistically optimal.* When Jun pointed out that the FCC's computers did not care when a person looked at them, Priya replied, *it's called an observational protocol,* and then, three minutes later, *it's a little bit hope.*

Theo treated every evening like a sign-off. *This is day four,* he announced via voice message, with full reverb, from what Jun was certain was his closet. *Four days since four heroes passed the easiest test in government. The database remains silent. The people wait.* He was leaving for college in twelve days, and had begun rehearsing his departure the way he rehearsed everything.

Bea said nothing in the chat for five days. Then, on the sixth morning: *checked at 6. nothing.* And then, a minute later, in a tone nobody had ever heard from her in writing: *also, i passed the general exam. just so you know.*

Theo responded with seven trophy emojis and a poll about what her call sign would be.

Jun did her waiting with her hands. She tuned the FT-60R to the repeater and the weather band and back, and listened, because listening was chapter one until the database said otherwise. She put fresh batteries in the salvage flashlight and took them out again because they were the good batteries.

And one night she got down SALVAGE LOG VOL. 4 and opened it to entry 214, written nearly a year ago, in a room lit aquarium-green by a dumpster radio's dial.

*Condition: dead, but hopeful. Potential: don't know yet. Something big.*

And under that, in the handwriting of a girl who had not yet met a tape-measure yagi or a man named Si or the sound of her own name on the air: *Everything. Check back in a year.*

A year. Jun closed the log without writing anything. Not yet. She knew what the next entry was going to say, but you did not write the ending before the comet came.

---

It came on a Friday, six days after the exam, at 6:04 in the evening, which Priya would later insist fell within her predicted window.

*CHECK THE DATABASE,* the group chat said, with no punctuation, from Priya, which was the textual equivalent of shouting. Then: *CHECK IT. CHECK IT NOW.*

Jun's hands were not steady. The search page took three seconds to load and she hated every one of them, and then it was there, in the plain blue-on-white bureaucracy of the federal government, the least poetic document she had ever loved:

ALVAREZ, JUNE. KI7JUN.

Her phone was already ringing. She let it ring. She read the line four times, the way you read something meant for one specific reader, someday, and then she said it out loud to the empty room, in the international alphabet, because it was her name now and names should be said properly:

"Kilo India Seven Juliett Uniform November."

The phone rang again. Theo's baritone came through with no reverb at all, which meant he had forgotten to go to the closet, which meant he was genuinely undone.

"You have a government name," he said. "We all have government names. Jun. *Kilo India Seven Tango Oscar Mike.* Somebody in a federal building looked at my paperwork and said, yes, this man deserves a suffix." A breath. "They're all there. All four. KI7JUN, KI7BEA, KI7PNA, KI7TOM. It's like they *knew.*"

The chat buzzed. Priya: *for the record, the probability of four sequential applicants receiving suffixes that spell-compatible with our names is so small that i am choosing to read it as a compliment from the universe. do not tell anyone i said that.* And then, immediately: *bea has been informed. bea responded in morse code. i believe it spelled her own call sign. she has been practicing for a week.*

*she has,* Bea confirmed — which was how Jun knew Bea had been refreshing the database just as hard as any of them.

"Tonight," Theo was saying. "Si already knows — Mr. O told him — he called the *shop,* Jun, he called the landline like it's 1987, and your mom put him on speaker in the whole store—"

"Wait," Jun said. "He did what."

But she was already going down to the shop, where Marisol stood at the counter with the cordless phone and an expression Jun had known all her life — the grim recognition that the fight was already lost, worn now for a different reason.

"That was Mr. Webb," her mother said. "He says sunset is at eight-eleven, the coffee will be terrible, and the newest hams in district seven are expected at the meadow." She set the phone in its cradle. "He used the word *hams* like it was a formal title. Like knights."

"Mom," Jun said, and held up her own phone, with the plain blue-on-white line still on the screen.

Marisol read it twice, the way Jun had, and when she spoke it was with the cadence of a woman delivering a diagnosis she had made years ago and been waiting ever since to confirm.

"KI7JUN," she said. "Mija. The disease is official now. It has a call number." She came around the counter and put her hand on Jun's shoulder, and something crossed her face that Jun kept, without examining it, the way she had learned to. "Your abuelo would be on the air tonight telling Argentina. He would not even wait for dinner. He'd say —" her voice did the thing it did maybe twice a year "—*el cielo es paciente, pero no toda la noche.* The sky is patient, but not all night."

"That's not what he wrote in the logbook," Jun said.

"No," said Marisol. "What he wrote in the logbook was for you."

---

Upstairs, Jun got the shoebox down and opened it on the desk where the Kenwood had once sat like an altar. The deck of QSL cards. The dense slanting handwriting of the logbook. The photograph, creased from a wallet: the little house in Tucson, the sky going violet, the single bare copper wire from the TV mast to the palm tree, catching the last of the light like it was glowing.

She looked at it for a long moment, and then she said it out loud — her call sign, her own, Kilo India Seven Juliett Uniform November — to the photograph, the way you tell somebody who has been waiting.

Then she opened SALVAGE LOG VOL. 5 and wrote, in her own slanting hand, the entry she had been composing for six days:

*One (1) amateur radio call sign, KI7JUN. Source: Federal Communications Commission, by way of a dumpster on Delaney Street. Condition: new, already worn in. Potential: check back in a year.*

She clipped the FT-60R to her belt. It had waited three years on a shelf. Tonight it was going to the meadow to watch its girl go to work.

---

The meadow at the end of Larkspur Road was going gold when they pulled in, six masts throwing long shadows, and Resistor gave the two barks he allotted to people he considered family. The barn doors stood open on the IC-7300's blue glow and the smell of solder and coffee. And between the two big oaks, catching the low light along its whole length, hung a familiar line of orange.

"You kept it," Jun said.

"Retired it," said Si. "Like a jersey." Thirty-three-point-two feet of salvaged extension cord, sixteen-point-six a leg, the choke balun at its center just as it had hung off the flagpole cleat the day the sky first answered them. "Figured the newest hams in district seven ought to have it here. Start where you started."

Jun ran the analyzer — Si handed it over without being asked, his whole teaching method in one gesture — and watched it settle at one-point-six to one, mid-band, same as the flagpole day. Some things you could rely on.

The little ten-meter wire from January was still up between the far oaks, sixteen and a half feet of it — the antenna you could lose in a laundry basket — and that was the one she'd be on, because Priya had already announced the terminator's schedule. "The D layer dies with the light," Priya said, "but the F2 stays charged a while. For maybe an hour the absorption is gone and the mirror is still up. The band opens from the edges, along the line between day and night." She said it the way other people said *sunset's at eight-eleven* — like a train time worth catching.

Babcia had sent pierogi. Theo ate four and narrated none of it, which was how you could tell he was nervous.

By eight-thirty the sky had gone violet over the meadow. The first stars came through like a signal rising out of the noise, and for one more minute the orange cord held the last of the light the way a wire does — end to end, glowing faint, strung between two oaks at the end of Larkspur Road.

For the first time in her life, nobody stood behind the chair.

Mr. O had drifted out to the meadow to hunt Perseids. Si was at the workbench pretending to organize connectors. Jun sat down at the IC-7300 with Abuelo Rudy's HT on her belt and a brand-new logbook at her elbow — LOG VOL. 1, lettered in the salvage-log hand — and the rig's blue waterfall glowed on her face the way the Kenwood's green dial once had.

She found ten meters where the little wire was cut, up around twenty-eight-four, and listened.

Nothing. The band hissed, thin and oceanic — the sound of the entire electromagnetic universe ignoring her. She knew that sound from a bedroom over a thrift shop, a year ago. It did not scare her now. But it was not voices, either.

"It's dead," she said, and heard how it sounded.

"It's August," said Si, from the workbench, not looking up. "Give the sun a minute to finish dying."

So she waited, hand on the big tuning knob, and then — the way it had in January, the way Si had promised it always would — the noise floor came up like a curtain, and out of it, one by one, the voices bloomed: a big relaxed signal somewhere close, a wavering one far away, the whole band lighting up like windows along a street at dusk.

The gray line. The door in the sky, propped open for an hour.

Jun tuned a little up from twenty-eight-four, found a clear spot, and did it the way Mr. O had taught them. "Is this frequency in use? This is Kilo India Seven Juliett Uniform November." She listened. Nothing. She asked again. The static said the room was hers.

Then she pressed the transmit switch and called, into the whole sky: "CQ, CQ, CQ. This is Kilo India Seven Juliett Uniform November, KI7JUN, calling CQ and standing by."

It came out too fast. The phonetics tripped over each other and her voice went up where it should have gone down. The speaker hissed. Nobody came, which was fair — she wouldn't have answered that either.

"From the diaphragm," Theo said from the barn door, gently, with none of the showboat in it. "You're talking to the edge of the world. Don't apologize to it."

"Sky's in no hurry," said Si. "Neither are you."

Jun took one breath, down where Theo's baritone lived, and thought of her grandfather on a stool in a little room in Tucson, talking to Argentina at two in the morning. She pressed the switch again and set the letters down one at a time, like parts on a workbench.

"CQ, CQ, CQ. This is Kilo India Seven Juliett Uniform November — KI7JUN — calling CQ and listening."

Static. A long moment of it, surf on a beach half a planet wide.

And then, thin and wavering, sliding out of the noise like a shape out of fog: "KI7JUN, KI7JUN, this is—"

The rest of it ducked under and came up again. She wrote the call down letter by letter as it repeated — patient phonetics on the other end too, somebody being careful, somebody new enough at this to still be careful — and it went into LOG VOL. 1 as entry one: a call she had never seen before, from somewhere to the east, where the dark had already come. The voice might have been seventy. It might have been fourteen. Its name and town got half-swallowed by a fade, and none of that mattered at all.

"...you're five and seven," the voice said, "five by seven, first contact of the evening for me." A pause, and a laugh, warm through the wavering. "Welcome to the band, June."

"Thank you," Jun said, and her hands were not quite steady, the way they had not been steady in January. "You're five and five, some fading but solid copy. Name here is June, and you are my very first contact. Thank you for coming back to me. Seventy-three. This is KI7JUN, clear."

"Seventy-three, June. Keep that wire up." And the voice slid back into the static and was gone, off to its evening, off to its tomorrow.

The barn was very quiet. Priya had written the time down before anyone thought to ask. Bea stood in the door to the meadow and nodded once — the nod that meant a thing had been filed under permanent. Theo took a slow, silent bow, all theater and no audience — for Theo, the purest form of respect there was.

Si set a mug of terrible coffee at Jun's elbow, looked at the first entry in LOG VOL. 1, and allowed himself the smallest possible version of a smile.

"There it is," he said. "No known cure."

---

The others drifted back to the pierogi and the coffee urn, and behind her, in the barn light, the J-38 came out of Bea's jacket and began its slow, sure music on forty meters — Bea's turn, Bea's tomorrow. Jun stepped out into the meadow with Resistor, who had decided the evening required supervision, and stood looking up.

The Perseids were at it overhead, quick strokes of light you could never quite look at directly, and the Milky Way had come up over the barn like a band opening. The orange cord was invisible now except where she knew to look — a wire against the stars, holding its shape in the dark by memory.

Somewhere to the east, along the edge of the night, somebody was writing four letters and a number into a logbook. Her call sign, in a stranger's log now — proof that on a Friday in August, on a farm at the end of Larkspur Road, a girl had asked the sky a question and the sky had answered to her name.

That was the whole of it, she thought. It had been the whole of it since Tucson, since a wire on a roof caught the last of the light: you put a little copper up into the dark, and the dark gave you back a voice. Copper wire and starlight. That's all it ever was.

The sky is patient, her grandfather had written. It is waiting for you.

It had waited a year. Behind her the key sang on, steady as a heartbeat, and out along the gray edge of the world the band was full of voices, and tomorrow — tomorrow was already on the air somewhere, calling CQ.

The sky could wait a little longer, Jun thought, and went back inside to answer it.
