## Chapter 12: When the Phones Went Down

The pop-can dipole lay across two sawhorses in the middle of Si Webb's barn like evidence in a soda-related crime, and the antenna analyzer was saying unkind things about it.

"Two point six to one," Priya said, turning the little screen so everybody could see the number being wrong at them. "And it drifts when I wiggle the coax. Watch." She wiggled. The reading wandered toward three and thought about staying there. "That's not a measurement. That's a mood."

"It worked on Field Day," Jun said. "It ran the talk-in table. It heard County Tech. It heard everything."

"Field Day was four days ago," said Si from his stool, with the mildness of a man delivering weather. "Things fall off trucks. Feedlines get tripped over. Antennas get rode hard and put away wet." He tipped his chin at the sawhorses. "Figure out what changed. That's the whole hobby in four words."

It was the Tuesday after Field Day, and nobody in the club had quite come down yet. Three hundred twelve contacts in the log. The midnight cell that had come howling through Fairview County Park and sent County Tech's beautiful crank-up tower cranking down in a panic while their own lashed-together junk antennas rode it out. The trophy that wasn't a trophy — the board chair herself, standing in the dawn mud with a paper cup of coffee, asking *is it always like this?*

And then, Sunday night, the news. The same storm system that had scattered Field Day's antennas had kept going east and dropped a tornado two counties over — eleven minutes on the ground, the kind of footage that made a whole region hold its breath. Nobody killed, which the anchor kept saying in the tone of a man knocking on wood. Roofs gone. A grain leg folded in half like a drinking straw.

Now the forecasters were using the careful voice again. Another line, they said. Wednesday night, maybe. This one aimed closer to home.

Which was why the pop-can dipole was on sawhorses in the barn instead of in its go-kit bucket on the storeroom shelf, and why Si had written two words on the barn chalkboard:

STORM KITS.

"If the phones go down," he'd said, "the county's going to need talkers, and talkers need antennas that go up in five minutes by flashlight. You already own the finest soda-can antenna in three counties. Make it a kit. Make it so you could hand it to a stranger."

The kit, as it took shape on the workbench under Bea's precise packing hands, was:

- one five-gallon bucket, ex-house-paint, washed, lid intact
- the pop-can dipole, broomstick boom and all — four cans a side, mouth to base
- a ten-foot mast of scavenged electrical conduit, in two five-foot sections that sleeved together
- a short length of PVC pipe, bolted upright inside the bucket, for the mast to stand in
- twenty-five feet of RG-58, plus the adapter that fit Jun's handheld
- rope, four tent stakes, a roll of duct tape, a paint marker
- four spare soda cans, "because cans get dented," said Bea, who did not say things twice
- a clipboard with a pencil tied to it, and an index card that read, in Priya's lettering: *146.760 — minus 600 — PL 100.0*

"The repeater card stays in the bucket," Priya said, taping the card to the clipboard like she was laminating a sacred text. "In a real emergency nobody remembers anything. You'll be standing in the rain going *what was the offset*, and the answer will be on a card, because I put it there."

"The bucket gets ballast on site," Si said. "Water jugs, gravel, whatever's lying around. Don't haul weight you can find when you get there." He let them pack. He never packed for them. He just sat on the stool with Resistor's head on his boot and asked the questions that cost them an hour.

The first one was: "So what's wrong with it?"

What was wrong with it took them forty minutes, and it was two things, because it was always two things.

Bea found the first. She had the boom in her lap, turning it slowly under the drop light, and she ran one finger along the wood and stopped.

"The marks are gone," she said.

The tuning marks. Back in October, when they'd first built the thing, they'd tuned it by sliding the can stacks along the boom — watching the analyzer, nudging an eighth of an inch at a time — and then they'd marked the sweet spots with a pen. Eight months of being hauled to Field Days and fox hunts and club demos had rubbed the pen marks to ghosts.

"Okay," Jun said. "So we re-measure. The gap between the inner cans is one inch — that part I remember — and then each stack is four cans, and we slide them until the analyzer's happy."

"Why does sliding the cans even matter?" Theo asked. He was coiling rope the way Si had shown him, over-under, with the tragic focus of a man who wanted to be done. "They're cans. They held root beer. Now they're an antenna, fine, I've made my peace with that. But an eighth of an inch of can?"

"Resonance," said Priya, in the tone she used when a question was so good it hurt. "At a hundred forty-six megahertz, a quarter wavelength is nineteen point three inches. That's four cans, because a soda can is four point eight-three inches tall, and I will never stop being pleased that it works out that cleanly. The antenna wants to be exactly the length that matches the wave. Slide the stack, you change the length, change the length, you change what frequency it's resonant on. Get it wrong and part of your power just bounces off the antenna, comes back down the coax, and stands in the feedline arguing with itself. That's standing-wave ratio. That's the two-point-six."

"An eighth of an inch of can," Theo repeated.

"Radio is the pursuit of exactness with garbage," said Si. "Proceed."

They re-measured. One-inch gap between the two inner cans, checked twice with Bea's steel ruler. Stacks snugged mouth to base. The analyzer came down from two point six to two point one — better, not right, and it still wandered when the coax moved.

"Still doing the mood thing," Priya said. She ran her hand down the feedline, and her eyes went narrow, and Jun watched her best friend become a detective. "The SWR shouldn't care where the coax *is*. Unless the coax is part of the antenna. Unless RF is coming back down the *outside* of the braid, which it's not supposed to—" She reached the feedpoint and stopped. "The choke."

The choke — four turns of the coax itself, coiled right at the feedpoint, the thing that kept radio frequency off the outside of the cable — had come half unwound in transport. It hung there like a dropped stitch.

"There," Priya said, with enormous satisfaction, and no small amount of pointing. "The choke is what makes the coax mind its own business. Without it, the feedline's radiating too, the whole system's the wrong shape, and the analyzer reads whatever it feels like. That was Field Day. Somebody's foot, probably."

"Probably my foot," Theo said. "I have large, talented feet."

They rewound the choke, four neat turns, and this time they zip-tied it hard. Then Jun took the paint marker and, with the ceremony of a woman christening a ship, re-drew every tuning mark on the boom and wrote above them: STORM MARKS. DO NOT ERASE.

The analyzer settled at one point eight to one and stayed there, even when Bea swung the coax back and forth like a jump rope.

"Kit rule," said Si. "Anything that can move, will move. Mark it, tie it, or lose it."

He slid off the stool and went to the barn door, and stood there with his coffee, looking southwest. The evening was calm and gold and entirely innocent-looking.

"Weather radio says Wednesday night," he said. "If it comes, it comes after dark, because they always do. Here's the drill, and it's the same drill as March and the same drill as Field Day: first thunder, everything comes off the radios. Everything. The net that's running during the storm is the Skywarn net, and it gets relayed from a grounded station — this one, by me. You four observe and log. After it passes, and only after, we find out what's broken and we get to work." He turned around. "Get permission from your people tonight. The farm's grounded, the generator's fed, and the barn sleeps five if nobody snores."

"I don't snore," Theo said. "I *project*."

"What about the school antenna?" Jun asked. "The J-pole. Should somebody check it before—"

"After," said Si, and it wasn't unkind, but it was final. "You check antennas after. You never climb toward weather. That pipe's been up there since March; it's survived every storm so far without you holding its hand. After this one passes, in daylight, with a spotter, you go up and you look at it. That's the discipline. Write it in your little book."

Jun wrote it in her little book.

She rode home with the windows down, and the whole town looked so ordinary it almost made her mad — sprinklers going, somebody grilling, a kid on a bike with a fishing pole. Above the shop she packed a bag the way Si had taught them to pack antenna kits: flashlight, batteries, the FT-60R with a full charge, her salvage log. Her mom pretended not to watch her do it.

"You'll be with Mr. Webb," Marisol said. It was not a question, and it was not permission, exactly. It was a mother doing math.

"And Mr. O. The farm's the grounded station."

"Mm." Marisol went downstairs and came back with a tin of the good coffee, the kind they sold to people who browsed. "Give this to Silas," she said. "Tell him it's a donation. What he serves is a crime, and during an emergency, crimes matter."

---

By six o'clock Wednesday the whole club was at the farm, and the barn smelled like ozone, coffee, and pierogi.

The pierogi were Babcia's — Bea arrived with a foil-covered tin and instructions: "For the radio people. She says don't be heroes." Marisol drove Jun out with the shop closed early and the good coffee on the seat between them; when Jun delivered it, Si held the tin up to the light like a man inspecting a gemstone.

"Your mother is a public servant," he said.

"She says during an emergency, crimes matter."

Theo came with his go-bag and his field recorder — "for the log," he said, with dignity, and nobody teased him. Mr. O arrived last, wearing Cassiopeia and carrying a county map.

"Shelter plan," he said, pinning the map to the chalkboard with two magnets shaped like planets. "If the power goes and stays out, the county opens a shelter at the Westridge gym. Ms. Delgado has the keys and the clipboard, which means the shelter will be the best-organized building in the county." He tapped the gym. "If it opens, I take the storm kit there and put a station on the air, so the shelter can talk to the county when the phones can't. Priya, if you're willing — I need a second brain."

"I bring my own clipboard," Priya said.

"Of course you do."

At dusk they walked the masts with Si — the ritual, the same one as March and as Field Day, and by now the kids knew it well enough to lead it. Bea checked the ground connections one by one, small hand closing on each clamp: the heavy copper strap running from each mast base to its ground rod, the arrestors where the feedlines came into the barn. "Grounded," she said at each one, quiet and exact, and Si said "say it like you checked it," and she did. Theo and Jun test-ran the generator in the lean-to — fuel, oil, choke — and the thing coughed once and settled into its ugly, wonderful chug, and the barn lights held steady.

"A machine you test before you need it," Si said, "or it ain't a machine. It's a wish."

Jun opened her salvage log to a fresh page and wrote at the top, because it felt like the kind of night that deserved its own columns: *STORM LOG. July 1.*

*20:15 — generator OK. Grounds OK. Sky going wrong to the southwest.*

The sirens went at 9:47.

They'd been watching the cell come on Si's weather radio and on the radar loop on Theo's phone — a red-and-purple line, unbroken, moving like something with intentions. At the first far-off thunder they'd done the ritual, same as March, same as Field Day: every feedline off the radios, the EFHW's coax unscrewed, switches thrown. The farm went from a station to a house in four minutes. The watch had become a warning, and the warning had Maple Run's name in it, and then the town's sirens stood up out of the dark — that long, rising, not-electronic wail that you feel in your back teeth — and Si was already moving.

"Cellar. Now. Radio people last, dog first."

Resistor, who hated thunder the way some dogs hate the mailman, had to be carried half the way, and Bea carried him. They sat in Si's cellar on canning jars and an old church pew he'd never gotten around to restoring — the shelves full of dusty Ball jars and two coffee cans labeled FUSES and FUSES (GOOD) — and they listened to the storm walk over the farm.

Jun had been in storms before. Everybody had. This one had a sound she'd only ever heard on the Field Day cell, and then only for twenty minutes: not thunder *cracks* but a continuous rumble, freight and low, and under it the rain arriving on the roof overhead like gravel poured out of a bucket that never emptied. The lights went out at 10:02. Bea clicked on a lantern. Nobody said anything for a while.

On the cellar steps, Si's handheld muttered the Skywarn net, and nobody answered it.

Jun's phone buzzed. She'd texted her mom at 9:50 — *at si's, cellar, all good* — and the message had been hanging there, undelivered, while the bars on her phone drained from two to one to none.

"Try a call," Priya said. Jun tried. *All circuits are busy. Please try your call again later.* Theo tried his. The same woman's voice, patient and useless, from four different phones in a row, until Bea pointed out they were making it worse.

"Every call we retry is one more person who can't get through," she said, and they all put their phones face-down like cards in a game they were losing.

"How do the phones go down?" Theo asked the dark. "Cell towers are *radio*. We're literally a radio club. Explain this to me."

"Towers are the last inch," Priya said. She had her knees hugged up on the pew, voice steady, the way she got steadier the worse things got. "Your phone talks to the tower, fine, but the tower talks to the world through cables and switches and buildings full of equipment, and all of it needs power. Knock out the power, the tower's a flagpole. Leave the power on and knock out everybody's *patience* — ten thousand people all calling their mothers at once — and the switchboards drown. Overloaded or dark, either way it's the same: the system needs the whole chain to work. Every link, all at once."

"And ham radio?" Bea asked.

"Ham radio is a person, a battery, and a wire," Priya said. "The chain is three links long and two of them are us."

Somewhere above them the freight sound passed and began, slowly, to not be over them anymore. At 10:21 the warning expired. They came up out of the cellar into a world that was dripping, black, and quiet — the whole of Maple Run dark except, far off, the blue strobes of a utility truck already moving.

The farm's power was out with everything else. Si stood in the barn doorway and sniffed the rain-washed air once, like a man tasting wine.

"Generator," he said.

The generator coughed alive in the lean-to, and everything went back on the way it had come off, Bea checking each connector against her list: off before, on after, never during.

The Skywarn net was still running on W7MR/R — had been for an hour — and for the first part of it Si did what he'd always done: reported from his own grounded station, rain and wind and *no damage observed from this location*, while the four kids sat along the workbench and logged, because that was the rule, the kids observe and log. Jun had the repeater up on her abuelo's FT-60R, volume low, and she wrote in the storm log by lantern light while Bea kept the formal sheet.

*22:38 — power out, town-wide as far as we can see. Cells dead (overloaded + dark towers). Repeater alive on site battery. Sirens 21:47. Nobody at this station hurt.*

The reports came in from all over the county, and they were the kind you wanted: trees on wires, wires down, a barn roof peeled on the county road, water over the low crossing on Route 9. One report came from their own side of town — a limb through a windshield on Delaney, two blocks from the shop, nobody in the car — and Jun felt Priya's hand land flat between her shoulder blades, once, and stay there a moment. The voice the club was waiting for never came: no injuries, no entrapments. At 11:15 the net closed with the weather service's thanks, and the repeater, for the first time all night, went quiet.

It stayed quiet for maybe four seconds.

"Whiskey Seven Echo Oscar Charlie," said a new voice, careful and official and tired, "the county emergency operations center, for any station monitoring." Si keyed from the big chair. "W7JUNK, go ahead, County." "W7JUNK, EOC. Phones are down county-wide, cells are saturated, and we're opening a shelter at the Westridge High gym within the hour. We could use a health-and-welfare net on this repeater for the night — shelter traffic, welfare inquiries, the works. Can the amateur community staff one?"

Si looked down the workbench at them, one by one, and Jun watched him do the arithmetic — four kids, two licensed operators, one grounded farm, one generator, one bucket — and then he keyed the mic.

"County, W7JUNK," he said. "Watch us."

---

They set the farm station like a crew that had rehearsed it — every Field Day position, every fox hunt, every Tuesday net had been rehearsal.

Si laid out the law before anybody keyed anything. "Theo is the voice of the net. I am the control operator — my station, my license, my responsibility, and my hand never more than a foot from the switch. That's the rule that's kept unlicensed voices legal on the air since before you were born: the licensed operator is right here, and we identify as W7JUNK at least every ten minutes. Bea's the log and the traffic desk — nothing moves unless Bea writes it down. Jun's on HF with me when the long-haul traffic comes." He looked around. "The repeater's running on its site battery. That means we keep transmissions short, we don't ragchew, and we leave pauses for stations with real emergency traffic to break in. Questions?"

"One," said Theo. "How is my voice?"

"Like a baritone with a job to do," said Si. "Net control. Open the net."

Theo took a breath you could hear, and Jun braced for the podcast voice, the *Marsh Hour* flourish — and it didn't come. What came instead was steady, low, and plain, a voice with the showmanship filed off of it.

"W7JUNK, W7JUNK. This is W7JUNK, net control station for the Fairview County health-and-welfare net, standing up at the request of the county emergency operations center. This is a directed net. All stations will hold traffic unless acknowledged by net control. Stations with emergency or priority traffic, call now." Pause. "Hearing none, we'll take check-ins. Come with your call sign, your location, and whether you have traffic."

They came out of the dark like porch lights turning on. Ed from the fairgrounds, fine, no traffic. A grandmother up on Larkspur Road with a battery radio and a downed maple, wanted her son in Bozeman to know she was fine — *traffic, one welfare, we'll take it after check-ins.* A firefighter at Station 3, listening from his truck. A voice that turned out to be Mr. Pruitt's next-door neighbor, checking in for Pruitt's whole street: "Pruitt says tell the radio kids the block's dark but nobody's hurt, and he knew it'd be you."

Bea's pencil never stopped. Theo worked them the way Si had taught them — acknowledge, confirm, move — and at the ten-minute mark, without being reminded, he glanced at the clock and said, "W7JUNK, net control standing by," smooth as turning a page. Si, one foot from the switch, said nothing at all, which Jun had learned was Si's highest praise.

*23:31 — net up. 14 check-ins first round. Theo steady. Storm log continues.*

Mr. O and Priya had left for the shelter at 11:40 with the bucket in the wagon, and at 12:20 the shelter came on the air.

"W7JUNK, W7JUNK, this is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, portable at the Westridge gym shelter." Mr. O's voice arrived scratchy, dropping syllables into the noise. "Shelter is open, sixty-some souls and counting, county staff on site, no injuries here. How copy?"

"N8OWL, W7JUNK, you're scratchy, three by three," Theo said. "What's your antenna?"

"Four cans and a bucket, net control," said Mr. O, and even through the static you could hear him smiling. "Stand by while my assistant performs a calibration."

What the assistant performed — Jun heard the whole story at dawn — was pure Priya. The gym's brick wall was eating the signal; the bucket stood in the building's shadow. She had Mr. Abernathy help her carry it forty feet out into the parking lot island, sleeved the second conduit section onto the mast to get the cans up over the roofline, guyed it with the rope and the stakes, and weighted the bucket with two gallon jugs of the shelter's drinking water.

"N8OWL testing," said the repeater, five minutes later, and Mr. O came through like the wall had never existed — full quieting.

"Shelter, W7JUNK," Theo said. "Now you're five-nine. Welcome to the net. We'll run a shelter status every hour. Do you have traffic?"

"W7JUNK, N8OWL. Affirmative. I have one welfare message, needs relay out of county — destination Denver, Colorado. It's from our custodian's wife." A pause, and the voice got gentler. "Dot Abernathy is here at the shelter with her husband. She's been trying to reach her sister in Denver since the sirens. Her mother's with the sister. The phones, as you may have heard, are down."

"Stand by, Shelter," Theo said. "Farm traffic desk, did you copy?"

Bea was already sliding a radiogram blank across to Jun, and Si was already pulling his chair closer to hers.

"You're the relay," he said. "I'll coach. Bea logs, you copy. Priya will read it slow. First thing to know about a radiogram: it's a form because forms don't panic." He tapped the blanks. "Preamble first — message number, precedence, station of origin, check, place of origin, time filed, date. Precedence for this is W — welfare. Why do you suppose we count the words?"

"So the far end knows if one got lost," Jun said.

"Look at you," said Si, almost to himself. "Count the words. X counts as a word — it's the period. Go."

Priya read it over the repeater, slow and clear, and Jun copied in her best block letters while the lanterns hissed:

*Number 7. Precedence W. Station of origin W7JUNK. Check 9. Place of origin Maple Run. Time filed 0030. Date July 2. To: Mrs. Helen Kessler, Denver, Colorado. Telephone 303-555-0134. Text: WE ARE FINE X TELL MOM WE ARE FINE. Signature: Dot.*

Nine words, X counted. Jun read it back to the shelter for confirmation, Priya said "QSL, solid copy," and then there was a radiogram on a clipboard at a farm in the dark, and the only way it was getting to Denver was the sky.

"How does a message for *Denver* move at half past midnight?" Jun asked.

"Forty meters," said Si, already reaching for the IC-7300's power switch. "D layer's gone to bed, so forty opens up regional — a thousand miles, easy, this time of night. Denver phones are fine; it's our end that's dark. Some ham down there takes the message, telephones the sister, and the radios have done what the phones can't." He looked at her. "And there's a net for exactly this, every night of the year, storm or no storm. The region traffic net. Most folks never spin the dial that far."

The EFHW — their wire, sixty-five and a half feet of dead-transformer magnet wire strung from Si's oak to the barn — took the IC-7300's tune at one point four and held it. Si found the traffic net the way you find a porch light, and checked in: "W7JUNK, with one welfare message for Denver."

The net control was three states away and sounded like a man reading a grocery list, bless him. "W7JUNK, hold for a liaison." Forty seconds of static. Then a new voice, older, unhurried, with the particular warmth of somebody who has done this ten thousand times and still likes it:

"Kilo Zero Oscar Lima Tango, K0OLT, Al in Denver. I can take the Denver traffic."

Mr. O, listening on the shelter frequency, broke in on the repeater at the same moment Jun said it out loud: "The log," she said. "November. The first contact this club ever made was a Denver station—"

"K0OLT, Al," Mr. O's voice came back through the repeater speaker, full of midnight delight. "I've had his QSL card on the corkboard since Thanksgiving. Al, this is N8OWL. You're the first voice these kids ever heard come out of a wire they built."

"Well," said Al, after a second, and you could hear him straighten up in his chair eight hundred miles away. "Then let's not keep their record waiting. W7JUNK, K0OLT — ready to copy. Who's sending?"

Si slid the mic across to Jun and kept his hand exactly one foot from the switch.

"K0OLT, W7JUNK," Jun said, and her voice only shook on the first syllable. "This is June, third party, with the control operator present. Number seven, precedence W, W7JUNK, check nine, Maple Run, zero-zero-three-zero, July two. To Mrs. Helen Kessler, Denver, Colorado. Telephone figures: three zero three, five five five, zero one three four. Text: We are fine — break — tell Mom we are fine — break. Signature: Dot. How copy? Over."

"W7JUNK, K0OLT. Solid copy, June, word after *fine* is *tell*. QSL number seven." A rustle of paper, eight hundred miles of sky collapsing into a voice in a barn. "I'll telephone Mrs. Kessler within the hour — her phone works fine, it's yours that don't. If her sister's got a reply, I'll bring it back at first light. Let's say fourteen point two-four-five megahertz, oh-six-hundred your time. Twenty meters wakes up with the sun — my sunrise first, then yours. K0OLT standing by."

"QSL, Al," Jun said. "We'll be here. W7JUNK."

"Course you will," said Al in Denver, and was gone into the static like a man walking back into his house.

The barn was very quiet. The repeater murmured. Bea turned the log page. And down on the workbench, in Jun's storm log, in handwriting that had gone a little loose:

*00:41 — Message #7 passed to Denver, 40m, K0OLT (Al — the Denver station from November, the FIRST contact we ever made, because radio is like that). Reply sked 0600 on 20m. Dot's mom is going to hear.*

The night went the way nets go — long, then longer, then strange and quiet around four in the morning, when the world has said everything urgent it has to say.

They worked in shifts and nobody slept. Si made the good coffee from Marisol's tin and drank it without comment, which was as close as he came to a testimonial. Theo ran the hourly shelter status with his voice going slowly husky, and caught himself, once, starting a flourish for the recorder — and flattened it back down before it got away from him. Jun saw Si see it, and saw Si let it pass unmarked, the way you let a good solder joint cool without poking it.

The Larkspur grandmother's message to Bozeman went out at 1:40, relayed up the line by the traffic net's Montana liaison as easily as if the phones had never existed. A utility crew checked in from the county line at 3:15 — trucks rolling in from two counties away, coming to put the grid back together. At 4:50, Bea ruled a line under the night's log and wrote the count in her precise hand: *forty-three check-ins, eleven pieces of traffic, zero injuries reported to this net.*

At 5:30 the barn door was gray. At 5:50 it was silver. Si tuned the IC-7300 up to fourteen point two-four-five and set the EFHW loose on 20 meters, and the band had that sound Jun remembered from January — thin and waking, like a house before the people are up.

"Gray line," Si said, nodding east, where the sky over the fields was going from charcoal to the color of a robin's egg. "Same trick as January, other end of the day. The absorbing layer's gone and the high layer's still charged from the night, and for about an hour the sky leans toward the sunrise. Denver's east of us. The band will come up with the sun and Al will be sitting in it."

The sun came up. At 6:03, out of a wall of static that parted like water, Al's voice arrived with the morning in it.

"W7JUNK, W7JUNK, Kilo Zero Oscar Lima Tango. Good morning, Maple Run. I have one reply, precedence W, for your number seven. Ready to copy?"

"K0OLT, W7JUNK," Jun said, pencil ready, and if her hand was steady this time it was because Bea's hand was resting flat and warm on the logbook beside her. "Ready to copy. Go ahead."

"Reply text: Mom is fine — break — told her — break — she cried then laughed — break — eat something green — break — love. Signature: Helen. Check seventeen. I delivered your message at one-fifteen this morning. Mrs. Kessler made me stay for coffee and told me about her sister's husband, who sounds like a man who keeps a good building." A pause, and the static sang under him like surf. "You did good work tonight, Maple Run. Seventy-three. K0OLT, clear."

*Check seventeen,* Jun wrote, and then sat looking at the words on the radiogram blank — *she cried then laughed, eat something green* — seventeen words that had crossed eight hundred miles of dark and come home with the sun, and her eyes stung and she decided it was the hour.

"Shelter, farm," Theo said into the 2-meter mic, and his voice did the steady thing. "N8OWL, this is W7JUNK. I have reply traffic for your desk — one message, for Dot Abernathy."

At the shelter, Priya copied it in her engineer's printing, and the net listened while she read it back, and then there was a long pause — the kind you don't fill — and Mr. O came back on.

"W7JUNK, N8OWL. Message delivered." You could hear the gym behind him, the echo of it, morning sounds starting. "For the log: she read it three times. Then she showed her husband. Then she read it to the front row of cots, and — Priya is informing me that the count was — eleven people cried, including one firefighter. Message delivered, net. That's — that's the whole ballgame, isn't it. N8OWL, shelter standing by."

The county stood the net down at 7:30, with thanks from the EOC. Theo signed them off — "W7JUNK, net control, closing the Fairview County health-and-welfare net at 07:30 local and returning the repeater to normal use. Stay safe out there. Seventy-three" — and set the mic down like it weighed forty pounds.

"Eight hours," he said. "I talked for eight hours. I've never talked for eight hours in my *life*."

"You ran a net," said Si.

"The net ran," Theo said. "I just held the mic."

Si looked at him for a long moment and then went to make more coffee, and Jun filed the exchange away the way you file a good part: *Theo Marsh, humility, one (1), condition: excellent, potential: high.*

At 6:58 that morning, while they were stacking cups, Jun's phone buzzed with a chime that sounded, now, almost quaint. Her text from 9:50 the night before — *at si's, cellar, all good* — had finally delivered, nine hours late, as the towers woke up one by one. Under it, new, from her mom:

*Knew you were fine. You're your grandfather's girl. Bringing breakfast to the farm anyway.*

---

They checked the J-pole on Friday, in daylight, after — always after.

Mr. Abernathy unlocked the roof hatch himself, and Theo stood spotter at the ladder base with both hands on it and his recorder nowhere in sight, because some jobs you don't narrate. The roof was littered with storm trash — twigs, shingle grit, one complete bird's nest from somewhere — and a dead limb the size of a baseball bat lay six feet from the scavenged tripod, where it had missed.

The J-pole stood straight. Fifty-seven and a half inches of half-inch copper, the nineteen-inch stub, the feed point exactly where they'd sweated it into place in March. Jun ran her eyes down it the way Si had taught them — mounts, joints, the four-turn choke, the coax drip loop — while Bea hooked up the analyzer they'd hauled up in a bucket on a rope, because you don't climb with your hands full.

"One point two," Bea said. "Same as spring."

"Log it inspected," Jun said, and Bea did.

Nobody had climbed toward the storm in the dark. Nothing on this roof had needed them to. That was the whole lesson, and it fit in one line in Jun's storm log: *antenna fine because we left it alone and built it right.*

Mr. Abernathy walked the roof edge with them before they went down, checking his building the way custodians do, hands in pockets. He didn't make a speech. He just said, at the hatch, "Dot's got that message form on the refrigerator. Next to my bowling schedule, which is the place of honor in our house." And: "Helen called Thursday night, soon as the phones came back. Mom's fine. Says eat something green." He held the hatch for them, and added, to the roof generally, "Built right," in a tone that suggested he was putting the radio club on the short list of things in his building he didn't have to worry about.

Saturday's *Courier* had the storm on the front page above the fold — the grain leg, the utility trucks, the shelter cots — and below the fold, the photograph that would live on the club's corkboard forever.

A photographer had come through the shelter Thursday morning, hunting for human interest, and had found Priya. Or rather, Priya had found him — "he was framing the cots, and I redirected him" — and walked him out to the parking lot island, where four soda cans on a broomstick, mounted in a five-gallon bucket guyed with rope and weighted with borrowed water, stood catching the morning light.

The caption read: *THE POP-CAN DIPOLE — an antenna built from scavenged parts by Westridge High's amateur radio club. Student-built stations kept the shelter talking to the county through the outage, relaying eleven messages.* And the headline over the whole spread, in the *Courier*'s biggest type:

**WHEN THE PHONES WENT DOWN**

Mr. Pruitt bought ten copies and brought nine of them to the barn.

"A bucket," Theo said, with the air of a man examining his own tragedy. "Eight hours of net control. A voice that FM stations would *kill* for. And the paper runs a photograph of a bucket."

"It's a good bucket," Bea said.

"The cans catch the light," Priya agreed. "I told the photographer to wait for it."

The school board's summer session met the second Tuesday of July, in the cafeteria, in that dead-air hush particular to meetings held while everyone would rather be at the lake. Ms. Delgado had called Mr. O the week before and said, "The club should attend," in the tone that made attendance a geological fact.

It took eleven minutes. Ms. Delgado read the record in her flat, exact voice: four members sustained since September, a public demonstration at Field Day in front of the board chair herself, and — she turned a page — eleven messages relayed during the July first outage, "when," she added, "nothing else in the county was working."

Mr. Pruitt stood up from the back row in a fishing hat, uninvited, and said he'd come as a citizen. "These kids fixed my speakers in April, when it was my problem," he said. "In July it was everybody's problem, and they fixed that too. I'd like that on the record, whatever the record is."

Mr. Abernathy didn't stand. He just held up, over his head, a photocopy of a radiogram form — the original was on his refrigerator next to the bowling schedule — and the room read *SHE CRIED THEN LAUGHED X EAT SOMETHING GREEN* in a silence you could have hung a coat on.

The board chair, who had stood in the Field Day dawn mud with a paper cup of coffee, looked down the table and asked, as if she already knew: "Is it always like this?"

"Apparently," said Ms. Delgado, "yes."

The motion passed without discussion. The Westridge High Amateur Radio Club was made permanent. The club received its own key to Room 114-B — an actual brass key, which Ms. Delgado produced from her clipboard like a woman who had been carrying it for months — and one line in the district budget, which the chair read aloud twice because she didn't believe it the first time:

"Four hundred dollars. Antenna fund."

That's when the room lost it, because Priya had stood up, ledger in hand, and said, "For the record: the club spent thirty-eight dollars all year, and that includes the solder."

"Spend it wisely," the chair said, when she could.

"We could buy *new* copper," Theo whispered, in the parking lot after, dizzy with possibility. "Copper that has never held *anything*."

"August," said Mr. O, straightening his tie. "License exams. The library. All four of you — you've borrowed my supervision long enough."

---

Si had come to the meeting in a clean shirt, which the club agreed not to discuss. He was waiting by his truck when Jun came out, and he didn't say anything about the vote, or the money, or the key she was turning over in her palm. He just watched her do it for a moment, and then said, quiet, almost to the windshield:

"Your grandfather would like this."

Jun thought about the green dial in the dumpster radio. The FT-60R on her belt, scuffed by two decades of somebody else's careful hands. The wire on the Tucson roof, catching the light.

"He built half of it," she said.

Si nodded once, like a man checking a ground clamp, and climbed into the truck. "August," he said, through the window, which was Si for *well done.* "Tell your mother the coffee was adequate."

Jun clipped the Room 114-B key onto her belt loop, next to the radio, where it belonged — brass next to battered black plastic, the school's past and her grandfather's, riding together into whatever August was going to be.

She had a feeling it was going to be loud.
