## Chapter 4: The Tape-Measure Yagi

The tape measure retracted with a sound like a starting pistol, and the loose end of yellow steel whipped back across Si's workbench and lay there shivering, like a snake that had made its point.

"Lock's busted," Si said, not looking up from the kerosene heater he was feeding. "That's why it was in the bin. Mind the edges, all of you. A broken tape bites whoever's holding it."

It was the second Saturday of December, the first real snow of the winter sifting down past the open barn door, the meadow beyond gone the color of notebook paper. The barn had its winter smell — cold sawdust, kerosene, the ghosts of every engine that had ever leaked onto that floor — and Resistor, Si's ancient mutt, had claimed the one warm square yard of concrete in the whole building and lay in it like a throw rug with opinions.

On the workbench, under the hanging lamp, Si had laid out the morning's materials with the ceremony of a man dealing cards: one twenty-five-foot tape measure, an inch wide, its yellow case cracked down the side; a ten-foot stick of white PVC pipe; a paper bag of PVC cross-fittings; a coffee can full of stainless hose clamps; and a coil of black coax cable, thick as licorice.

"Last month," Si said, settling onto his stool, "you put a wire in the sky and the sky answered. And at the end of it I asked who wanted to hunt a hidden transmitter, and every hand in the room went up. Even hers." He nodded at Bea, who was sitting on an upturned bucket with her mittens still on, and who gave him one solemn nod back. "So. The game is called a fox hunt. Somebody hides a transmitter — the fox — and you find it with a receiver, an antenna, and your own two legs. It's older than I am, which is saying something."

"How big is the transmitter?" Theo asked.

"Handheld radio in an ammo can. Sits in the woods all day on a battery, saying its name."

"Then how hard can it be?" Theo said. "It just sits there. Saying its name."

"Fairview County Park," said Si, "is eleven hundred acres." He let that sit. "You were saying?"

"I was saying," said Theo, "that I welcome the challenge."

Si reached under the bench and came up with a handheld radio of his own — old enough that its corners had worn smooth — and set it on the bench next to Jun's FT-60R. Two radios, two stubby rubber antennas, identical cousins. "You already own the only receiver you need. The trouble is this fellow." He tapped the rubber duck. "It hears equally well in every direction. North, south, up, down, off the barn roof. No opinions whatsoever. That's a virtue in a friend and a disaster in a fox hunt. You can stand ten feet from the fox and your meter reads about the same as it did a quarter-mile back. What you need is an antenna with a front on it."

"A directional antenna," Priya said.

"A yagi," said Si. "Invented by two Japanese gentlemen named Uda and Yagi, about a hundred years back, which is why it properly has both their names and why nobody ever says the first one."

He chalked three parallel lines on the painted blackboard rectangle that hung on the barn's one clean stretch of wall, and labeled them, back to front: REFLECTOR. DRIVEN. DIRECTOR.

"Three pieces of metal, each one around a half-wavelength, lined up on a stick. Only the middle one — the driven element — connects to your radio. The other two are freeloaders. The wave comes in, every element soaks it up and shouts it back out — re-radiates, is the five-dollar word — and because the reflector is a little too long and the director is a little too short, all that shouting stacks up in one direction and cancels in the other. The antenna listens hardest forward. That's *directivity*. Nothing electronic in it. No batteries, no brains. It's geometry with opinions."

"How much harder does it listen?" Priya asked. The mittens were off. The graph-paper notebook was out. Jun recognized the posture: a bird dog on point.

"This design, about seven dB over a dipole," said Si. "Seven dBd, we say."

"Decibels," Priya said. "Three dB is double the power."

"Very good."

"So seven dB is—" the pencil moved— "about five times. Five times the received power of a plain dipole, in the favored direction."

"Like cupping your hands behind your ears," Si said, "except it works on radio waves, and you look marginally less silly doing it. Marginally."

Priya did the rest of the math out loud, because in three months Jun had never once seen her do math silently. "One-forty-six megahertz — two-meter band; they had done this math in October. Half-wave by the four-sixty-eight rule: thirty-eight and a half inches; a quarter wave, nineteen point three." She looked at the chalk lines. "But your reflector is *longer* than a dipole and your director is *shorter*. Why does long-behind and short-in-front push the pattern forward?"

Si glanced at the rafters, the way he sometimes glanced at Mr. O when Mr. O was there to be glanced at. "Timing," he said. "The reflector's too long to be resonant, so it re-shouts a beat late. The director's too short, so it's a beat early. Late behind you and early in front of you both add up to *right on time* out ahead — and off the back they land on each other's heads and cancel. That's the whole trick. A century of antenna engineering, and half of it is getting metal to argue with itself on purpose."

He pulled a folded sheet of paper from his back pocket — a photocopy gone soft at the creases, covered with dimensions in a draftsman's tilted hand. "This particular design comes from a ham named Joe, call sign WB2HOL, who got tired of watching fox hunters smash four-hundred-dollar antennas against tree branches. His costs nine dollars, folds flat, fits under a car seat, and does not care what it hits. People have run them over with trucks." He handed the sheet to Priya, who received it like a relic. "You'll build two, because you hunt in teams. I'll be over here drinking my coffee and supervising, because that's the arrangement."

"You made that arrangement with yourself," Jun said.

"It's held up for years," said Si.

Jun opened her salvage log on the corner of the bench and wrote under the date:

*Entry 241. One 25-ft tape measure, lock busted, case cracked. Source: Si's bin. Condition: vengeful. Potential: it knows which way north is. It just won't say.*

---

They laid everything out before anything got cut — the way Si had made them do in October with the pop-can dipole — and Priya pinned the WB2HOL sheet to the blackboard with a magnet and chalked the cut list beneath it in her small furious handwriting:

- Reflector — 41 3/8 in (1)
- Driven element — 17 3/4 in (2)
- Director — 35 1/8 in (1)
- Boom — 3/4-in PVC, 24 in (cut 2)
- Spacing on the boom — reflector to driven, 8 in; driven to director, 12 1/2 in
- Feed — hairpin match, about a 1-in gap; RG-58 to a BNC plug

"One tape measure builds both antennas," Priya announced, "with feet left over. Forty-one and three-eighths, plus thirty-five and an eighth, plus two seventeen-and-three-quarters — about nine and a third feet of tape per antenna. The tape holds twenty-five. The arithmetic is generous."

"Generous is good," Jun said. "Generous means I can mess up once."

"You can mess up twice," said Priya. "I did the arithmetic for that too."

Cutting a tape measure, they discovered, was a three-person job: one to stretch it along the bench, one to snip at the mark with Si's tin snips, and one to stand clear. The tape did not go quietly. It was spring steel in a paint job, and it wanted to curl, and it wanted to snap home, and when Theo — measuring out the director — let his thumb wander onto the release button out of pure curiosity, the entire twenty-five feet came home at once with a noise like a deck of cards hitting a fan, and everyone in the barn ducked, and Resistor lifted his head, judged the matter beneath comment, and went back to sleep.

"I meant to do that," said Theo, in the voice of a man who had absolutely not.

"The edges," said Si, from his stool, "are now scalpels. Every cut end gets filed, and every filed end gets a cap of electrical tape, or you'll donate blood to this project before lunch. Ask me how I know."

"How do you know?" Bea said.

Si held up his left thumb, which bore a thin white scar across the pad, and went back to his coffee.

So Jun filed — sixteen razor edges into sixteen blunt ones — and Bea followed behind her with the electrical tape, folding a neat black cap over every end, each one pressed smooth with her thumbnail, each one exactly like the last. It took the better part of an hour, and nobody complained, because there was something deeply satisfying about it: the pile of bright yellow elements growing on the bench, measured to the sixteenth, ready to become an instrument.

---

Jun took boom duty. The PVC cut like cheese in Si's miter box — two twenty-four-inch sticks, square-ended — and then came the layout. With a tape from Si's tool wall — a working one, which got a laugh — she marked each boom at one and three-quarter inches from the back end, then eight inches on, then twelve and a half. Reflector, driven, director. Twenty and a half inches of business on a twenty-four-inch stick.

"A two-foot boom for a two-meter band," Theo said. "I approve of the economy."

The cross-fittings slid on at the marks, and then came the fussy part: the three crosses on each boom had to lie in the same plane, so the elements would run parallel to one another. Priya sighted down each boom like a surveyor and had Jun nudge the fittings a degree at a time. "The geometry argues with itself on purpose," she said. "Which means we don't get to argue with it by accident. Parallel."

"Parallel," Jun agreed, nudging.

The elements went through the crosses — one piece of tape per cross, pinned under two hose clamps apiece — and centering them was an exercise in patience: measure the overhang left, measure the overhang right, nudge, measure again. Bea turned out to be the best at it. She had small hands and no hurry in them at all, and she could feel a sixteenth of an inch of difference with her fingertips the way Jun could feel a stripped screw head. By the fourth element, Jun had stopped double-checking her. "Center," Bea would say, barely aloud, and tighten the clamp with the nut driver, and it was.

Then came the driven element, which was the heart of the whole machine and therefore the hardest.

Two seventeen-and-three-quarter-inch pieces of tape went onto the middle cross with their inner ends facing each other across a one-inch gap. Jun scraped an inch of paint off each inner end with the file, down to bright steel — the tape was painted like a road sign, and solder, Si observed from his stool, would no more stick to paint than a cat to water. Then the hairpin: a U of bare solid copper wire, bent like an oversized staple, bridging the gap and soldered to both bare ends.

"What's it *for*?" Theo asked, watching Jun tin the iron.

"Matchmaking," said Si. "Those two halves, with that gap between them, present themselves to the coax at the wrong price. The hairpin haggles it down to a number the radio likes. A folded dipole's worth of cleverness in a penny of copper wire."

"That's not an explanation," Theo said. "That's a parable."

"Parables are what you get before lunch," said Si.

The soldering was the kind of work Jun liked best — the kind that punished impatience. Steel tape drank the heat straight out of her little iron, so she brought up the big gun, Si's ancient Weller with the trigger, and held it until the metal itself wanted the solder. The first joint she made came up a gray, grainy blob that cracked the moment she flexed it. Cold joint. She did it again, hotter and longer, and the second one flowed on like mercury and stayed. Si watched all of this without comment, the way he watched everything, and when the joint held he said only: "There you go."

The coax dressed straight back along the boom, center conductor to one half of the driven element, braid to the other. BNC plugs on the radio ends (through the adapters from October) — crimp, check, tug. And then, because Jun came from a family that labeled things, two bands of colored vinyl tape went onto the booms: blue on one, red on the other.

"Blue is ours," Priya said immediately. "Accuracy is a form of respect."

"You just want the blue one," Theo said.

"Accuracy," Priya repeated, "is a form of respect."

They held the finished antennas up at arm's length, one apiece — three and a half feet of bright yellow steel in a T, light as a tennis racket, the tape elements curling gently upward at the ends, as if each one were still trying to measure the sky. Outside, the snow had thickened. The meadow was gone entirely now, erased to soft gray-white, and the flakes came through the open barn door a few at a time and died on the concrete.

"Test," said Si, and drained his coffee, and stood.

---

Si's test was simple the way a chess problem is simple. He took a third handheld off the bench, clipped it to his coat, and shrugged the coat on. "Every minute, on the minute, I'll key five seconds and say my name. You'll stand in the yard and tell me which way I went." He whistled Resistor up off the warm concrete. "No peeking out the door."

"What stops us from just listening for the dog tags?" Theo called after him.

"My integrity," Si said, and walked out into the snow and was gone — swallowed by it before he'd crossed the barnyard, which was, Jun thought, exactly the sort of exit he enjoyed most.

They gave him five minutes by the clock on Priya's phone. Then they carried the radios and the yagis out onto the packed gravel of the barnyard apron, where the snow came down on their hoods and gathered in faint white stripes along the yellow tape.

The radio came alive on the minute, as promised: five seconds of dead carrier, and then Si's voice, flattened and dry — "W7JUNK, test" — and silence. Jun swept the blue yagi through a slow circle, arm's length, elements vertical like fence posts.

The meter did nothing useful at all.

Full scale toward the barn. Full scale toward the meadow. Full scale — impossibly — toward the woodpile, and Jun had just opened her mouth to say *he's behind the woodpile* when Priya, twenty feet away with the red antenna, said, "I have a maximum toward the road," in a tone of scientific affront.

They swept again, slower. Peak everywhere. Null nowhere. The meter sagged a little here and there, vaguely, like it was thinking things over, and then the next transmission came and pegged the needle in whatever direction they happened to be pointing. Theo tried his with the solemnity of a man dowsing for water, and announced that Si was inside the barn, behind them — which was the one place they had all watched him *not* go.

"Maybe there are two transmitters," Theo said.

"Maybe the snow is reflecting the signal," Priya said, and then, immediately, to herself, "no, that's not a thing at two meters — is it—"

Jun had stopped listening, because she'd caught something. The coax came off the driven element and hung in a loose black loop from the boom down to the radio in her other hand, and when she shifted her grip — gathered the slack into her fist to keep it out of the snow — the meter *moved*. Not a little. A whole division. She let the loop dangle; the needle moved back. She wound two turns of coax around her forearm, and the entire pattern slid sideways, the peak wandering off the barn like a dog losing a scent.

"Priya," she said. "It's the cable."

Priya came over and watched her do it again — coil, and the meter swung; dangle, and it swung back — and Jun could see the argument happening behind her best friend's eyes, the hypothesis assembling itself with its usual total lack of hesitation.

"The feedline is receiving," Priya said. "The coax is supposed to be a pipe. Signal on the inside, world on the outside, braid in between like pipe insulation. But if RF current is flowing on the *outside* of the braid—"

"—then the whole cable is an antenna," Jun said. "A long, floppy one with no pattern at all, bolted onto our good one."

"And it hangs off the boom right beside the driven element, and we're waving the entire mess around in the near field, and every time the cable moves, the fake antenna moves with it." Priya stopped. "Common-mode current. Si said those words in October and I wrote them down and I did not understand them, which I *hate*." She already had her phone out, texting one-handed, the other hand still holding the red yagi over her head like an extremely nerdy Statue of Liberty. *TEST FAILED,* Jun read over her shoulder. *BEARINGS ARE NONSENSE. IS IT THE COAX?*

The reply took thirty seconds: *WHAT DOES THE COAX DO WHEN YOU MOVE IT?*

"He knows," Priya said. "He knew the whole time. He sent us out here to fail on purpose."

"That's his entire teaching method," Jun said. "Failure with a dog present."

*IT MOVES THE METER,* Priya typed.

This time the reply was one sentence: *OCTOBER. THE POP CANS. WHAT DID I MAKE YOU PUT ON THE FEEDLINE?*

And Jun had it, all at once, the memory arriving whole: the broomstick, the soda cans, Si's knotted finger pointing at the coax where it left the feedpoint, and the coil — four neat turns, taped, right at the junction. *Keeps the RF off the outside of the coax*, he'd said, *where it doesn't belong.* She had written it in the log without understanding it, same as Priya.

"A choke," she said. "Priya. It needs a choke."

They did it in the barn, on the bench, out of the snow, with Si still lurking somewhere out in the white — he texted once more, *7 TURNS, BEHIND THE REFLECTOR. DON'T MAKE IT PRETTY. MAKE IT WORK.* So they took the slack of each coax and wound seven turns of it into a coil you could just fit a fist through, zip-tied the coil flat against the boom right behind the reflector, and dressed the rest of the cable straight back along the boom the way it should have been all along. It took eleven minutes, counting the two zip ties Jun put on crooked and had to cut off and do over. The finished coil looked like a black doughnut zip-tied to the boom — homely, slightly lopsided — and Jun loved it with a fervor all out of proportion, because it worked.

"Why behind the reflector?" Bea asked. It was her first question of the day, and it was a good one — the coil could have gone anywhere along the cable, physically, and she wanted to know why it went *there*. Priya answered while Jun held the turns in place.

"Because the current sloshes back onto the cable right at the feedpoint, so that's where you cork it. And the back of the boom is the dead zone — behind the reflector, where the antenna isn't listening. You tuck the coil there so the coil itself doesn't get into the geometry." She cinched a zip tie. "Also it's out of the way. Engineering is partly aesthetics."

They went back out. The next transmission came on the minute, and Jun raised the blue yagi and swept it, and the difference was not subtle. There was a peak — broad, honest, a good thirty degrees wide, out over the meadow toward the creek line — and when she turned her back on it and swept again, the meter didn't just sag. It fell off a cliff: one spot in the circle where Si's five seconds of carrier nearly vanished into the snow-hiss of the band. A null like a notch cut with a knife.

"Oh," Priya said, beside her, hearing her own radio do the same thing. "Oh — the *null* is the instrument. Look. The peak is a mile wide, but the null is five degrees. You don't aim the loudness. You aim the silence."

Bea took her turn with the red boom without being asked. She swept it once, found the notch, held it there, and made a small, surprised sound — half a laugh — the first one Jun had heard from her all day. Then she found it again, to be sure.

"Front-to-back," Jun said, remembering the chalk lines. "How much louder the front is than the back."

"Si says gain impresses your friends," Theo called, from the far end of the yard, sweeping the red antenna like a saber, "but the null wins hunts."

"Si said nothing of the kind," Priya called back.

"He was about to," Theo said. "You could tell."

They took turns: one bearing each, then a walking fix — fifty paces left, bear again, the lines crossing — and the lines met at the woodpile behind the barn. Which was, Jun noted, the first place she had guessed, back when the meter was lying to her.

Si was sitting on a stump on the far side of it, out of the wind, reading a paperback with Resistor's chin on his boot, exactly as dry as if he were indoors.

He looked up and checked his watch. "Half an hour to find a man with a dog, at two hundred feet, with a nine-dollar antenna," he said. "Sloppy. But you'll do." He stood and knocked the snow off his coat. "What was wrong?"

"Feedline was hot," Jun said. "Common-mode current on the outside of the braid. The cable was its own antenna."

"And now?"

"Seven turns," Priya said. "Behind the reflector. Don't make it pretty, make it work."

Si's mouth did something that, on anyone else's face, would have been a smile. "Common-mode current. Write it down this time, both of you. It'll be back. It always comes back."

He looked at the coil zip-tied to the boom, and at the four of them standing in his woodpile with white shoulders. "Now it'll hunt," he said, and went in to make more coffee, which was as close as the man came to applause.

He paused at the barn door. "One more trick for Saturday. When you get close and the meter pins and the null drowns — tune five kilohertz off frequency. Deafen the radio on purpose. A half-deaf meter is a sharp one." The door swung shut behind him.

They trooped in after him. The coffee was terrible. Nobody said a word about the coffee, because it was hot.

The hunt was set for the following Saturday, at Fairview County Park, under a sky the color of dishwater that was giving off a light, patient snow — the kind that could keep it up all day without ever committing to being a storm. The park in winter was a different country from the picnic grounds Jun knew from July: the lake skimmed with ice at its edges, the softball fields plowed under white, and the sledding hill standing up out of the middle of it all like a breaching whale, dotted with the bright specks of kids on plastic sleds.

Mr. O was waiting in the main parking lot with a clipboard and a thermos, wearing a parka and — in defiance of the season — a constellation tie under the open collar. Cygnus again. Si had come and gone at dawn to hide the fox; nobody had seen where. He sat now in his truck with the heater running, reading his paperback, officially uninvolved.

"Rules," said Mr. O, and flipped a page on the clipboard. "The fox is somewhere within the park boundary. It is a handheld radio in an ammo can, running on a battery, transmitting on one-four-six point five-six-five megahertz, simplex—"

"Why not the repeater?" Theo said.

"Because we know where the repeater lives," said Mr. O, "and because the entire county would like to use it this morning. The fox is simplex, twenty seconds out of every minute, and it ends every transmission with its call sign in Morse code, because the FCC is the FCC, even in a snowstorm. Si built a little keyer into the can that does it all automatically. You will take bearings, you will walk, and you will find it. On foot." He looked at Theo over his reading glasses. "Feet. No wheels. The walking is the game."

"Noted," said Theo, with dignity.

"One more thing," Si called through the truck's open window. "The fox's antenna is vertical. Hold your elements up-and-down, like fence posts, or you'll hear half of nothing. A wave has an orientation — polarization — and yours had better match his. And sing out before you swing. You're carrying three feet of steel at eye level."

Before they started, Mr. O made them run the control experiment — the one that made the whole morning make sense. Jun unscrewed the yagi's coax and switched the FT-60R back to the little rubber duck it had been born with, and when the fox came up on the minute — twenty seconds of quiet, dead-air carrier, then a brisk string of dits and dahs, then silence — she turned a full slow circle in the middle of the parking lot with the meter held where everyone could see it.

Full quieting. Full scale. All the way around. The signal was enormous, and it was *everywhere*.

"That," said Mr. O, "is an antenna with no opinions. A fine antenna for talking to the repeater. It could not find its way out of this parking lot. That is why you spent last Saturday getting bitten by a tape measure." He clicked his stopwatch. "Teams: Jun and Priya, blue. Theo and Bea, red. The fox is live. Go."

The first bearing was easy money. Both teams swept from the middle of the lot, and both nulls notched down on the same line: northeast, past the picnic grove, somewhere out toward the hill country of the park. Priya photographed the big trail-map sign at the lot's edge, zoomed it on her phone, and drew a line across it with her finger, mumbling the heading. Bea watched her do it and then quietly drew her own line in a pocket notebook, in pencil, with a protractor she produced from her jacket like a card sharp's ace.

"Sweeping," Jun called, and turned, and they walked.

The snow lay an inch deep on the trail and filled their footprints behind them almost as fast as they made them. Jun carried the blue yagi at arm's length, coax gathered in a neat loop now, zip-tied discipline from wrist to boom, and every sixty seconds the fox spoke and Priya hissed "mark," and they stopped and swung and walked the silence instead of the sound. Second fix, from the footbridge over the creek: the null swung east of north. The two lines crossed, on Priya's phone map, in the rough ground just beyond the sledding hill.

"He's up by the hill," Priya said. "It's a twenty-minute walk from here, and we could drive it in two—" she stopped herself. "I'm not suggesting it. I'm observing that it would be faster, which is a different thing."

"Observed," Jun said.

Which was exactly when the Volvo went by.

It was tan, and ancient, and roughly the shape of a loaf of bread, and it was proceeding up the park's loop road at a dignified twelve miles an hour with both passenger windows down and three and a half feet of yellow steel — banded with red vinyl tape — protruding from the passenger side at eye level, elements vertical, correct as you please. Bea was visible behind it, in the passenger seat, holding the boom with both hands and wearing the expression of a person who has lost an argument and is keeping the receipt.

Jun and Priya stood in the middle of the trail and watched it pass. Bea looked straight at them through the open window as it went by. The Volvo did not slow down.

"He's cheating," Priya said, with the flat certainty of a person reading a thermometer. "Theo is cheating at *radio*."

Eleven minutes later, climbing the far side of the creek valley, they watched the Volvo go by again in the opposite direction, red-banded antenna still out the window, still vertical, still correct as you please. Priya stared at it with mounting scientific outrage. "It's not even *working*," she said. "A car is a metal box. Every bearing they take in there bounces off the inside of the vehicle. They are direction-finding their own doors."

"Maybe they'll figure it out," Jun said.

"They will," Priya said, "the minute they consult their consciences. Bea's in that car. Bea's conscience could anchor a ship."

---

Jun and Priya had lost four minutes to standing on the trail laughing, which Priya logged, formally, as "mirth delay," and then they were moving again, up through the bare sumac toward the sledding hill. The fox was getting loud now — loud enough that the meter pinned hard at the top of every transmission and the null started to smear, drowned in sheer signal.

"Too loud to aim," Priya said. "We're going to walk past it and not know."

So Jun did the thing Si had shown them in the barn the week before, the fox hunter's poor-man's attenuator: she tuned the FT-60R five kilohertz up, off frequency, so the radio heard the fox the way you hear a party through a wall — present, muffled, manageable. The meter dropped into the middle of its range and became an instrument again. The null came back, narrow and clean, and they walked it the last two hundred yards: bear, walk, bear, walk, the silence swinging across the hill's shoulder like a searchlight in reverse.

The sledding hill rose out of the gray ahead of them, its flank scraped to dirt and slush by a morning of plastic sleds, kids trudging up it in single file like a very cheerful chain gang. The bearing did not point at the hill. It pointed *behind* it — into the windrow of plowed snow and dead leaves that the county plows had heaped along the tree line, where a row of low pines hunched against the wind.

"It's not on the hill," Jun said. "It's behind it. He hid it behind the sledding hill, where nobody sleds."

"Radio shadow," Priya said, delighted. "The hill's between the fox and the parking lot. He made the easy bearings wrong on purpose. The man is a *menace*."

They went around the shoulder, "sweeping" called at every turn, and the null walked them down the tree line, pine by pine, until Jun stood in front of one particular low bough, swept twice more — peak behind her, null dead ahead — and dropped to her knees in the leaf drift and lifted the bough.

Olive drab. Stenciled. A steel ammo can, half-buried in leaves, with a crack of open lid and, muffled inside it, a tiny electronic voice going *dit-dah-dah, dah-dah-dit-dit-dit, dit-dah-dah-dah* —

"Mr. O!" Priya bellowed, in a register Jun had never heard from her, and across two hundred yards of windrow Mr. O raised the stopwatch over his head like a finishing judge.

"Forty-one minutes!" he called, arriving at a half-run with Si strolling behind. "Forty-one minutes even, from the lot, on foot, in snow." He wrote it on the clipboard with ceremony. "For the record: an excellent time. For a first hunt, a suspiciously excellent time."

Jun opened the can. Inside, wedged in gray foam, sat a handheld radio wired to a keyer board the size of a matchbox, all of it running off a battery pack wrapped in a plastic bag against the damp — Si's handiwork, tidy and brown and functional, the soldering on the board as neat as stitching. The radio's speaker was aimed up through the crack of the lid, and as they knelt there in a ring, the twenty seconds came around again: the hiss of the carrier, and then the code, brisk and even, a little drumbeat of dits and dahs spoken into the snow.

Theo and Bea arrived on the third transmission — on foot, breathing hard, red yagi over Theo's shoulder like a rifle. The Volvo had been abandoned in the lot by mutual, unspoken agreement, and they had re-taken their bearings honestly from the trailhead and followed the same nulls in. "We were four minutes behind you," Theo said. "On foot. I want it noted that the fastest legs in this park belong to *Marsh Hour*."

"Noted," said Mr. O, and made an actual note on the clipboard. Then he looked at Theo over his reading glasses. "How were your bearings, Mr. Marsh? Before you parked."

Theo winced — Mr. O never called him Mr. Marsh — and looked at the sky, where his innocence was presumably written. Bea gave Mr. O the truth, because Bea did not lie; she merely declined, as a rule, to volunteer.

"Terrible," she said. "The car ate them."

"You were, in effect, measuring the shape of a Volvo," said Mr. O. "Disqualified from the running. You may keep the find — for honor."

"Mobile direction finding," Theo said, "is a recognized and time-honored—"

"So is walking," said Mr. O.

"It was an *experiment*," Theo said. "For the podcast. There is a whole episode in this. 'The Limits of Convenience.'"

"Twice," said Si, from the edge of the ring, holding up two fingers like a man calling balls and strikes — the number of times he had counted the Volvo on the loop road. He said nothing more, but Jun could see him shaking his head slowly, composing the version of the story he would tell at every hamfest for the next ten years.

"I was for honor the whole time," Bea said.

There was no answer to that, and nobody attempted one, because the fox was talking again, and this time they all simply listened. The dits and dahs came out of the can and up into the pine boughs, as even as a metronome, the same pattern every time, and it took Jun three full cycles to hear what was sitting in plain sight: it wasn't a jumble. It had a shape. *Dit-dah-dah. Dah-dah-dit-dit-dit. Dit-dah-dah-dah. Dit-dit-dah. Dah-dit. Dah-dit-dah.* A beginning, a middle, an end. The same nine seconds, over and over, like a chorus.

It was Bea who said it, kneeling closest to the can, her mittens planted in the leaves on either side of it.

"It's the same every time," she said. Not a question.

"Says my name," said Si. "Whiskey Seven Juliett Uniform November Kilo. The law says even a fox has to introduce itself."

"Your name has a *tune*," Bea said.

Si looked down at her. "Everything does, in code. Letters are just little songs. That one's mine."

"Say it again," Bea said, and nobody treated this as an odd request; they simply waited the forty seconds in the snow, a ring of people around a green can, until the carrier came up and the little drumbeat played, and Bea closed her eyes and moved one mitten, almost imperceptibly, with the pattern — *dit-dah-dah, dah-dah-dit-dit-dit* — getting it half right and not caring. Jun watched her best friend's quietest clubmate kneel in a leaf drift and conduct Morse code with a mitten, and she thought: *of all of us, Bea was the one who was going to hear it as music.*

---

Mr. O's thermos turned out to contain cocoa — "I have met Si's coffee," he explained, "and I plan ahead" — and they drank it in the picnic shelter with the fox can on the table between them, still dutifully naming itself to the empty park every sixty seconds, until Si reached in and switched it off with the tenderness of a man putting a good tool to bed. Theo took a photograph of the can for the group chat and captioned it *FOX: LOCATED. HONOR: INTACT.* Priya's reply appeared before he had pocketed his phone: *your honor was in a volvo.*

The two yagis leaned against the shelter post, and the light snow went on falling, furred along the yellow tape like icing on a bakery case.

Bea had been quiet all through cocoa, which was not unusual, and watchful, which was. She waited until the others were arguing about whether the drive home counted as a mobile fox hunt — Theo, warmed by cocoa, was testing the waters and finding them icy — and then she crossed the shelter to where Si stood zipping his coat.

"Could a person learn that?" she asked, very quietly.

Si didn't ask what. He knew what. He looked at her for a moment — the way he looked at a piece of salvage he was deciding about — and then he looked out at the snow coming down over the ball fields, and when he spoke it was as short and plain as everything he ever said that mattered.

"A person could," he said. "Some of the best ones do it in a winter. Come see me when the thaw starts, and bring your patience. It's the only part that costs."

Bea nodded once — the solemn nod, the flag going up — and went to help Jun coil the coax for the drive home, and Jun said nothing at all about what she had overheard. She just filed it away, the way you file a measurement you'll need later.

Behind them, under the pine at the tree line, the snow kept filling the leaf drift where the fox had been, and over the sledding hill the sky was the color of notebook paper, patient as ever, waiting to be written on.
