## Chapter 9: Morse in the Moonlight

The key under Bea's finger had been a clothespin in a previous life, and it was embarrassing how well it worked.

She had built it in February: one spring clothespin, one six-inch strip of brass from the three-for-a-dollar bin at Second Wind Thrift, two wood screws, and a drawer-pull knob, and she had never once shown it to anyone, because it was the kind of thing that required explaining, and Bea did not enjoy explaining. Press, and the brass touched the screw. Release, and the spring lifted it away. A heartbeat of contact was a dit; three heartbeats was a dah. The whole alphabet, hiding in a clothespin — exactly the kind of secret Bea liked best.

At the moment, the clothespin was clipped into a pair of alligator leads running to the ugliest noisemaker in Si Webb's barn — which was saying something, in a barn that Jun estimated held nine thousand objects, four hundred of them noisy. The noisemaker was an army-surplus practice buzzer the color of old soup, its buzz less a musical note than an insult with a pitch. But it was what the barn had, and the barn was where Bea was, on the third Saturday of April, with the big door open to the evening and Resistor asleep on her sneaker like a sandbag with opinions.

She sent her name, the way she always started, the way other people test a pen. Dah-dit-dit-dit. Dit. Dit-dah. B-E-A. The buzzer made her name sound like it had a sinus infection.

Across the barn, Theo was filming for the podcast until Si's voice came out of the stacks — "Nothing in here's for the podcast" — and Priya, turning over something with a waveguide flange, asked who on earth kept one in a barn. "Same man who keeps a lighthouse lens in the loft," Si said, and Theo lowered the phone.

The IC-7300 on Si's desk murmured a slow Morse conversation in the background — two operators trading dits in the early-evening band — and Bea found herself copying without meaning to: an R, an S, a 5, a 9, the way you start overhearing Spanish at the grocery store once you've begun to learn it.

"You wait for the letter," Si said.

He had come up behind her without any noise at all, holding a mug of the coffee the club had long ago agreed to stop describing out loud. He nodded at the clothespin. "Most people guess. They hear the first two elements and their pencil's already writing the letter they *want* it to be. You wait for the letter to finish. That's a good fist. Where'd a person get a good fist?"

Bea considered the question with her usual care. "The fox," she said.

Si would have remembered the fox even if she'd said more. The December hunt in Fairview County Park, the ammo can in the snow behind the sledding hill, the little transmitter inside it patiently spelling its own name in code, over and over, forever — the first time Bea had ever heard Morse as something other than noise. She had heard it as *music*, which was the only word that fit and which she had never said out loud to anyone: a song with letters in it. At the end of that frozen afternoon she had asked Si, very quietly, "Could a person learn that?" and Si had said, "A person could," and that had been the entire conversation.

What Si didn't know was everything after. The printed chart taped inside her history notebook. Three letters a day, every day — the alphabet in two weeks, then a second pass, because the first had been for her eyes and the second was for her ears. The free app that turned her finger-taps into beeps. Chanting under her breath in the lunch line — dit-dit-dit-dah, dit-dit-dit-dah — until Priya, without looking up, said, "V. You're doing V. Beethoven's Fifth. Congratulations, everyone at this table now knows." The night Babcia caught her at the kitchen counter tapping out P-I-E-R-O-G-I with one fingernail while the water boiled, and said only, "Your dziadek talked to ships like that," and never asked her to stop tapping again — which, from Babcia, was a parade.

"Four months," Si said, when she had told him the short version, which was very short indeed. He looked at the clothespin for a while. Then he looked at the buzzer with genuine sorrow. "And you've been learning music on *that*."

"It works," Bea said.

"It *works*," Si agreed, in the tone of a man conceding that a shovel is a violin. He set down his mug, said, "Wait there," and disappeared into the dark end of the barn, where he kept the things that were not for the podcast.

He came back wiping his hands on a shop rag, and put a small black object on the bench in front of Bea the way you set down a tool, not a present.

It was a telegraph key. A real one — built, like the Kenwood, in a decade that did not believe in plastic. A black crinkle-painted base the size of a paperback, worn through to brass at the corners. A navy-blue knob shaped by somebody else's decade of dits. A little shorting bar on a hinge, and a pair of coin-silver contacts that somebody — Bea would figure out later it had been Si, that morning — had recently cleaned to a shine. Stamped into the base, half worn away: J-38.

"Nineteen seventy," Si said. "Surplus store in Spokane. Four dollars, which was money back then. It's the key I learned on, when the government still thought I might grow up to be a radioman. There's a million of them in the world and most of them are asleep in drawers, which is a waste of a perfectly good switch with opinions."

Bea put her finger on the knob. The spring was firmer than the clothespin's, exact and clean, and the contacts met with a tiny precise *tick* instead of the clothespin's clatter. She sent one letter. Dah-dit-dah-dah. The buzzer, for once, sounded almost respectful.

"C," said Priya from the radio, without turning around. "Show-off."

"It's not a gift," Si said. "A gift is a thing you get from people who don't know you. It's a *tool*. Tools are for wearing out. That's the whole job of a tool." He picked up his mug and looked out at the light going long and gold across the meadow, and Bea understood — the way you understand a sentence in a language you're still learning — that the ceremony was over, that she had been handed something that mattered to him, and that the correct response was to not make it weird.

"Thank you," Bea said, which was two more words than she usually spent.

"Hm," said Si, which was one more than he usually spent back.

Then he nudged the buzzer with one boot, gently, the way you nudge a dog off a couch. "But that thing is a crime against music, and now you've got a decent key, which makes it worse. You tell Okafor —" he leveled the mug at her "— a five-five-five. Little timer chip, costs less than a pizza. Kit makes a practice oscillator with a proper tone, solder it in an evening, every one of you ought to have one. He'll know. Tell him if he buys the ones with the counterfeit chip, I'll know that too."

The J-38 sat on the bench in the long light, black and brass and patient. It fit under her hand as if it had been measured for somebody's hand once, and the somebody had turned out, four dollars and fifty-six years later, to be her.

The kits arrived in a padded envelope the size of a sandwich, and Mr. O opened it on the storeroom desk on Wednesday evening with the ceremony of a man dealing cards.

"One per victim," he said. "Si has spoken, and I do not argue with Si. He outranks me in the only army that matters."

Room 114-B had been rearranged for soldering, which meant the good lamp, the fan in the window, and the rules, stated once by Mr. O and once more directly at Theo: the smoke goes out the window, the solder goes on the board, and nobody — *nobody* — touches the iron to find out if it's hot. Bea sat at the end of the bench with the J-38 in front of her, because before any kit got soldered, the key needed a cord: three unlabeled screws on its back, because 1970 assumed you could figure it out. Priya slid the multimeter across without being asked, already set to continuity — Bea felt the small warm glow of having friends who answered questions with instruments — and two minutes of beeping later the cord, a two-conductor length off a dead lamp from Jun's backpack, went on under the two best solder joints Bea had made all year.

"Now," said Mr. O, and dealt the kits.

Each kit was a plastic bag of parts and a circuit board the size of a matchbook, with instructions photocopied so many times the schematic looked like a treasure map. Bea laid her parts out in ranks, which was how she liked to meet anything new:

- one NE555 timer chip, eight legs, smaller than a postage stamp;
- two resistors, striped like tiny candy;
- one little disk capacitor and one bigger one with a stripe down its flank;
- one small knob that was secretly a variable resistor;
- one speaker the size of a poker chip;
- one nine-volt battery clip;
- one jack, for the key.

"A code-practice oscillator," Mr. O said, "is the oldest first project in radio. Older than most air forces. You are joining a very long line of people who burned their first finger building a box that goes beep."

"The chip," Priya said, reading the schematic upside down because she could, "charges the little capacitor through the resistors until it hits a threshold, then dumps it and starts over. Charge, dump, charge, dump, hundreds of times a second. Every dump is one wiggle of the speaker. Seven hundred wiggles a second—"

"—is the note you hear," Mr. O finished. "And the knob changes how fast it charges, which changes the note. Which each of you will discover separately, at different times, at different volumes."

They built. Jun soldered the way she did everything with her hands — fast, neat, slightly showing off, each joint a bright little Hershey's Kiss. Bea went slowest and did not care; she did each joint shiny and conical and moved on, in order, the way she copied code — one element at a time, no guessing. Theo finished first, by a lot, announced it to the room, and clipped on his battery.

His oscillator produced a thin, dying squeal, like a balloon apologizing, and then nothing.

"The smoke stayed inside the components," said Mr. O, not looking up. "That is the entire warranty."

"My beautiful box is dead," Theo said. "I want it noted that I built it the *fastest*."

"Noted," said Priya. "In the log of things that don't work."

Bea pulled the magnifier lamp over and bent over Theo's board. The joints were fast joints — blobby, gray. And there, between two legs of the timer chip, a hair of solder no wider than an eyelash, shiny with guilt.

"There," she said. "Pins two and three are holding hands."

"That's not a technical term," Theo said.

"It's extremely a technical term," said Priya. "It's a bridge. Hold still."

Bea did the fixing — the wick, the iron, one patient pass, and the solder hair lifted away like it had never happened. Theo clipped the battery back on, and the oscillator woke with a clear round tone that slid up and down as he cranked the knob, and he made it sing opera until Mr. O reached over and turned the knob to a sensible pitch with two fingers.

By eight o'clock there were four oscillators on the bench, and the room sounded like a very small, very earnest swarm of bees. No two sang the same note, because the little capacitors were only accurate to twenty percent, which Priya declared "not a flaw, a *choir*," and they spent ten minutes tuning all four to match the sidetone of the club's Kenwood by ear — which was exactly like tuning four guitars, if guitars had one string and opinions.

Bea didn't say anything. She had her own oscillator hooked to the J-38 — her key, her cord, her joints — and she was sending her name again, slowly, and the tone was clean and round and entirely unlike the barn buzzer, and the letters came out of her fingers like they'd been waiting there all along. *It's not a beep,* she thought. *It's a voice that knows exactly one word, and the word is whatever you say it is.*

Mr. O was reading his phone. "Si says Saturday," he announced. "Quote: bring work gloves, bring the girl with the key, the moon is coming. Unquote."

Bea looked at the window, where the evening was settling in violet over the practice field. The moon was coming. She put the J-38 in her bag like it had always lived there.

---

The patient was waiting on Si's workbench Saturday morning: a transformer the size of a loaf of bread, black, heavy as a sin, with a faded paper label that said MAPLE RUN SKATING RINK in letters out of a vanished decade.

"Output transformer," Si said, "out of the rink's PA amplifier. The rink's been a parking lot since '98 and the amplifier died before that, and do you know what the copper thinks about any of that?"

"Nothing," said Jun, with the confidence of a girl answering her own catechism. "The copper doesn't care."

"The copper does not care." Si handed her the wire cutters. "Skin it."

The transformer came apart in layers — steel laminations fused like the pages of a drowned book, then the winding itself: turn upon turn of copper the color of strong tea, each strand wearing a jacket of varnish so thin you could see through it. "Magnet wire," Si said. "Enamel-insulated. The beautiful part is that a fifty-year-old transformer is just a very patient spool." Jun unwound it while Bea and Priya walked the free end down the driveway, the wire coming off kinked and springy, until Theo learned to straighten it with one hard pull around a fence post.

They needed sixty-five and a half feet, and the reason was Priya's, delivered with a stick in the driveway gravel because Si's barn had a whiteboard but the driveway was *right there*.

"Forty meters is the band, and seven point one five megahertz is the middle of where we want to live on it. The wire wants to be a half-wave long, and the recipe for a half-wave in feet is four sixty-eight divided by the frequency in megahertz." She wrote it in the gravel: `468 / 7.150 = 65.45`. "Sixty-five point four five. Call it sixty-five and a half, because my tape measure does not believe in hundredths."

"Why that frequency exactly?" Jun asked.

"Because a wire that length is *resonant* there," Priya said. "It rings, like a guitar string rings at its note. Feed it seven point one five and the wave runs down the wire, bounces off the far end, runs back, and meets the next wave at exactly the right instant — they stack instead of fighting. On resonance a piece of wire stops being a piece of wire and starts being an *instrument*."

"The radio can *taste* the difference," Si said. "String it."

They measured sixty-five and a half feet with Si's hundred-foot tape, added a dog's length at each end for tying and soldering, and cut it — Jun did the honors, and logged the transformer in her salvage log like a concert setlist. Then Si laid out the rest of it on the workbench: a gray ferrite donut the size of a cookie, a plastic electrical box, two binding posts, a coax connector, and a short doubled piece of the same magnet wire.

"FT240-43," Si said, tapping the donut. "Two-forty is the size of the donut, forty-three is the recipe of the iron. And this is an *unun*. You all met a balun in November; a balun marries balanced to unbalanced. An unun marries unbalanced to unbalanced — coax on one side, the end of a long wire on the other, two souls who want nothing to do with each other. Forty-nine to one."

"Why forty-nine to one?" Bea asked.

Si looked at Priya. So did everyone else. Priya sighed the sigh of a person who had been waiting to be asked since breakfast, and picked the stick back up.

"The end of a half-wave wire is a high place," she said. "All voltage, hardly any current — the wave gets to the end of the wire and has nowhere left to go, so it piles up. Electrically, that end looks like about two thousand four hundred and fifty ohms. The radio wants fifty. Twenty-four fifty divided by fifty—" she wrote it "—is forty-nine. So we need a transformer that steps forty-nine-to-one, and in a transformer, impedance goes as the *square* of the turns. Square root of forty-nine is seven." She underlined it twice in the gravel. "Seven to one in turns. Two turns for the radio, fourteen for the sky. Two to fourteen. That's the whole spell."

"A transformer," said Si, "is arithmetic you can hold in your hand. Wind it honest."

Bea wound it, because it was that kind of day, and because her hands were the patient ones. Fourteen turns around the gray donut, each passed through the hole and dressed snug — and for the first two turns, a second wire alongside the first, because the radio's two turns lived *with* the bottom of the big winding. The trick was the crossover: four wire ends that had to be twisted in exactly the right pairs, so the coax sat across the bottom two turns and the antenna wire rode all fourteen. Si drew the pairs on the whiteboard in marker, twice, and did not touch the iron.

It was around turn twelve that Theo, inventorying the binding posts, did his announcer voice — "*and the winding of the donut enters its final, fateful laps*" — and Bea laughed, and finished the turn she was on, and said "thirteen," and kept going.

Nobody heard her say thirteen. That would matter later.

They soldered the crossover, boxed the donut, and by the time the sun started its long spring slide they were ready to put a wire in the sky.

The plan was sixty-five and a half feet from the oak to the barn: the unun box bolted to the barn wall at twelve feet, the wire sloping up from it to a limb of the big oak at the meadow's edge. Before the ladder came out, Si ran the litany, like a checklist on an airplane.

"How far from power?"

"Twice the antenna's length," the four of them said, in ragged unison; they'd had the drill since October.

"Wire's sixty-five and a half. Nearest line is two hundred feet thataway—" he pointed west, at the poles marching down Larkspur Road. "Twice the length is a hundred and thirty-one. We're clear by half a meadow, and we *check it anyway*, because the day somebody doesn't check is the day the math changes." Then Jun took the foot of the ladder, both hands, planted — "spotter's not a suggestion" — and Bea went up with the rope over her shoulder, because it was her wire, and everybody knew it, and nobody said so.

The rope went over the oak limb on the second throw. They hauled the far end of the magnet wire up with a little sag left in it — "guitar string, not clothesline; a wire that can't move is a wire that breaks" — and tied off on a cleat Si had screwed into the oak's trunk in some earlier decade. At the barn end, Bea soldered the wire to the antenna post of the unun, scraping the enamel off the last inch first so the joint could bite, and fastened the counterpoise to the ground post: six and a half feet of hookup wire, stapled in a lazy zigzag down the barn wall.

"That little thing?" Theo said. "Six and a half feet of wire just *lying* there?"

"That's about a twentieth of a wavelength," Priya said. "The unun pushes against the antenna, and it needs something at its back to push *from*. A twentieth of a wavelength is all the ground a forty-meter wire asks for. It's not the earth. It's just… a handhold."

They ran the coax in through the barn wall to Si's desk, and Si hooked his analyzer to the far end, and everybody crowded in to watch the little screen draw the truth.

The truth was nine to one.

The analyzer's line ran flat and ugly across the whole band — nine-to-one, ten-to-one, no dip, no note, nothing. It was not an antenna. It was a very long resistor with a view.

"Huh," said Si, with no panic in it whatsoever. He flipped the coax switch to his own forty-meter antenna, and the speaker filled with a strong CW signal, somebody's evening ragchew, crisp as typing. He flipped back to the new wire. The same station was there, but smeared and small, like the band had caught a head cold — everything mush, nothing with edges. "Receivers are kind," he said. "Transmitters are honest." He plugged the IC-7300's key line in, turned the power down to a whisker, and held the key down for twenty slow seconds while the SWR meter climbed off the page. Then he laid two fingers on the little plastic box bolted to the wall.

"Warm," he said. He held his hand out. Bea touched the box. It was distinctly, undeniably warm, after twenty seconds at low power, the way a mug is warm.

"Heat's energy," Si said. "Energy costs. You paid the wall for that energy, and it went somewhere." Bea recognized the look: he had already seen the answer, and found answers stay found. "Where was that energy *supposed* to go?"

"Up the wire," Bea said. "Into the air."

"So if it's sitting in the box instead—"

"Something in the box is eating it," Priya said. She was already unlatching the lid.

They lifted the gray donut out into the lamplight. Priya traced the windings with one fingernail, then went still. "Count the turns," Si said. "Out loud."

Priya counted, touching each turn where it crossed the top of the core. "Ten, eleven, twelve." She stopped. Counted again. "Twelve."

Silence, except for the band murmuring.

"Fourteen," Priya said. "The spell says fourteen." She looked up, stricken, at Bea — and it was Theo who said, quietly and without his announcer voice, "That's on me. I did the voice. She laughed on thirteen."

"It was a *good* voice," Jun said.

"And this," Priya went on, because grief makes her thorough, and pointed with the iron's cool tip at the crossover: the four wire ends, twisted in their pairs, neat as embroidery — and wrong. The tap that should have put the coax across the bottom two turns was twisted one pair over, and the effect of it, when she traced it out loud, was a shorted turn sitting right on the core. "A shorted turn on a transformer is a furnace door," she said. "Everything the radio sends, the core pours into that one loop instead of the sky. That's the heat. That's the mush."

"Two mistakes," said Si. "One of counting, one of marrying the wrong wires." He handed Bea the cutters. "A person could get discouraged. Or a person could remember that the fix for both is the same fix, and it's forty minutes, and the donut does not hold a grudge."

Bea cut the winding off and unwound it, and started over.

This time they counted in pairs — Bea wound, Priya counted aloud, Jun counted silently against her, and any disagreement stopped the world. Fourteen turns, verified three ways. And at the crossover, Si made them read his whiteboard drawing back to him before a single twist was made — which pair, why that pair, what does the radio see, what does the sky see — and Bea twisted the right pairs and soldered them and trimmed the leads, and her joints shone.

"Hold the arithmetic honestly," Si said, which was as close to a blessing as he got.

They boxed it, reconnected the antenna wire and the counterpoise and the coax, and turned the analyzer on.

The line swept across the band, and this time it *moved* — sloping down, down, through two-to-one, through one-and-a-half, to a clean bright dip, and the number at the bottom of the dip, at seven point one five megahertz exactly, read: 1.4:1.

"One point four," Priya breathed, like it was a test score.

"Under two is flyable," Si said. "One point four is *singing*."

But the wire wasn't done showing off. Priya drove the analyzer up-band, curious, and the dip's sisters appeared: down around fourteen megahertz the line fell again — one point six — and again near twenty-one, and again near twenty-eight, all under two, all flyable. "Four bands," Priya said, looking up with her eyes doing arithmetic nobody had assigned. "One wire. It works on forty, twenty, fifteen, *and* ten."

"Overtones," Si said. "A bugle's got one tube and no valves and it plays a whole reveille. Second overtone, third, fourth. Your sixty-five and a half feet is one half-wave on forty meters — and two half-waves on twenty, three on fifteen, four on ten. And on every one of those, the end of the wire is still a high place, voltage piled up with nowhere to go — so the same forty-nine-to-one donut feeds them all." He flipped the coax back to the IC-7300, and the band came up out of the speaker with edges on it — stations standing up out of the noise like fence posts in low sun. "A wire with a chord in it. You built a chord."

They listened for a while, because you have to sit a minute with a thing that works. Outside, the magnet wire was catching the last of the light, a faint straight glint from the oak to the barn, sixty-five and a half feet of copper that had once made a skating rink louder.

"Come back when the moon's up," Si said. "This wire's a night animal. Full moon Friday — the almanac calls it the flower moon." He shut the analyzer off and reached for the terrible coffee. "I call it a sked."

---

Friday was the first of May, and the moon came up over the meadow like something the sky had been saving.

The whole club came out after dinner for the wire's first real night, and forty meters did not disappoint them. All week the band had been a polite hum in the afternoons; now, with the sun down and the D layer gone to wherever absorbed days go, it was a city at night — signal after signal standing up in the dark, and down at the low end, under the voices, a whole other weather system of dits and dahs, layered like crickets. Bea sat at Si's desk and copied letters off the air in the lamplight, whole call signs some of them, while the magnet wire outside caught the moon along its whole length, faint and steady, as if the meadow had grown a string.

One by one, the night collected its due. Priya's mother texted. Jun's mother texted twice, the second time in all caps. Mr. O gathered his carpool with his keys. And Bea had Babcia's rule — home by ten, no exceptions, *and I mean the door, not the driveway* — which Si had settled weeks ago in a negotiation conducted entirely in pierogi.

"I'll run her home at half past nine," Si told Mr. O. "Kowalczyk and I have an arrangement. I provide transportation. She provides the pierogi. It is the most balanced treaty I've ever been party to."

Then the taillights went down Larkspur Road, and it was Bea and Si and Resistor, and the barn door open to the moon, and the wire.

Si turned the dial down to the low end of forty, where the dits lived, and let a strong one fill the room — a station sending slow, even code, each letter a small exact shape in the air.

"Here's why that fellow gets through and the voices up the band are fighting for their lives," Si said. "A voice signal — single sideband — spreads its hundred watts across twenty-four hundred hertz, like a porch light, everywhere at once. CW is one note. All of it — every watt — in a lane maybe a hundred and fifty hertz wide. That's not a porch light; that's the same hundred watts in a flashlight beam."

He let the note hang. "Twenty-four hundred over a hundred and fifty is sixteen — and three decibels is a doubling, so that's a dozen decibels of free muscle, same radio, same wire. And your ear can find a tone in noise long after speech turns to soup. CW doesn't get through because it's old. It gets through because it's *narrow*."

Bea looked at the wire through the open door, sixty-five and a half feet of moonlit glint, and then at the J-38 where it sat on the desk beside Si's own key. Her heart had started doing something specific.

"Break-in's off," Si said quietly, and slid the J-38 in front of himself. "The key only talks to the room right now. Not the sky." He put his hand on the navy knob, and sent, slow as honey:

Dah-dit-dit-dit. Dit. Dit-dah.

Bea wrote it down before she knew she was writing: B-E-A. Her own name, in her own handwriting, out of the hand of an old man in a barn, in the oldest code on the air.

"Again," Si said, and sent it again, and then: dit-dit-dah-dit, dah-dit-dit-dit — F-B, and then dah-dah-dah dah-dah — O-M. "Fine business, old man. That's the highest compliment a straight key can give. Everybody on a key is OM, even you. *Especially* you, after tonight." He slid the key across to her. "Now. The license on the wall says this station is me, and every dit that leaves this wire is W7JUNK, and I'm the control operator, which means I sit right here and I'm responsible for all of it. The rules say a guest may operate under a control operator's supervision, and the rules, for once, are entirely right. You send. I log. If I touch your elbow, you stop mid-letter, whatever it is, and I take the key. Deal?"

"Deal," Bea said.

"Eight words a minute," Si said. "Walking speed. Slower than you think, and he'll love you for it." He spun the dial to seven oh five five and parked it. "The straight-key crowd keeps a porch light burning around here. They've even got a club — a century club, all fists, no keyboards. Somebody'll be home."

He took his own key, and Bea watched him call, patient and unhurried, with gaps between the calls long enough to hear the band breathe:

*CQ CQ CQ DE W7JUNK W7JUNK W7JUNK K*

Nothing. He sent it again, same speed, same gaps, a man who had been fishing all his life. Bea found she was holding her breath. Outside, the moon came up higher, Resistor sighed in his sleep, and then —

Out of the speaker, thin but clean, like a voice from a far porch: *W7JUNK DE N7QRS N7QRS KN.*

"Slow and polite," Si said softly. "He's yours."

Bea's hand went to the J-38 and hovered, and for one long moment the whole alphabet left her. Then she felt the shape of the first letter in her fingers the way you feel a step in a dark stairwell, and she pressed the knob, and the sidetone said her thoughts out loud:

*N7QRS DE W7JUNK — GE OM TNX FER CALL — UR RST 559 — NAME HR IS BEA BEA — GUEST OP — MY FIRST QSO — HW? KN*

Eight words a minute. Slower. In the middle of her own name she sent a B that started to be something else — one dah too many, a stutter in the dark — and her whole body went cold, and Si did not touch her elbow, so she simply stopped, took one breath, and sent the name again, clean: B-E-A. The band waited. Nobody hurried her. The moon did not hurry her.

The answer came back slow — slower than the man had called, because he had heard her speed and matched it without being asked, which Bea would understand later was the whole soul of the thing:

*BEA DE N7QRS — FB OM ES TNX — UR RST 579 — NAME HANK HANK — QTH BOISE ID — HW? K*

Bea laughed out loud.

It burst out of her before she could stop it — one bright bark of a laugh, loud enough that Resistor's head came up off his paws — because there it was, copied in her own hand: *FB OM*. Fine business, old man. Her name had gone out of the barn on a wire she had strung, through a donut she had wound, and come back from *Idaho* wearing a compliment meant for old men.

"Steady," Si said, but he was smiling into the dark end of the barn. "Answer the man."

She answered the man. *HANK DE W7JUNK — TNX FB OM — UR 559 HR — WX CLR ES MOON FULL — 73 ES GN — SK* — and Si leaned in and added the station's legal amen with his own key, *DE W7JUNK SK*, every dit accounted for. Hank's last transmission came back gentle as a porch light going off:

*GE BEA — TNX FER QSO — UR DOING FB — HPE CUAGN — 73 — N7QRS SK*

And that was the whole of it: two minutes of slow code, a name, a report, a state she had never seen. Si wrote it in his logbook — date, time, frequency, N7QRS, Hank, Boise, 559 sent, 579 received — and then slid the log to her, and Bea signed the remarks line in her small exact hand: *Guest op Bea, first QSO, straight key, full moon.* It looked, she thought, like the first line of a much longer book.

"N7QRS," Si said, leaning back, tasting it. "Q-R-S. Do you know what QRS means in the code?"

Bea did. It had been on her chart, in the Q-signals column, the very first one she'd loved. "Send slower."

"Send slower," Si said. "The man's *call sign* is the code for slow down. He picked it on purpose, same as I picked mine. There's a whole nation of us, Kowalczyk — people who chose slowness on purpose, in a world that pays for fast — and they all know each other, and now one of them knows you." He corked the pen. "He'll be on that porch most nights. They keep the light on."

---

The truck rattled down Larkspur Road at half past nine with the moon pacing it off the driver's side, huge and yellow and full, and for a long while neither of them said anything, because the night had already said it.

"Most people go their whole lives," Si said finally, eyes on the road, "and never once hear their name come back to them out of the sky. They don't know it's a thing a person can *do*." He downshifted for the turn into town. "You know now. That's not a thing you un-know."

Bea watched the moon in the side mirror. "No," she said. It came out steady.

At the Pinewall, Babcia was a shape behind the sheer curtain of apartment 3-B before the truck had fully stopped, and she had the door open on the second stair, arms crossed, the hallway light behind her.

"Nine twenty-seven," Babcia said.

"Ma'am," said Si, touching the brim of a cap he wasn't wearing, and drove off into the dark, leaving the smell of coffee and hay behind him.

Babcia looked at her granddaughter for a long moment — at the canvas bag with the J-38's cord hanging out of it, at the log sheet Bea had copied down and folded into her pocket like a letter. "So," Babcia said. "Did you talk to the sky?"

"Idaho," Bea said. "A man named Hank. I sent my name and he sent it back."

Babcia uncrossed her arms. Something moved in her face that Bea had seen only once before, at the meeting with the pierogi. "Your dziadek," she said, "sent his name to ships in storms, and the ships always sent it back. He said that was the whole job — say *I am here*, and wait until someone says *we hear you*." She opened the door wider. "Eat. Then sleep. Tomorrow you can be a radio person on a full stomach."

Upstairs, Bea set the J-38 on her desk beside the little 555 oscillator, in the pool of moonlight coming through the blinds, and connected nothing, and sent nothing. She just put her finger on the navy knob once — *tick* — the way you touch a photograph.

She dreamed in dit-dah.

All night, under the ordinary dreams — the hallway that was also the barn, the test she hadn't studied for that turned out to be in code, which she passed — the letters ran like water under a bridge, slow and even, spelling nothing, meaning everything. She woke at six with the gray light coming up, and her fingers had been sending in her sleep. Dah-dit-dah-dah. C. She lay there and watched the ceiling and did it on purpose, once: her name. B-E-A. *FB OM*, she thought, and grinned at nobody, at the ceiling, at the whole dumb beautiful sky.

---

School became a countdown to night.

It was not that Monday was bad. Monday was fine. But Monday was also a sequence of rooms she sat in while the day burned down toward dark like a fuse — first period, where the clock's second hand was a metronome set too slow; lunch, where she caught herself tapping *dit-dit-dit-dah* on the table and Priya, across from her, said "V, again, you're obsessed" without looking up; physics, where Mr. O wrote WAVE PROPERTIES on the board and Bea wrote *seven oh five five* in the corner of her notes and had to stop her hand from doing it again. The day was not a day anymore. It was the space between letters. Night was the word.

The group chat lit up at 3:47, which meant Theo had lasted almost an entire school day without hijacking it, a personal record.

*THEO: people. PEOPLE. i have downloaded an app. there is a SPACE STATION and it goes over our TOWN and you can hear it with the antenna we BUILT*

*PRIYA: the pass predictions look right. there's a doppler shift, about three and a half kilohertz either way, across the pass. i have questions. this is not over.*

*JUN: the yagi is still in the storeroom. the TAPE MEASURE yagi. we could hear SPACE with a TAPE MEASURE*

Bea read it twice, smiling, and put the phone face down on her desk — her desk, with the key, and the oscillator, and the moon already up somewhere behind the daylight, patient as Idaho. Under the desk, out of sight, her fingers answered the chat the way the sky had taught her: dah-dah-dah, dah-dit-dah. O. K.

Then she went back to watching the clock — not for the end of school, but for the end of *day*, for the hour when the D layer let go and forty meters filled up with crickets and a wire in a meadow woke up singing. Everything else was just the space between dits.
