## Chapter 8: The RFI Detective

The contact was going beautifully right up to the moment the door hit the wall.

"CQ, CQ, CQ twenty meters, this is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, calling CQ and standing by." Theo let go of the push-to-talk and Room 114-B filled with the surf of the open band — static, a far-off Morse signal threading through it like somebody sewing. It was the second Thursday of April, four-twenty in the afternoon, and the storeroom smelled of warm dust because the Kenwood had been running since three-thirty and its tubes were in a good mood.

They had a routine now, Tuesdays and Thursdays, since Mr. O had decided the club was ready for regular after-school operating. Mr. O sat at the second desk with the logbook, being the control operator — which meant, as he had explained roughly forty times, that every transmission the club made was legally his: his call sign, his license, his body in the room and awake. Theo did most of the talking, because he had a baritone built for a movie trailer. Priya kept the log in her own handwriting, because she trusted nobody else's. Bea sat by the windowsill with her homework open, listening the way other people read. And Jun watched the SWR meter, mostly out of loyalty — it sat at one-point-six to one at 14.100, same as it had all spring, and she liked watching it be right.

A voice rose out of the static, thin and friendly. "November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, this is November Seven Mike Tango Romeo, N7MTR, name here is Ellen, Bozeman, Montana. You're giving me five-five, five-five into Montana. Go ahead."

"She's on a vertical," Jun said. Priya had trained her to guess antennas from signal reports, like a party trick nobody had asked for.

Theo pressed the switch. "N7MTR, this is N8OWL — five-five, good to meet you, Ellen. The operator of record is Mr. Okafor, we're a high school club in Maple Run, and we're running a Kenwood TS-520S into a dipole made from a hundred-foot extension cord, which I mention because it's true and because it never stops being funny—"

That was when the door banged open hard enough to make the Tesla coil ring like a small, insulted bell.

The man in the doorway was tall and narrow, sixty-something, with a gray mustache, a denim shirt, and reading glasses on a cord around his neck. He had a sheet of notebook paper clenched in one fist, and he surveyed the five of them the way you survey a fresh dent in your car door.

"Which one of you," he said, "is playing the radio?"

Mr. O stood up. "That would be me, in the legal sense. David Okafor. I'm the club's sponsor, and the control operator of that transmission — which we are now interrupting." He nodded at Theo, who, to his credit, was already reaching for the switch.

"Ellen, N7MTR, this is N8OWL, I have to cut this short, we've got a situation in the shack. Thanks for the five-five, seven-three, N8OWL clear." The room went very quiet except for the band, still murmuring, indifferent.

The man looked at the Kenwood, at the coax disappearing into the windowsill, at the corkboard of QSL cards. He unfolded the paper in his fist. It was covered in columns.

"My name is Pruitt. I live in the gray house on the far side of the practice field, the one that backs up to the fence. For three weeks—" he held up the paper "—my computer speakers have been buzzing. Loud. Like a hornets' nest with a grudge. And because I am a careful man, I wrote down when." He read from the columns. "Tuesday the twenty-fourth of March, three-forty to five. Thursday the twenty-sixth, three-thirty to four-fifty. Tuesday the thirty-first—"

"Every Tuesday and Thursday," Priya said, notebook already out. "Since the last week of March."

"Every Tuesday and Thursday," Pruitt agreed, "since the last week of March. And only then. My speakers don't buzz at breakfast. They don't buzz at midnight. They buzz after school." He looked around the room again, and his mustache did something that was not a smile. "So I asked the custodian who comes in here after school, and he said — and I want you all to appreciate that I am quoting — 'the radio kids.'"

"Mr. Abernathy has a gift for description," said Mr. O. "Mr. Pruitt, you were right to bring this to us, and I'm glad you brought data. What you're describing has a name — radio-frequency interference, RFI — and I'll promise you two things. One: we will take it seriously. Two: we will fix it."

"Fix it by when?"

"By the time we understand it," said Mr. O, "which is the only honest schedule there is. Leave us your phone number. Give us a week."

Pruitt looked at him for a long moment, the way you look at a man who has used the word *honest* and forced you to believe him. He wrote his number in the corner of his log sheet, tore it off, and laid it on the desk. At the door he stopped and looked back at the Kenwood — the green dial, the needle, the warm attic smell of it.

"I used to work in radio," he said. "Real radio. Eight years behind a microphone. It never buzzed." He said it to the radio more than to them, the way you address an exasperating relative. Then he left, and his footsteps faded down the hall at a pace Jun would later describe in the group chat as *storming, but sad.*

Nobody said anything for a moment. Ellen in Bozeman was gone. The band murmured on.

"Well," said Theo, "the podcast episode writes itself. I'm calling it *The RFI Detective*."

"It does not write itself," said Mr. O, "until you understand what happened." He slid Pruitt's torn-off number into the logbook like a bookmark. "Here is the rule of this room. It is not a school rule; it is a radio rule: *our signal, our responsibility.* It does not matter whose equipment is misbehaving. If our transmission ends up in a neighbor's speakers, then we are the ones with the transmitter, and we are the ones who fix it. That is what hams do. It's printed in every licensing manual ever written, and more importantly, it's right."

Jun looked at the log sheet Pruitt had left — neat columns of dates and times, ruled with a straightedge. A man who logged things. In the back of her salvage log she wrote: *Pruitt. Gray house, across the practice field. Buzz, Tue/Thu only. Keeps a log. A man after Priya's heart.*

"Our neighbor has three weeks of data," Mr. O said, "and we have a transmitter and a storeroom full of test equipment." He uncapped the whiteboard marker. "Detectives. What do we know?"

Priya took the marker from him, which was her way of agreeing to be in charge of the board. "One: the buzz matches our HF schedule exactly — and he noted it himself; his log skips spring break, when we weren't here. Two—" she wrote *2 m, 5 W* and circled it twice "—he has never logged a buzz when we run the two-meter rig, five watts into the roof J-pole. Different frequency, different power, different antenna. Whatever this is, it's the *big* radio and the *big* antenna."

Then she drew the map: the school as a rectangle, the flagpole, the inverted-V drooping from the cleat, the coax running down the pole and across the grass to their windowsill, and — she paced it off in her head and wrote *~100 yd?* — the fence, and a small square labeled *PRUITT*. She drew arrows from the antenna to the square, stopped, and very deliberately drew a second set: from the *coax line itself*, all along its length.

"Why two?" Bea said. It was the first word she'd spoken since the door hit the wall.

"Because I don't believe in one-arrow answers," Priya said. "A hundred watts, a hundred yards away, should *annoy* a cheap amplifier. What he described is loud. Hornets'-nest loud. Either his equipment is spectacularly bad, or our signal is getting a ride we're not accounting for. Possibly both."

"Experiments," said Mr. O, leaning back with his arms folded, the way he did when a physics problem finally got interesting. "Name them."

"Dummy load," Priya said. "Transmit full power into a dummy load. If his speakers still buzz, the RF is leaving the rig itself — power lines, chassis, something direct. If they go quiet, the signal leaves through the antenna system, and the problem is in the air."

"We don't have a dummy load," Theo said.

"We know a man who has everything," said Jun, already reaching for her phone, because Si Webb picked up on the second ring, the way he always did, as if the telephone were an interruption in a life otherwise full of interesting wiring.

She explained it in four sentences. There was a silence on the line that she had learned to recognize as Si being pleased and deciding how to hide it.

"RFI," he said at last, savoring it like a curse word. "Tell Okafor I'll be there Saturday with the Cantenna. And tell him the coffee in that building is a crime."

"He knows," said Jun.

"And Jun." Si's voice did the thing where it went almost gentle. "Tell the kids this, from an old man who has chased RF into a Baptist church organ, a garage door opener, and one electric fence: *it's never the thing you think it is.* It's always the thing next to the thing you think it is."

---

Saturday was mild and bright, the practice field still half mud, and Si's truck sat at the curb with a gallon paint can in the bed and a coffee can on the seat. The paint can had a coax connector in its lid and *50 OHM — DO NOT OPEN* on the side in marker so old it had gone brown.

"Heathkit Cantenna," Si said, handing it to Theo like a chalice. "Built it when I was seventeen. There's a big resistor in there hanging in oil. Transmit into it, your signal becomes slightly warm oil instead of radio waves. It's an antenna that can keep a secret." The coffee can was full of snap-on ferrite cores, gray-black cylinders in plastic clamshells, clicking like mah-jongg tiles. "Flea markets," Si said. "Ferrite follows me home. Take what you need; the barn grows more."

The plan was simple, and Si approved of it because it had phone calls in it. Jun and Bea would walk to Pruitt's with an open line; Theo would stay at the Kenwood, Mr. O as control operator, Priya logging. Transmit into the antenna, then into the Cantenna, and let the speakers tell the truth.

Pruitt answered the door in the same denim shirt, and Jun decided it was not the same actual shirt but one of a set, like Mr. O's ties. His house smelled like vinyl and wood polish and, very faintly, like the same warm dust as the Kenwood, which made Jun like him immediately against her will.

The den was at the back, overlooking the fence and the field and — Jun noted, the way you note a suspect in a lineup — a clear line of sight to the flagpole. One wall was floor-to-ceiling records, alphabetized. On the sideboard sat a turntable with a hinged dust cover, and flanking the window, on little stands at ear height, sat the criminals: a pair of powered computer speakers, beige gone yellow, each trailing a good fifteen feet of thin zip cord that ran down the wall and along the baseboard in great lazy loops.

Above the turntable hung a framed photograph: a much younger Pruitt, sideburns down to here, headphones the size of bagels, leaning into a studio microphone. He was grinning like the microphone owed him money.

"Eight years," Pruitt said, catching her looking. "Evening slot, six to midnight, jazz on weeknights. A station two hours from here — close enough to matter, far enough that nobody I knew could hear the mistakes." He reassembled his scowl. "The speakers are good speakers."

"They are not," said Bea quietly, looking at the zip cord, "good *wires*."

Pruitt opened his mouth, closed it, and looked at her with the expression of a man re-racking an entire argument. "No," he admitted. "The speakers came with the computer. The cord came with the speakers."

Jun raised the phone. "Theo. Full power into the antenna, talk steady."

Right on cue, both speakers began to *bray* — a harsh, garbled blare that rose and fell, that had cadence, that was unmistakably Theo's voice with all of the Theo removed, like a man talking through a kazoo.

Pruitt pointed at the speakers with both hands, vindicated and miserable. "*That.* Three weeks of *that.*"

Bea had tilted her head, listening with her whole attention, eyes gone somewhere else. "It's not a buzz," she said. "It's talking. Garbled talking. It has the *shape* of talking."

They ran the experiments in order, because Priya had written them on a card and Bea read them out like a judge reading charges. One: Pruitt turned the volume knob to zero. The bray continued, unchanged, from speakers whose volume was now nothing. Two: Jun unplugged the input cable from the powered speaker entirely. The bray continued. Three: Bea unscrewed one speaker lead at the spring clip. That speaker fell silent; the other kept braying at the empty room.

"The leads," Jun said into the phone. "Priya, did you get that? Unplugged from everything, volume at zero — it's the speaker wires themselves."

"Switch to the Cantenna," Priya's voice said, tinny in the phone, after a pause full of relayed instructions. "Theo. Full power into the dummy load. Same steady talking."

Theo talked. The speakers stood there with their fifteen-foot antennae spread across the baseboard and produced nothing — nothing at all, just room tone and the track team faint across the field. Into the silence, from the phone, Jun could hear the storeroom laughing: Theo was telling the dummy load about his weekend.

"Twice more," Priya said. "Antenna — dummy load — antenna. Same result twice and we log it."

They did. Antenna: bray. Dummy load: silence. Antenna: bray. Bea checked off the boxes on Priya's card, and Pruitt sat down slowly in the armchair, looking at his speakers the way you look at a dog that has learned a trick you hate.

"So," he said. "It's not your radio leaking into my walls. It's my wires listening to your antenna."

"That's half of it," Priya said, back in the storeroom with the door shut and the whiteboard squeaking. She drew a speaker, a long wavy line of zip cord, and a stick-figure transistor. "Fifteen feet of wire at fourteen megahertz isn't a cable, it's a *receiving antenna* — a decent fraction of a wavelength, lying on the baseboard, broadside to our signal. And every amplifier chip on Earth is a part-time diode. It *detects* the RF, turns it back into audio, and amplifies the audio, because that is its whole job. He isn't hearing our signal. He's hearing our signal *demodulated by his own equipment*. We're not loud. We're being decoded."

"His amplifier is a radio receiver," Theo said slowly, "that thinks it's a speaker."

"Everything is a receiver," Priya said, "if you feed it RF and it contains a junction. The church organ, the garage door opener — it's never the thing you think it is."

"And the volume knob did nothing," Bea said, "because the RF comes in *after* it."

"Okay," said Jun. "So we choke his leads. Ferrites on the speaker wires, problem solved, Pruitt forgives us, roll credits."

"Maybe," said Priya. She went back to the map — flagpole, coax, windowsill, the little square labeled *PRUITT* — and tapped the second set of arrows, the ones along the coax itself. "But I keep thinking about *hornets'-nest loud* at a hundred yards. A clean dipole at the flagpole, fed properly, puts its energy toward the sky, not down the feedline. So before we march across a muddy field with a coffee can of ferrites, I want to know why our feedline doesn't worry me, when everything about it should worry me."

Si had come in behind them, quiet as weather — he liked to hear the end of a person's thinking before he answered it. He looked at the whiteboard map for a long moment.

"Smart girl," he said. "Monday after school, bring the analyzer and a steady finger. There's a test my old boss taught me, back when the world was young. It's called *touch the coax and see what flinches.*"

---

Monday they ran Si's test, and it took Bea about four seconds to find the snake.

The setup was nothing: the Kenwood on, Theo talking steady with Mr. O logging, full power into the dipole. The coax drooped from the windowsill feedthrough to the desk in a long black curve, same as always. Si stood with his arms folded and did not touch anything, because Si never touched anything.

"Transmitting," said Mr. O. "Bea. When you're ready."

Bea put one finger on the coax, light as a piano teacher.

The SWR meter's needle kicked. She lifted her finger; it settled. She touched it again. Kick. On receive, between transmissions, the noise floor changed texture when she gripped the braid, like a room going from empty to occupied. She held her finger up and looked at Si, because there was nothing to say that the needle hadn't said.

"There's your snake," said Si. "RF riding on the *outside* of your feedline."

Priya was already at the whiteboard, drawing three nested lines. "Inside the coax, the wanted current flows on the center conductor and the *inner* face of the braid — opposite directions, balanced, contained. At fourteen megahertz the RF only uses the outer few thousandths of an inch of copper, so the braid has, in effect, two skins. The inner skin belongs to the signal. The outer skin—" she drew arrows on the outside "—is up for grabs. The dipole's near field bathes the whole cable and pushes current onto that outer skin, and that current is *common-mode*: same direction on everything, nothing to cancel it. It runs down the flagpole, across the grass, radiating the whole way. Your feedline becomes a second antenna you never meant to build."

"A hundred-foot antenna in reverse," Theo said. "We've been beaming *Pruitt* with our own coax."

"Partly," said Si. "Which is why I want to see the choke." He looked at Jun. "You built one in November. Eight turns, six inches, right at the feedpoint, like I showed you. That coil is the wall that's supposed to keep the outside of the braid honest. Either it failed, or it was never as good as we thought — and I don't bet against your work. So let's go look."

Mr. Abernathy met them at the flagpole with his keys and his opinions — a broad man who treated the halyard the way sailors treat lines: one hand for the rope, one eye on everything else. "Feet clear of the coil," he said, "nobody under the line, and when I say hold, you hold." He lowered the inverted-V hand over hand while Jun and Bea took the ends, and the apex came down with its little tangle of hardware: the cutting-board insulator, the coax connection wrapped in self-amalgamating tape, and below it, the choke.

Jun's stomach did a small, specific flip. She had built that choke in November, on the storeroom floor, with Si watching and Priya measuring. Eight neat turns of RG-58, six inches across, bound with electrical tape and pride. What hung from the feedpoint now was a black pretzel. The tape had gone gray and peeled back in curls; three turns had slipped loose and splayed; the whole thing dangled like a dropped Slinky.

"Winter," said Mr. Abernathy, not unkindly. "Winter eats everything on this pole."

"I taped that," Jun said. "I taped it *well*. I remember taping it."

"You did," said Si. He turned the sad coil over once and set it gently in the grass, like a bird that hadn't made it. "Tape is a letter of intent. Zip ties are a contract." He let that sit, because he was Si and aphorisms were his entire labor-management policy. "Nothing here is yours to own alone. We taught it the way we learned it, and the sky graded the homework. Build, weather, rebuild — and every rebuild is better, because you *saw* the failure instead of guessing it."

Priya had her notebook out, sketching. "So the common-mode current had an open road all winter — down the coax, across the grass, straight at the fence where a man's stereo lives. And the dipole still *worked* — Denver, Austin, half the country — because common-mode current doesn't break your match. It just borrows your feedline for its own purposes."

"It doesn't hurt *you*," Si agreed. "It hurts your *neighbors*. That's what makes it sneaky. A bad antenna announces itself. A dirty feedline shakes your hand and bills the man across the field."

"Then the fix list," said Mr. O, and counted on his fingers. "One: rebuild the feedpoint choke, properly, with zip ties. Two: a second choke where the coax enters the shack. Three: ferrites on Mr. Pruitt's speaker leads — that's his half of the treaty. Four—" he looked at Si "—you've been quiet about the station itself, and I know that means you have opinions."

"The station's been riding a hundred watts with no ground and no bonds," Si said. "Rig, tuner, power supply — three boxes sharing a power strip and hoping. When common-mode RF comes into a shack like that, every chassis gets to vote on what zero volts means, and the disagreement flows down your coax shield looking for Mr. Pruitt's stereo. So, four: a ground bus behind the desk, every box bonded to it, one wide strap to a rod in the dirt. Grounding and bonding. Tuesday, build day." He looked around at them — the dead pretzel of a choke in the grass, the dipole ends drooping patient in their hands. "You kids played radio all winter with your fly open," he said. "Time to dress the station."

---

They rebuilt the choke on Tuesday afternoon, on the grass by the flagpole, and this time they built it like a contract.

The form was Si's coffee can, emptied of ferrites into a milk crate for the occasion — Priya measured it twice and announced six and a quarter inches, "close enough to six, and honest, which matters more." Jun wound while Bea held tension: eight turns of the coax itself, snug against the can, each turn laid against the last like rope on a cleat. Priya followed with zip ties, four of them, cinched at the quarters and trimmed flush. When Jun slid the coil off the can it held its shape like it had grown that way — a fat black donut of RG-58 with the connector ends coming off it clean.

"That," said Si, looking at it the way other men look at engines, "is a 1:1 choke balun. One-to-one because it changes nothing — balanced line in, balanced line out. Its only job is to be a wall. To current on the *outside* of the braid, eight turns in open air is a big inductor at fourteen megahertz, a mountain range of reactance; the common-mode current hits it and dies of exhaustion. The signal on the *inside* never knows the wall is there, because its two currents cancel in the coil. That's the whole trick of this hobby, half the time: build a wall that only stops the thing you want stopped."

They wound a second, identical choke for the shack end. Mr. Abernathy re-hoisted the antenna while they stood clear, and when it was up Priya swept the band with the analyzer: one-point-six to one at 14.100, where the dipole had always lived. Bea put her finger on the coax during the next test transmission and the needle sat still as glass, and she looked at Si and allowed herself the smallest possible nod.

Then they built Pruitt's half of the treaty, out of the coffee can. Si snapped a core open to show them: two dull gray half-cylinders of ferrite nested in a plastic clamshell, like a locket made by a ceramics class. "Ferrite's powdered iron fired like pottery," he said. "Clamp it over a cable and to the current going the *same* way on both wires — common-mode, your speaker-lead RF — it's a wall again, except this one isn't a mountain range, it's a sponge. The RF pushes its magnetic field into the ferrite and the ferrite eats the push, turns it into a whisper of heat. Gone. But your music, flowing opposite directions on the two wires, cancels its own field and walks through untouched. Every pass through the hole counts as a turn." He snapped the clamshell shut around a scrap of zip cord — once, twice, three turns through the core. "Three turns per lead, snug against the back of the speaker. Do the power brick's cord too, and the input cable. RF isn't picky about which wire it rides."

The bonding took the rest of the afternoon and most of Jun's patience, which was the good kind of spending.

The bus bar came out of Si's truck — a foot of heavy copper drilled with holes, salvaged, he said, from a transmitter site that went dark in 1987. They clamped it to the back rail of the desk and made straps: Jun skinned the braid off scrap coax, flattened it with the back of a screwdriver, and soldered on ring lugs. One strap from the Kenwood's chassis screw to the bus. One from the tuner. One from the power supply. Short, wide, direct.

"Why straps?" Theo asked, holding one up. It looked like a tiny metal necktie. "Why not wire? We have a mile of wire."

"Because RF doesn't travel in the middle of a wire, it travels on the *surface*," Priya said, "and a flat strap has acres of surface for its weight. Every bond is a promise that two boxes will agree about what zero volts means."

"Grounding and bonding are not the same animal," Si said, and tapped the bus bar. "The rod we're about to put in the dirt — that's *ground*. It talks to the earth, keeps the AC mains honest, gives lightning an address that isn't your radio. *Bonding* is this bar: every chassis in the shack holding hands, so no two boxes can disagree, and no disagreement can go wandering down your coax looking for Mr. Pruitt's stereo. Ground is dirt. Bonding is family."

"Why not just run a wire from the rig to the radiator pipe across the room?" Theo said. "Twenty feet of wire to a perfectly good pipe."

"Sixteen point six feet of wire at fourteen megahertz is a quarter-wave radial," Priya said, without looking up, because that number lived in her bones now. "A long ground wire on twenty meters isn't a ground. It's an *antenna*, bolted to your radio. The strap has to stay well under that. Wide, short, and boring."

The rod went in outside the window, in the damp April soil by the foundation — five feet of copper-clad steel that Mr. Abernathy drove with a sledgehammer, waving them off with "I have tools you children don't have the upper-body strength for." Jun fed the wide copper strap — flashing from the thrift shop's scrap bin, steel-wooled until it shone — from the bus bar, through the feedthrough, down the wall to the clamp. Eight feet of strap, total. Wide, short, boring.

"And this changes nothing about storms," Mr. O said, while they stood admiring it, because he was a teacher and knew exactly when a lesson was feeling too pleased with itself. "At the first thunder, the coax comes out of the feedthrough and the radios stay dark, same as always. A ground rod is manners and safety, not permission."

---

Wednesday after school, they carried the coffee can across the field to make the peace.

It went the way Si had taught them. Three turns of each speaker lead through a core, snapped snug against the backs of the speakers. A core on the power brick's cord. A core on the input cable. Then Priya and Bea took up the lazy loops of zip cord and dressed them short along the baseboard with cable clips from the thrift shop, while Pruitt stood in his own den with his arms folded, supervising like a man at his own surprise party.

"Moment of truth," Jun said into the phone. "Theo. Full power. Talk steady."

The Kenwood brayed a hundred watts into the dipole a hundred yards away, and in the den there was — almost — nothing. The hornets' nest was gone. In its place, faint as a radio in a neighbor's yard, a garbled whisper rose and fell with Theo's voice.

Pruitt uncrossed his arms, slowly. "That's a tenth of it. Maybe less."

"It's still there," said Bea.

Nobody argued with her, because she was right — and because her verdict had gone down the open line, and in the storeroom a celebration died in its crib.

"*Beaten,*" said Theo's voice, tinny with despair, "by a stereo from a cereal box."

"RFI is an onion, kid," Si said, somewhere near the phone. "You peel a layer, you cry a little, there's another layer."

"What did we change," said Mr. O, calm as a metronome, "and what didn't we change?"

So they changed things, one at a time, Theo transmitting on cue. The leads were choked and stayed choked — Jun unplugged the input cable at the speaker and the whisper died outright, which meant the speaker chain was clean. It was coming in the *front* door. She worked back along the input cable to its other end, and found what the Saturday tests had hidden under the louder problem: between the turntable and the speakers sat a plastic box the size of a deck of cards, PHONO PRE-AMP on a peeling sticker, a wall-wart cord, two thin RCA leads drooping off the turntable like an afterthought.

Jun unplugged the RCA leads at the box. Whisper: gone. Plugged them back. Whisper: back. She pulled the wall-wart and the den went dead silent, transmitter or no, and she stood up with the little wart in her hand feeling like a detective in the last ten minutes of the episode.

"It's the preamp," she said. "It's been the preamp all along — under the buzz. We fixed the loud path and found the quiet one."

Priya was already nodding, the nod of a person whose map just gained a road. "Of course there's a preamp. A turntable whispers — the cartridge puts out *thousandths* of a volt, so that box is a megaphone, and megaphones don't check ID. RF walks in on the RCA leads and gets rectified like everywhere else — and after that it's not RF anymore, it's *audio*, riding the intended wires to the intended amplifier. No choke downstream can touch it, because chokes stop RF, not music. You have to stop it at the threshold."

"Then we stop it at the threshold," said Jun.

They did. The twin RCA leads went together through one snap-on core — both cables, same core, three turns, snug at the preamp end. The wall-wart's cord got a core of its own. And Priya, rummaging in Pruitt's kitchen junk drawer with his blessing — every house has one, and his held a spool of bell wire — ran a short green wire from the turntable's ground lug to the little screw on the preamp's back panel.

"Bonding," she said, cinching it down.

"You people have a word for everything," said Pruitt.

They dressed the cables short, set the preamp on the sideboard, and ran the test again. Theo transmitted, full power, and the den held its silence. Then Pruitt, with the air of a man who does not trust good news until it has survived Miles Davis, put on a record and dropped the needle — mid-transmission — and the room filled with clean, unbothered jazz. "Keep going," Jun told the phone.

Pruitt reached over and turned the volume *up*.

Nothing but the trumpet.

He laughed — a single bark, rusty from disuse, the first they'd heard out of him. "Son of a gun," he said, to the speakers, to the record, to the flagpole across the field. "You fixed my stereo *and* your radio."

---

The following Tuesday, Pruitt came to them.

He arrived at three-thirty sharp, denim shirt, a milk crate of records under one arm and the wary politeness of a man revisiting the scene of his own complaint. "I want to see the machine," he said.

The tour took four minutes, because the shack was small and the machine was honest. He looked at the Kenwood the way Mr. Hackett had — that look old radio men gave it, like a dog they used to own. He inspected the fat new choke at the windowsill and the bus bar with its neat braid straps. "You did all this in a week." It was not quite a question.

"Radio rule," said Mr. O. "Our signal, our responsibility."

"Huh," said Pruitt, and set the milk crate on the desk. "For the room. Jazz, mostly. Not for sale, and not a gift — indefinite loan, which is a thing I just invented. You'll want a record player." He paused. "I know a man with a spare."

At four o'clock Mr. O settled into the control operator's chair, and Theo called CQ.

The answer came from Sacramento. "November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, this is Kilo Six Victor Mike Papa, K6VMP — name is Gus, Gus in Sacramento. You're five-nine, five-nine into California, and say — N8OWL. I worked this call twenty years ago. Is that Dave Okafor's station?"

It was. The contact became a reunion conducted at the speed of voice. Pruitt stood with his hands in his pockets and watched the ritual like a man at a church he'd lapsed out of — the log in Priya's furious handwriting, the signal reports traded like compliments. "ID," Mr. O murmured at the ten-minute mark, and Theo gave the call sign again without breaking stride, and Pruitt nodded once, slowly, at all of it.

"It's radio with the commercials taken out," he said, very quietly, to Jun. "You log it. You confirm it. Nobody ever confirmed *me*."

Mr. O heard him, because Mr. O heard everything. "K6VMP, this is N8OWL — Gus, before we sign, I have a guest in the shack who'd like to say hello. I'm the control operator; this is a third-party contact, and the guest is a gentleman your signal has been *visiting* for three weeks." He held out the microphone. "Mr. Pruitt. It's traditional. Every ham you'll ever meet started exactly this way."

Pruitt looked at the microphone the way you look at a bicycle after thirty years. Then he took it, pressed the switch, and eight years of rust fell off his voice in a single syllable.

"K6VMP, this is — my name is Walt Pruitt, I'm a neighbor of this club, I used to spin records for a living, and your friends here have spent a week teaching my stereo manners. I just wanted to say—" he stopped, and the room waited, and he found it "—this is the finest use of an afternoon I've seen in years. Back to you."

Gus's laugh came through six hundred miles of sky. "Walt, you're five-nine, and welcome to the real radio — the kind with no ratings. Tell them to keep that extension cord in the air; it's working." They signed with QSLs promised both ways, and seven-three, and the band filled the room again with its patient surf.

Pruitt handed the microphone back and sat down slowly. "Eight years behind a microphone," he said, to nobody in particular. "Every night, six to midnight, talking to the whole county. Nobody ever told me I was five-nine."

Si made hot-plate coffee, which tasted like it had been described to someone over a bad phone line; Pruitt drank a whole cup without comment, which Jun decided was its own kind of five-nine. They talked about Gus, and about whether a turntable could live in the storeroom — it could; Mr. O ruled that it qualified as audio test equipment, and Theo, who had been about to argue the same with more adjectives, closed his mouth and looked faintly cheated. Pruitt asked for the operating schedule, wrote *Tue/Thu, 3:30–5* on an index card, and said it was going on his refrigerator, "so I know when to put on a record."

The club's first fan, Jun wrote in the salvage log that night — because some entries are not salvage, but you log them anyway.

Pruitt was shrugging on his jacket when he stopped at the corkboard — really stopped, the way you stop when a face you know walks out of a crowd. His finger came up and tapped the eighties photograph: three kids with enormous collars, grinning on the school roof next to a tower, like they'd stolen something.

"Now *that*," he said, "is a tower." He looked back at them — at the chokes and the bus bar and the coax out the window, four kids and a teacher and a seventy-one-year-old with ferrite in his pockets, in a storeroom full of rescued radio. "You kids want problems? Try keeping a tower up through Field Day."

"What's Field Day?" Bea asked.

Mr. O and Si looked at each other, and whatever passed between them had been there a long time.

"Last full weekend of June," Mr. O said. "Every ham in North America drags their equipment into fields and parking lots and runs on emergency power for twenty-four hours straight, to prove they can. It's a contest, a campout, and an argument, all at once."

"It's where you find out what your antennas are made of," said Si. "Usually the answer is wind."

Pruitt left with his empty crate and his five-nine, and the room stayed quiet after him, the particular quiet of a room doing the same arithmetic. Priya picked up the marker and went to the whiteboard, to the four boxes Mr. O had drawn in September — *members*, checked; *build something*, checked and checked again; *licenses*, waiting on summer; *demonstration*, still empty. Under the empty one she wrote two words and a question mark, in her small furious handwriting:

*FIELD DAY?*

"We're doing it," Theo said, "aren't we."

Nobody in the room said no.

Outside, beyond the windowsill and the new ground strap and the flagpole rope ticking in the April wind, the extension-cord dipole hung quiet in the last of the light — thirty-three feet of lamp-cord wire, minding its manners at last, and listening.
