## Chapter 1: The Club with Three Members

The Westridge High activities fair was held in the gym on the second Friday of September, and it had the acoustics of a aircraft hangar full of kettledrums. Forty folding tables ringed the basketball court — robotics, debate, drama, three separate dance crews engaged in a cold war over the good corner — and the noise came off the polished floor in waves.

At the far end, past the ski club and the table selling spirit hoodies, stood the loneliest booth at Westridge High.

It had: one card table, one folding chair, one hand-lettered sign on a piece of poster board (AMATEUR RADIO CLUB, and under it, smaller, *Room 114-B — Wednesdays*), one signup sheet with zero names on it, and one teacher, grading quizzes as if the gym were not exploding around him. There was also a small black radio with a whip antenna, sitting on the table like a centerpiece nobody had asked about.

Jun knew the teacher. Everyone did. Mr. Okafor — Mr. O — taught physics, wore a different tie with a constellation on it every Friday, and was famous for demonstrating the Doppler effect by throwing a shrieking stuffed monkey across the classroom on a string. She'd had him freshman year. He had once returned a test of hers with *you think like an engineer, stop hiding it* written next to the B-plus.

She drifted over, mostly because it was the only quiet square yard in the building.

Mr. O looked up over his reading glasses. "Ms. Alvarez." He said it like he had been expecting her specifically, which was either teacher radar or a guess. "You've come to save the club."

"I've come to ask what the radio's for," Jun said.

"Same thing," said Mr. O, "by a longer road." He patted the little radio. "This is how you talk to the entire world with no internet, no cell towers, and no permission from any company. Just a license from the government, a wire, and physics." He let that sit, like a good demonstration. "The club teaches it. Or it did. We are currently experiencing a membership shortage."

Jun looked at the empty signup sheet. "How short?"

"Two," said Mr. O. "The minimum is four. And the two are seniors, who graduate in June, which is a rounding error away from zero." He clicked his pen. "The school board passed a rule. Any club below four members at the end of September, or unable to demonstrate one public event by June, is dissolved. Its room is reallocated." He said *reallocated* the way people say *put to sleep*. "Room 114-B is my storeroom, and it contains forty years of oscilloscopes, a Tesla coil, and the club's equipment. The district would like it for testing storage."

"That's all that's left?" Jun said. "A room?"

"A room," said Mr. O, "a storeroom full of treasure, one radio on this table, and a teacher with a tie for every constellation. Cygnus today." He straightened it. "And you, potentially. I hear things, Ms. Alvarez. Shop teachers talk. They say you fixed the theater department's sound board with a paper clip and spite."

"It was a solder bridge," Jun said. "The spite was free."

She put her name on the sheet. *June Alvarez, grade 11.* The pen was chained to the clipboard like it had tried to escape before. While she wrote, Mr. O reached down and switched the little radio on, and the noise of the gym was joined — underneath, like a tide under a dock — by a soft, steady hiss.

"That's static," Jun said.

"That's *the band*," said Mr. O. "Listening is chapter one."

Jun pulled out the second folding chair — there was only one — and sat on the table's edge instead. "How hard is the test?" she asked. "The license test."

"Thirty-five questions," said Mr. O. "Multiple choice. Ohm's law, some safety, some rules, a little math. People study for a few weeks and pass. There's no age limit — a six-year-old in Wyoming holds a license, which I mention whenever a seventeen-year-old tells me it's too hard." He flipped a page of quizzes. "The test is the easy part. The hobby is the hard part, and the hobby is the fun part. The test just keeps the riffraff off the airwaves."

"Are we the riffraff?"

"You are the *future*," said Mr. O, "which is worse. The riffraff just want to talk. The future wants to build things."

A pair of freshmen drifted up, read the poster board, and drifted away again, one of them mouthing *radio?* at the other with the universal face for *why*. The dance crews' speaker thumped from across the gym. Jun watched the freshmen get absorbed by the ski club crowd and felt something unexpected: not embarrassment, but a protective, contrary little flare, the same feeling she got when someone at the thrift shop looked at a perfectly good lamp and saw only that it was old.

"Does the radio work?" she asked. "Like, right now?"

"It receives," said Mr. O. "Beautifully. Transmitting requires a licensed control operator — which I am — and a reason." He glanced at the hissing little set, then at her, and something in his face said he had already decided what the first meeting would be. "Come Wednesday, Room 114-B. Bring your curiosity and one friend. The friend is mandatory. The curiosity, I suspect, takes care of itself."

"Listening is chapter one," Jun said, sliding off the table. "Wednesday," said Mr. O, and went back to his quizzes, with the band murmuring under the kettledrums like the tide under a dock.

---

Priya Nair was Jun's best friend, which both of them would describe differently — Priya would say "she is statistically the person I eat lunch with most" and Jun would say "Priya pretends to be a robot but I have seen her cry at a documentary about an octopus" — and both would be right. Jun found her at their usual cafeteria table at lunch, doing physics problems for fun in a graph-paper notebook, which Jun was fairly sure was not even assigned.

"Sign the sheet," Jun said, dropping the flyer between them.

Priya read it without picking it up. "No."

"You didn't even think about it."

"I thought about it in the time it took to read twelve words, because I am efficient." Priya turned the notebook a quarter turn and kept working. "Jun. It's *radio*. You know I love you, but it's a solved problem. If I want to talk to someone in Brazil, it's called the internet. It works *better*."

"It's not about talking to Brazil," Jun said. "It's about *how*."

"The how is undersea fiber-optic cables," Priya said. "They're extremely cool. There are sharks involved."

Jun sat down across from her and deployed her ammunition. "There's a test. A real government license test. Math. Circuit theory. Wave propagation." She watched Priya's pencil slow down the way you watch a cat notice a bird. "Antenna design is literally just applied trigonometry with consequences. And the physics room storeroom has forty years of oscilloscopes and a Tesla coil that we're going to lose to *testing storage* if the club dies."

"That's emotional manipulation," Priya said.

"It's emotional *adjacency*," Jun said. "Manipulation is next week."

"I will attend one meeting," Priya said, "to gather data. And I want it in writing that you admitted radio is obsolete."

"You want it in *writing*?"

"Radio people love QSL cards," said a voice from behind Jun, "that's their whole thing, little postcards. My grandpa had shoeboxes of them."

The voice was a baritone — a genuinely absurd, radio-station baritone, coming from a gangly senior with headphones around his neck and a lanyard that said MARSH HOUR in laminated letters. Theo Marsh. Jun knew him the way everyone knew him: the school podcast, the morning announcements when they let him, the talent show where he'd done a live radio drama with sound effects and somehow made the gym cry. He was pulling out the chair next to Jun like he had been invited, which he absolutely had not.

"Theo Marsh," he said, shaking Priya's hand and then Jun's. "I couldn't help overhearing, because I eavesdrop professionally. There's a *radio club*? With a Tesla coil? And actual transmitting equipment?"

"A storeroom," Jun said. "A dead club. And a card table."

"A card table with *range*," Theo said. He was already reading the flyer with the focused hunger of a man reading a menu. "Do you understand what I could do with real broadcast equipment? The *Marsh Hour* is currently recorded on a laptop in a closet. This is like finding out the school has a hidden recording studio that's just been sitting there since—"

"It doesn't have the internet," Priya said.

"Even better," said Theo, "no comment section." He looked around the cafeteria, stood up on his chair — actually stood up on it, in the crowded cafeteria, cupped his hands around his mouth, and announced to nobody in particular: "I'm joining the radio club!" and sat back down to scattered applause from the theater table, who appreciated the commitment.

"That's three," Jun said.

"It's two and a skeptic," said Priya. "Count properly."

It was Bea who made it four, though nobody knew it yet, because Bea signed the sheet on Wednesday afternoon with no audience at all.

---

Room 114-B turned out to be behind the physics lab, through a door marked STORAGE with a strip of ancient tape over it that somebody had long ago relabeled 114-B CLUB ROOM in ballpoint. Jun and Priya got there at 3:10, twenty minutes early, because Jun had not slept properly in five days and Priya said she was "only there to keep Jun from vibrating."

The door was unlocked. The lights were off. And the room was *full*.

It was not a big room — maybe the size of two parking spaces — but it was shelved floor to ceiling on three walls, and every shelf was loaded with the sediment of forty years of physics teaching: oscilloscopes with round green screens like submarine portholes, a Van de Graaff generator with its dented silver dome, ring stands and prisms and boxes of brass weights, a cardboard carton labeled DEAD MICE (which turned out, on inspection, to be computer mice), and in the corner, under a dust cover with the solemnity of a museum piece, a Tesla coil the height of a floor lamp.

"Okay," Priya admitted, stopping in front of it. "That's a little bit cool."

The fourth wall was the club's, and you could tell, because it was the only part of the room that had been *loved*. A long desk ran under the window — two operating positions, with coax feedthroughs in the windowsill and a power strip that had yellowed to the color of old piano keys. Above the desk, a corkboard the size of a door, and the corkboard was a palimpsest: layer upon layer of QSL cards, postcards from Iceland and Tahiti and ships at sea, corners of other cards sticking out from beneath them like geological strata. Tacked along the top edge, a row of fading photographs.

Jun stood on her toes to look. The oldest photo was black-and-white, 1960-something by the haircuts: a dozen boys in skinny ties around a desk of hammer-paint radios, one of them pointing proudly at a Heathkit. Then color ones — the seventies, enormous collars; the eighties, a tower on the school roof with three kids on it grinning like they'd stolen something; the nineties, fewer kids each time, the hairstyles modernizing, the club shrinking in frame after frame until the most recent photo, maybe six years old: three students and Mr. O, younger, his tie less confident.

"They used to have a tower," Jun said. "On the roof. Priya. They used to have a *tower on the roof*."

"Used to," Priya said, but she'd come to look too, and she was reading the QSL cards one by one, and Jun knew her well enough to see the math happening behind her eyes: all those distant postmarks, all that proof.

Below the corkboard, on the desk, sat the modern remnant: the little black portable from the club fair, a plastic box of connectors, and a logbook with WESTRIDGE HIGH ARC lettered on the cover, open to a page where the last entry was dated fourteen months ago.

Theo arrived at 3:20 with a folding tripod and a condenser microphone in a tote bag, took one slow turn in the middle of the room, and said, with the hush of a man entering a cathedral: "I can *work* with this." He ran his hand along the desk. "Two operating positions. Coax through the wall. Do you know what the reverb is like in the closet I record in? Do you know what I would *trade* for a room with no parallel—" He stopped. He had found the Tesla coil. "Is that—"

"Don't touch it," said Priya.

"I was going to *commune* with it," Theo said.

At exactly 3:30, while Theo was still communing and Priya was reading QSL cards and Jun was paging backward through fourteen silent months of logbook, the door opened, and Beatriz Kowalczyk came in.

---

Beatriz Kowalczyk was a sophomore, small enough that the hallway crowds moved around her like water around a stone, with a curtain of dark hair she used as a privacy screen and a denim jacket with a hand-embroidered octopus on the back that Jun had privately admired for a year. She had not come to the club fair. She had not been in the cafeteria. Nobody had recruited her at all, which — they would learn much later — was exactly how she preferred it.

Bea stopped in front of the signup sheet — Mr. O had brought it to the storeroom and put it on the desk with a pencil — read it, and picked up the pencil.

"Hi," Jun said. "Are you here for the club?"

Bea wrote *Beatriz Kowalczyk, grade 10* in small, precise letters, put the pencil down exactly parallel to the sheet's edge, and looked at Jun. She had dark eyes and an expression of total, unruffled calm, like a judge.

"Yes," Bea said.

"Cool," said Jun. "I'm Jun. That's Priya. Priya's observing."

"I'm a participant," Priya said. "I'm participating by observing."

Bea looked at her for a moment, then nodded once — not agreement, exactly; more like she had filed Priya under a category. Then she took a seat in the second row, folded her hands, and waited for the meeting to start, with the patience of someone who had decided this was where she was going to be and saw no reason to make a speech about it.

Mr. O came in carrying a cardboard box, took in the four names on the sheet, and did not smile, exactly, but he set the box down with unusual care, the way you put down something you are glad you didn't drop.

"Four," he said. "Four is a quorum. Four is a club." He wrote his own name on the whiteboard, and under it, *N8OWL*. "That's my call sign. November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima. It is, in every way that matters on the radio, my name. You will all have one someday, if you want one — after a license exam, which this club will teach you to pass, between now and June, alongside everything else. But that is chapter six. Chapter one is this."

From the box he took the little black radio from the club fair, a coil of coax cable, and a length of wire with an alligator clip on one end. He clipped the wire to the radio's antenna and held the other end up.

"Somebody open the window."

---

The storeroom window faced west, over the practice field, and the wire went out it and up, draped over the little ledge above the security light, twelve whole feet of it. It was, Jun thought, the saddest antenna imaginable — a strand of hookup wire held on by optimism. Mr. O seemed to read her face.

"The entire art of radio," he said, "is doing a lot with a little. This is a *random wire*. It is not a good antenna. It is barely an antenna. It is absolutely enough." He clicked the radio on and turned a knob. "Twenty-meter band. Fourteen megahertz. Lunchtime, which matters — the sun is up, and the sun is what makes this band work, and in about a month you'll be able to tell me why."

The hiss came up. And then he began to tune.

Jun had found the hiss of the Kenwood oceanic, abstract — the sound of nothing in particular. This was different. This was tuning, and as the dial moved, the nothing *resolved*. A rise and fall of beeping, slow and deliberate — "Morse code," Mr. O murmured, "somebody in a hurry, not for us today" — and then, sliding out of the static like a shape out of fog, a voice.

It was speaking Portuguese.

It was thin and it wavered and it ducked under the static and came up again, and it was utterly, perfectly clear that it was a *person*, an actual human throat, talking in a relaxed, rolling, unhurried way, laughing at something somebody else had said. Mr. O turned the volume up a notch and let it play. The voice was joined by a second one. There was a burst of static, and under it, faintly, a third.

"PY-something," Mr. O said, listening to the letters woven through the noise. "Brazil. Probably São Paulo state, this time of day, this time of year." He looked around at them. "No wires between here and there. No internet. His signal went up off a wire in his yard, bounced off a charged layer of the upper atmosphere — the ionosphere, that's chapter three — came down in the ocean, went back up, came down again, maybe twice, and arrived here, in my storeroom, on twelve feet of hookup wire hanging out a window. Two thousand miles a bounce." He let the radio murmur. "People have been doing this for a hundred years. Every day. All day. Most people just never tune in."

The room had gone very quiet. The gym-class whistles and the band-room trumpet bled through the wall from the world outside, and inside, over the little speaker, two men in Brazil laughed about something in Portuguese, at lunchtime, from five thousand miles away.

Priya had her graph-paper notebook out. She was not writing in it. She was staring at the window with her pencil stopped, and Jun watched her best friend lose an argument she was still pretending to have.

"Questions," said Mr. O. It was not a question.

"Why does it waver?" Bea said.

It was the first thing she had said all meeting, and it was, Jun would think later, such a perfect question — the exact right thread to pull — that it was like watching someone walk up to a tangled knot and pick the one loop that matters. Mr. O looked at her with frank delight.

"Because the ionosphere moves," he said. "It ripples, like the surface of a pond, and the signal ripples with it. The whole sky is moving, all the time. Radio people just learn to surf it." He nodded at her, once, the same nod she'd given Priya. "That's chapter three. You asked it early. Good."

He kept tuning. Past the Brazilians the band thinned, then rose again — a voice in English this time, flat and unhurried, with the particular sound of a man talking with his feet up. *"...yeah, the new antenna's just a wire off the chimney, but I'm hearing you five-nine, Tom, you're the strongest signal on the band..."*

"Five-nine," Mr. O translated, "is a signal report — readability five, strength nine. Top marks. He's saying the other fellow is loud. They always say the other fellow is loud; it's polite, like waving in traffic." He tuned a hair and found the other end of it: *"...QSL, QSL, good to hear you, Jim. QSL?"*

"And *QSL*," Mr. O said, "is one of the Q-codes. Three-letter shorthand from the telegraph days, older than any country that's still on the map. QSL means *I confirm, I heard you, acknowledged*. You'll see it on the postcards." He nodded at the corkboard. "Every card up there is a QSL. Somebody's proof that a contact really happened — that on some Tuesday in 1963, a kid in this room talked to Iceland, and Iceland wrote back."

"Where are they?" Theo asked. "The guys talking right now."

"Colorado and Texas, by the call signs," said Mr. O. "Amateur call signs are issued by district — you can read geography off them like a postmark. You'll learn the map without trying. Everybody does." He turned the volume down to a murmur and looked around at them, four students and a room gone quiet. "Understand what you're hearing. Not a broadcast. Not a stream. Two men, five hundred miles apart, talking to each other through equipment they built and maintain themselves, on a frequency that belongs to nobody, for no reason except that it's *interesting* and they *can*. The largest, oldest, most patient conversation in the world, going on twenty-four hours a day, and all it costs to join is a little math and a wire."

Priya's pencil had started moving again. Jun leaned over. On the graph paper, in Priya's small furious handwriting: *signals BOUNCE OFF THE SKY?? off WHAT, exactly??* — and then, a moment later, the beginning of a diagram.

Theo raised his hand halfway, didn't wait to be called on. "Can I touch the microphone?"

"There is no microphone on this one," Mr. O said. "We are listeners until you're licensed. The rule is absolute: I am the control operator of every transmission this club makes until the FCC says otherwise. But—" he raised a finger "—a licensed operator may let a guest speak on the air under supervision. It's called a third-party contact. It is legal, it is traditional, and it is how every ham you will ever meet had their first taste." He looked at Jun. "Which brings us to you, Ms. Alvarez. I hear you brought me a present."

---

Jun had hauled the Kenwood to school in a red wagon borrowed from the thrift shop's display window, and it sat by the door under her jacket like a smuggled pet. She uncovered it. Mr. O came around the desk and stood looking at it the way Mr. Hackett had — the way men of a certain age looked at this radio, she had begun to notice, like it was a dog they used to own.

"TS-520S," he said. "Where on earth."

"Hackett's dumpster," Jun said. "I replaced the fuse and cleaned the holder. It powers up. The dial glows. But it just hisses — worse than your little radio, and it has a *real* antenna socket on the back, so I figured it should hiss *less*, or better, or—" she ran out of radio words "—more usefully."

"You figured right," Mr. O said. He crouched, plugged it in, switched it on. The green glow came up. He tuned across the band slowly, listening, and his eyebrows did something complicated. "It's deaf," he said finally. "Alive, but deaf. Like a man with a head cold. My guess — and it is only a guess — is forty years of crud on the band-switch contacts and the controls. Oxidation. You can fix a lot of old radios with nothing more exotic than contact cleaner and patience."

"Can we do that?" Jun said. "As a club?"

"In this room? We can do it *properly*." Mr. O was already moving. The storeroom yielded, in quick succession, a Phillips screwdriver from a mug of them, a drop light, and a magnifying visor he wore with total disregard for how it looked. He had the Kenwood's case off in four minutes — "note the screws," he said, "real ones, from the era before plastic tabs" — and the five of them crowded around the opened chassis under the drop light while the Brazilians murmured from the windowsill.

It was the first time the others had seen inside. Jun watched them see it: Priya leaning in so far her hair nearly brushed the boards, Theo holding his phone up for photos like a tourist, Bea very still, taking it in with her judge's eyes. The dust. The lake-green boards. The two big tubes in their metal hats.

"Here is your radio's life story," Mr. O said, pointing with the screwdriver. "Power supply — heavy iron, built to outlive its owners. These are the final amplifier tubes — the muscle; when you transmit, these do the work, and they glow, and they are the reason this radio smells like a warm attic when it's happy. And here —" he tapped a stacked rotary switch wired in a spiral "—is the band switch. Forty years of air means forty years of oxide on every one of those contacts. The signal has to fight through rust to reach your ear. So the radio is not dead, Ms. Alvarez. Your fuse saw to that. It's just deaf in the way old men are deaf — mechanically, selectively, and with dignity."

"My grandfather's radio," Jun said slowly, "in Tucson. It smelled like that. Warm dust. I thought it was him."

"It was both," said Mr. O, and didn't make it weird, which she appreciated. "Now. To do this right we need the service manual — schematic, alignment procedure, voltages. They'll be online, scanned by some saint, but the print is better. We also need contact cleaner, which I have, and patience, which—" he looked at Theo "—we'll be developing."

"I have the manual," Jun said.

Everyone looked at her.

"The operating manual. It was in the dumpster with him. With the radio." She was already digging in her backpack — she'd been carrying it for five days like a library book she couldn't renew. She put it on the desk: the foxed cover, the crisp pages, OPERATING MANUAL, KENWOOD TS-520 SERIES. "It's not the *service* manual. But it's a start."

Mr. O picked it up with both hands, the way you handle something that survived against the odds. "Operating manual, original print, found *with the radio in a dumpster*," he said. "Ms. Alvarez, the ancestors are watching this club." He laid it on the desk with unusual care. "We'll find the service manual too. And then this radio is going to teach you more about electronics than I could in a year of physics, because it is *real*, and reality is the best textbook there is."

"Oh, it's worse than that," said Mr. O, and now he did smile. "A restoration this size is going to need more than my two hands — it's going to need somebody who has brought more of these back from the dead than I have. Luckily —" he uncapped the whiteboard marker and wrote two words on the board: *SI WEBB* — "I know a man who knows antennas the way your grandfather knew people. Silas Webb. W7JUNK. He picked that call sign on purpose, which tells you everything. He retired out Larkspur Road with six antenna towers and a barn full of the most glorious garbage in three counties, and he is — how do I put this kindly — allergic to visitors. He'll pretend to hate that we're coming."

"And will he?" Priya said. "Hate that we're coming?"

"He will pretend to," said Mr. O. "What he will actually hate is how much he loves it."

He capped the marker. Then, because it was the first meeting and he was a teacher to his marrow, he drew four small boxes on the whiteboard and wrote above them: *SEPTEMBER — JUNE.*

"Here is what the board requires," he said. "Four members, sustained." He tapped the first box. "One public demonstration, by June." He tapped the second. "The third and fourth boxes are mine. One: every one of you passes the Technician license exam. Two: this club builds something — with its hands, from scratch — that puts a signal into the sky." He put the marker down. "The board's boxes keep the room. My boxes keep the faith. Any objections?"

"Can the something we build," Jun said slowly, thinking of a copper wire catching the light on a roof in Tucson, "be an antenna?"

Mr. O looked at her for a second, and then at the sad strand of hookup wire hanging out the window, with two Brazilians still laughing somewhere on the other end of it.

"Ms. Alvarez," he said, "it had better be."

---

They voted, because Mr. O insisted on doing it properly even with five people in a storeroom. "Motion," he said, "to resurrect the Westridge High Amateur Radio Club for the 2025–26 school year. Discussion?"

"I move we discuss why we ever let it die," Theo said. "This room is the coolest thing in this school and it's been sitting here like a *Narnia* wardrobe."

"Second," said Priya. "I mean — I second the motion. Not the discussion. The discussion is obvious. Budgets."

"All in favor," said Mr. O.

Four hands went up. Bea's went up first, straight and unhesitating, like a flag. Mr. O raised his own and noted for the record that his didn't count, and raised it anyway, and the Westridge High Amateur Radio Club was, on paper, resurrected. Ms. Delgado, the vice principal, appeared at the door at four o'clock with her clipboard to collect the signup sheets of every club in the building, and Jun watched her count the names twice with an expression of total neutrality. Ms. Delgado had silver glasses and a reputation for knowing everything that happened in the building eleven minutes before it did.

"Four," Ms. Delgado said.

"Four," said Mr. O.

"The board rule also requires one public demonstration by June, Mr. Okafor."

"We are aware of the rule," said Mr. O pleasantly. "We intend to be a nuisance about it."

Something happened to Ms. Delgado's face that was not a smile but was in a smile's family. She wrote on her clipboard. "The storeroom stays, then. For now." She paused at the door and looked back — at the radio on the desk, the wire out the window, four students and a teacher in a room full of forty-year-old oscilloscopes. "My father listened to shortwave," she said, to nobody in particular, and left.

---

They stayed. Nobody had said they should, and Mr. O had technically dismissed them twice, but the four of them orbited the desk in the long September light, not quite ready to give the room back to the silence.

Theo was at the corkboard with his phone, photographing QSL cards one at a time. "Iceland, 1963," he announced. "This is *content*. You understand this is a hundred years of content just hanging on a wall. There's a card here from a *ship*." He turned around. "We should have a name. The club should have a name, a real one, for the podcast crossover episode. Something with *sky* in it. Skywire. The Skywire Society."

"We have a name," Priya said. "It's the Amateur Radio Club. It's on the sheet."

"The Amateur Radio Club is not a name, it's a *description*. Nobody's scared of a description."

"It's radio," Priya said. "Nobody's scared of us anyway."

"I'm scared of you a little," Theo said.

Jun had drifted to the window. The hookup wire hung down the wall outside, limp and ordinary, catching the late light. Somewhere five hundred miles east, Colorado and Texas were still talking, or had said seventy-three and gone to dinner. "What about you?" she asked, turning around. "Bea. You signed before any of us knew you were coming. What made you sign?"

The room settled. Bea was sitting in the second row again, hands folded, and she took the question the way she took everything — held it for a moment, examined it, gave it a fair hearing.

"I like things that are quiet," she said, "but aren't off."

Nobody had a follow-up to that. It hung there, exact and complete, the way a well-cut wire is exactly the length it is. Theo opened his mouth, closed it, and — to his credit, and to Jun's private delight — did not try to top it.

"Group chat," Priya said briskly, into the silence, because she was Priya. She held out her phone. "Numbers. All of you. I'm naming it *Room 114-B*, because that's accurate, and accuracy is a form of respect."

They put their numbers in. Jun's phone buzzed as Priya created the chat, and then buzzed again as Priya immediately sent, with no preamble, a photograph of the whiteboard — the four boxes, SEPTEMBER — JUNE — and under it, typed: *agenda item one: why does the sky bounce. i have questions.*

*That's chapter three,* Jun typed back.

*you say that like i'm going to wait,* Priya sent, and then, a second later: *it's called the ionosphere. i already looked it up. i'm going to check the math and if the math is wrong we're all going to hear about it.*

Jun grinned at her phone all the way down the hall.

Jun stayed after, helping coil the hookup wire. Outside, the practice field was going gold, and the JV soccer team was running laps, and through the little speaker, very faint now, Brazil was still talking.

"Mr. O," she said. "The twelve-foot wire. If that's a bad antenna and it still hears Brazil—"

"What happens when we build a good one?" he finished. He handed her the coil of wire. "That, Ms. Alvarez, is the entire book. Come back next week and we'll start writing it."
