# Copper Wire and Starlight

*A ham radio story for young builders*

## Introduction: How This Book Was Made

Before the story starts, you deserve to know a few things about who — and what — wrote it.

*Copper Wire and Starlight* was written by an artificial intelligence: an AI called Kimi, working in a coding tool called Kimi Code CLI, on July 22–23, 2026. In a single long session, the AI planned the story, wrote it, illustrated it, narrated the audiobook you may have found through the audiobook player, built the website and PDF you're reading now, and set up the machinery that publishes all of it around the world.

Here is what that actually looked like. Planning took about fifteen minutes: the outline, the characters, and a style bible with every antenna dimension pinned down so the book would never contradict itself. Building took the rest of a day — about nine hours from first plan to finished book, audiobook, and Docker image. The AI wrote the Prologue and Chapter 1 itself, then coordinated thirty-three helper agents: fourteen writers drafting chapters in parallel, fourteen artists drawing the pictures, three continuity reviewers who read the entire book hunting for mistakes (they found about forty-five, and every one was fixed), and one tooling agent that built the publishing pipeline. The audiobook — nine and three-quarter hours of narration in an AI voice named Ava — took about an hour to synthesize.

Counting the way an AI counts, the project used about seventy-two million tokens — tokens are the little pieces of words an AI reads and writes. About one and a half million of those were tokens it wrote itself: the story, the code, the pictures. The rest was reading, mostly the agents re-reading the growing book to keep it consistent. Seven hundred ninety-two model turns, one session, one day.

Why make a book like this? Because somewhere out there is a kid who has never felt the joy of building an antenna out of junk — out of soda cans, a broken tape measure, a salvaged extension cord — and hearing a voice come back out of the sky. This book is for them.

One more thing, and it matters more than who wrote what: **everything technical in this story is real.** Every antenna Jun, Priya, Theo, and Bea build actually works — the dimensions are correct, the math is correct, and the recipes are in Appendix A at the back of the book, written so you can follow them. The safety rules are real too, and they are not optional: antennas never go up anywhere near power lines, everything gets disconnected when thunder is in the air, and transmitting takes a licensed operator. Build like the club builds — carefully, with an adult who knows the rules — and the sky will answer you too.

*73 — that means best wishes, in ham talk. Now, about that dumpster…*

## Prologue: The Radio in the Dumpster

The dumpster behind Hackett's TV & Stereo smelled like hot plastic and old cardboard, and June Alvarez had her left leg hooked over its rim like a pirate boarding a ship.

It was the last Tuesday of August, the kind of Maple Run evening where the heat came up out of the sidewalk in waves and the sky stayed stubbornly, endlessly light. Tuesday was pickup eve on Delaney Street, which made Monday Jun's favorite salvage run of the week — the night people put out the things they were finally giving up on. She had a route. She had a flashlight clipped to her belt loop. She had a skateboard with a milk crate zip-tied to it, which her mom called "the saddest vehicle in three counties" and Jun called the *Alvarez Salvage Rig, Mark II*.

The Hackett's dumpster was not usually on her route, because Mr. Hackett repaired things and people mostly brought him good stuff. But Hackett's TV & Stereo was closing. After forty-one years, the hand-painted sign in the window — WE FIX ALMOST ANYTHING — had been joined by a printed one that said EVERYTHING MUST GO, and then, that week, by nothing at all. Dark windows. Empty shelves visible through the glass like ribs.

The dumpster, though. The dumpster was full.

"Careful up there," said a voice from the alley door. Mr. Hackett himself stood holding a box of tangled power cords, looking up at her with the expression of a man who had decided he was too tired to be surprised by anything ever again. He was maybe seventy, with magnifier glasses pushed up into white hair. "There's a microwave in there with a capacitor that'll bite you through your sneakers."

"Sorry," Jun said, not climbing down. "Are you throwing away the *good* stuff or the broken stuff?"

"Yes," said Mr. Hackett, and dropped the box of cords in with a clang. He looked at her for a second longer, at the flashlight, at the milk crate. "You're Rudy Alvarez's granddaughter, aren't you. You've got his exact same look. He used to come in for tubes."

"Yes sir," Jun said. Her grandfather had been dead for two years and it still rearranged something in her chest when a stranger said his name.

"Your grandfather could fix anything with a heating element," Mr. Hackett said. "Once watched him resurrect a television that had been *rained on.*" He squinted into the dumpster, then reached past her leg and hauled out a cracked plastic boombox. "Knock yourself out, kid. But if you cut yourself, you didn't get it from me."

---

She almost missed it. It was under the microwave, wrapped in a garbage bag that had split open down one side, and at first she thought it was a stereo receiver — one of the big silver ones from the seventies, all brushed aluminum and attitude. She got both hands under it and lifted, and it nearly took her back down into the dumpster with it, because the thing weighed as much as a Thanksgiving turkey.

It was not a stereo receiver.

It was the size of a toolbox and built like a bank vault, battleship gray, with a face full of knobs the size of gumballs and one enormous tuning dial, round as a soup can, with numbers printed in glowing green rows: 3.5, 7, 14, 21, 28. There was a window with a needle in it. There were toggle switches that clicked like a ratchet when she flipped one. There was a dent in the top left corner and a scorch of dust along the vents, and across the front, in the confident blocky lettering of a company that had never heard of minimalism, it said:

KENWOOD TS-520S.

Jun knew what it was. She knew it the way you know a face from a photograph you've looked at a hundred times. On Abuelo Rudy's workbench, in the little room behind her grandparents' garage in Tucson, there had been a radio almost exactly like this one, with a dial that glowed green and a microphone on a coiled cord. She had been seven the last time she saw it. She remembered the smell — dust and warm metal and her grandfather's coffee — and she remembered him lifting her onto the stool and saying, in his careful English, *Mija, this one talks to the whole world.*

She sat down right there in the alley, on a busted futon, with seventeen pounds of dead radio across her knees, and laughed once, out loud, at nobody.

"Find something?" Mr. Hackett called from the door.

"I don't know yet," Jun said.

She went back in, more carefully now, because a radio like that did not travel alone. The microwave came out with a shriek of metal. Under it: a sodden cardboard box, collapsing at the corners, and inside, wrapped in a grocery bag that had kept it miraculously dry, a spiral-bound manual — OPERATING MANUAL, KENWOOD TS-520 SERIES — its cover foxed with age but its pages crisp. Jun held it the way you hold a map to a place you have always wanted to go.

Mr. Hackett had come out to watch, leaning on the alley doorframe with the box of power cords at his feet. "That thing came in last month," he said, nodding at the Kenwood. "Estate cleanout. I told them I don't do radios anymore — nobody does radios anymore, that's what I told them — and they left it anyway. Like leaving a piano on a curb." He toed the box of cords. "There's a microphone in here somewhere with a Kenwood plug on it. Four pins, round connector. Came out of the same estate, if that tells you anything." He pushed the box toward her with his shoe. "Consider it a going-out-of-business sale. One item. Price: you carry it all away before I change my mind about the whole human race."

Jun found the microphone at the bottom of the box, under a nest of RCA cables — a heavy black fist mic, its coiled cord kinked but intact. It had a satisfying, serious click when she pressed the switch. PTT, it said in the manual diagram she would memorize three nights later. *Push to talk.* Push, and talk, and the whole world might answer.

"You fix it," Mr. Hackett said, "you come back and tell me. That's the only warranty I offer."

Getting it home was an engineering problem. The Kenwood did not fit in the milk crate. Jun ended up bungee-cording it flat to the skateboard deck and pushing the whole rig uphill on Delaney Street like a very small, very important parade float, one hand on the radio at all times, because if it rolled off it would roll *through* something.

Second Wind Thrift sat on the corner of Delaney and Fifth, and the sign in the window said QUALITY USED GOODS in letters her mom had painted when Jun was nine. The apartment was upstairs. Jun came through the shop because the back stairs were blocked with furniture, and because part of her wanted to be seen carrying it.

Her mother was pricing a box of lamps and looked up exactly halfway through writing a tag.

"No," said Marisol Alvarez.

"You don't even know what it is."

"It's a twenty-pound *no* is what it is." Her mom came around the counter, wiping her hands on her apron, and looked at the Kenwood the way you look at a stray dog your kid has just brought home — with the grim recognition that the fight was already lost. She read the badge. "TS-five-twenty. It's a ham radio. A big one." She closed her eyes. "Mija. You have your grandfather's disease, and there is no cure."

"You knew what it was that fast?"

"I grew up with one of those on the kitchen table," her mother said. "Your abuelo's voice would come out of it at two in the morning, talking to Argentina. Your abuela would throw a towel over it like a parrot's cage." She put her hand on the dented corner, briefly, and something crossed her face that Jun filed away for later. Then she was Mom again. "We *sell* things. We do not keep things. That is the entire principle of this household."

"It's not a thing," Jun said. "It's a project."

"That's what your grandfather said about the boat."

---

Upstairs, in her room, Jun cleared her desk in one sweep — salvaged alarm clocks in various stages of resurrection, a turkey-baster soldering iron stand, three dead laptops she was down to two dead laptops from — and set the Kenwood in the middle of it like an altar.

Then she got the shoebox.

It lived on the top shelf of her closet, behind the winter sweaters. ABUELO — RADIO, it said on the lid, in her mom's handwriting, because after the funeral in Tucson nobody had known what to do with the little stuff and Marisol had packed it for someday. Inside: a battered black handheld radio the size of a candy bar, with a stubby rubber antenna and YAESU FT-60R printed on it. A deck of QSL cards — postcards from Brazil, Japan, South Africa, every one stamped with a call sign and N6RUA in confident block letters. A logbook filled with her grandfather's dense, slanting handwriting, columns of dates and frequencies and names of people in other hemispheres. And a photograph, creased from being carried in a wallet: the roof of the little house in Tucson at dusk, the sky going violet over the mountains, and strung from a TV mast to a palm tree, a single bare copper wire catching the last of the light like it was glowing.

Copper wire and starlight, Jun thought. She had never said it out loud. It was just what the picture was *called*, in her head, and had been for two years.

She opened her salvage log — a composition book with SALVAGE LOG VOL. 4 on the cover, columns for date, item, source, condition, and potential — and wrote:

*Entry 214. Kenwood TS-520S ham transceiver. Source: Hackett's dumpster. Condition: dead, but hopeful. Potential: don't know yet. Something big.*

---

The autopsy took an hour. She wasn't afraid of it — she'd been dissecting dead electronics since she was eleven, when she'd realized the thrift shop's "as-is" bin was basically a free university. Unplugged, case off, flashlight in her teeth. Inside, the Kenwood was beautiful and filthy: dust like gray felt over circuit boards the color of lake water, two big vacuum tubes in metal hats, a power transformer the size of a fist.

The fuse had blown so hard it had gone cloudy. The fuse holder's metal ends were furred with green corrosion, like something that had sunk in the ocean. That was probably why it was in a dumpster — somebody had plugged it in, gotten nothing, and given up four screws short of the actual problem.

Jun knew about the corrosion because she knew about corrosion. What she did not know — and knew she did not know — was everything above the fuse: what the tubes did, why the dial had five bands, what "SSB" and "CW" and "VOX" meant on the switches. She could make it safe and maybe make it *glow*. She could not make it talk.

She dug a replacement fuse out of her parts bin, cleaned the holder with a pencil eraser and a cotton swab of vinegar, and screwed the case back on. She said "please," out loud, and plugged it in.

The dial lamp came up out of nothing, a deep aquarium green. From the speaker, very faintly, came a hiss — thin and oceanic, the sound of the entire electromagnetic universe ignoring her. She turned the big dial with two fingers and the numbers slid past and the hiss rose and fell like weather.

No voices. Not yet. But a glow, and a hiss, which meant the heart was beating.

She did what she always did when a salvage came home breathing: she opened the least-dead laptop and asked the internet what she was holding.

The internet, for once, did not waste her time. The TS-520S, she learned, was a legend from the year before disco died — a *transceiver*, which meant it transmitted *and* received, which meant the microphone in the cord box wasn't for listening. It covered the *HF bands*: 80 meters, 40, 20, 15, 10 — the frequencies that bounced off the sky and landed on other continents. There were forums full of people who still used exactly this radio, daily, on purpose, in the year of our lord two thousand twenty-five. There were restoration threads with photographs of the exact circuit boards now glowing four feet from her pillow. Somebody had written *the 520 is a radio that assumes you are smart, and it is right.*

And then she fell all the way down the hole, the way you do.

She learned that ham radio was not a museum. It was a license — an actual government license, with a test, like driving but for the sky — and at the end of the test they gave you a *call sign*, a letters-and-numbers name that belonged to you and nobody else on Earth. She learned that people her age had call signs. She watched a video of a girl who could not have been twelve call *CQ* — three letters that meant *is anybody out there* — on a scratched-up radio on a kitchen table, and get an answer from a man in Finland, and try to play it cool and completely fail, her whole face igniting. Jun watched it twice.

She learned that the green numbers on the dial — 3.5, 7, 14, 21, 28 — were *megahertz*, millions of waves per second, and that hams called them by wavelength instead: 80 meters, 40 meters, 20 meters, like neighborhoods in a city that wrapped all the way around the planet. She learned the word *ionosphere*: a layer of the upper atmosphere, charged by the sun, that bent shortwave signals back down to Earth, so that a wire in Arizona could be heard in Argentina at two in the morning.

She sat very still when she read that part, because that was no longer an abstraction. That was the kitchen table in Tucson. That was the towel thrown over the parrot's cage.

She got the shoebox down again and opened her grandfather's logbook, and this time she read it like a book instead of an artifact. The columns were a code she was starting to crack: date, time, frequency, call sign, name, *RST* — a three-digit signal report, she now knew, readability-strength-tone — and a last column of little notes in his dense slanting hand. *Luis, Mendoza — his tomatoes again.* *JA-something, a woman named Keiko who sent a card with a painted crane.* *VK, thunderstorm there, short QSO.* Page after page of weather and tomatoes and grandchildren, conducted in a code of dits and dahs and scratchy voices, decades of it, a whole hidden social network that had hummed along invisibly above everybody's heads, all the time, like the plumbing of the sky.

On the very last page, after the final dated entry, there was nothing — and then, near the bottom, a single penciled line. No date. Written lightly, the way you write something that is only meant for one specific reader, someday:

*Para mija — el cielo es paciente. Te espera.*

For mija — the sky is patient. It is waiting for you.

Jun read it four times. Then she closed the logbook very gently, the way you close a door on a room where someone is sleeping, and sat in the aquarium-green glow with her eyes stinging and her heart absolutely full of radio.

Jun sat in front of it for a long time, and the house went quiet around her — her mom closing up the shop below, the Tuesday-night sounds of Delaney Street — and she thought about her grandfather on a stool in Tucson, talking to Argentina at two in the morning, and about a wire on a roof catching the light.

It was on her way to brush her teeth that she saw the flyer on the shop's community corkboard, sandwiched between a lost-cat poster and a church rummage sale. It was clearly photocopied from a hand-drawn original, and the hand that drew it had spent most of its effort on a little picture of a wire antenna strung between two towers with radio waves arcing off it.

**WESTRIDGE HIGH AMATEUR RADIO CLUB**, it said. **Room 114-B, Wednesdays. New members wanted. All you need is curiosity.**

And underneath, in smaller print, in parentheses, in a tone Jun would later learn to recognize perfectly:

*(If we still exist.)*

School started Thursday. Jun stood there with her toothbrush in her mouth, reading the flyer twice, while behind her, up the stairs, in the dark of her room, a green dial glowed like a porch light for a house she hadn't visited yet.

She took the flyer down, photographed it, and pinned it back crooked so some other kid might tilt their head at it. Then she went upstairs and opened the salvage log to entry 214 and, in the *potential* column, crossed out *don't know yet. Something big.* and wrote:

*Everything. Check back in a year.*
