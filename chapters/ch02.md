## Chapter 2: The Pop-Can Dipole

The little black handheld on the storeroom desk was hissing like a tire with a slow leak, and under the hiss a robot was patiently spelling its own name in Morse code.

"Dit-dah-dah. Dah-dah-dit-dit-dit." Mr. O translated from the stool by the window, where he was grading lab reports with one ear cocked. "W, seven. Then M, R, slash R. It identifies itself every ten minutes, around the clock, even when nobody has talked on it in days. Federal regulations. Even a robot has to say its name."

"There's a robot," Priya said slowly, "broadcasting in Morse code. On purpose. In the year 2025."

"It's a repeater, which is better than a robot," said Mr. O. "It's a radio on a hilltop northeast of town, with very good ears and a very big mouth. It listens on one frequency and repeats whatever it hears on another, all day, every day, so that somebody with a five-watt handheld in a basement can be heard thirty miles off. This one is W7MR, slash R — the Maple Run machine. For practical purposes, it's the town square."

The robot finished spelling, paused, and gave way to humans — two men discussing a truck, at length, with feeling. It was wonderful for about ten seconds, and then it was awful, because both voices kept dissolving into static and surfacing again like swimmers. Jun found herself leaning closer to the radio, as if proximity would help. It was her radio — the battered Yaesu FT-60R out of the shoebox on her closet shelf, the one her abuelo had carried. She had the squelch cracked open like a door, letting the whole noisy world in.

"It keeps *dropping*," she said.

"You are listening to a hilltop, twenty miles away, on a rubber antenna the size of a cigar, inside a brick building," said Mr. O. "That little antenna is called a rubber duck, which is the technical term, go ahead and laugh — Theo laughed — and it is a compromise. Somebody once called it a dummy load that occasionally radiates. Which is rude, and also fair. It hears. It does not hear *well*."

"So we need a better antenna," Jun said.

"Now you are a radio club," said Mr. O, and set down his red pen.

It was the first Wednesday of October, the light giving up earlier every day, and the club had four members, one small radio, and a room full of other people's treasure. Mr. O came around the desk and took the FT-60R, not ungently.

"Understand the rules first. You four cannot transmit. Not one watt — not unless a licensed control operator is physically present and in charge of the radio, and for this club, that operator is me, or someone like me. That is federal law, not teacher fussiness. But *receiving* takes no license at all. The sky is free to listen to." He turned the little radio over in his hand. "And there is much more to hear than two gentlemen discussing a truck. Tuesday nights, seven o'clock, the Maple Run net meets on this machine. Thirty, forty check-ins. Weather, swap-and-shop, who's building what, who needs a hand putting up a tower. It's the hobby's front porch. I'd like you to hear it properly — not through a keyhole."

"Then how do we hear it properly?" Priya said. "Buy an antenna, I assume. There's always a catalog."

"There is always a catalog," Mr. O agreed. "We are not going to use it." He handed the radio back to Jun. "Program it first. You should know your own machine. Frequency: one four six point seven six zero."

Jun thumbed the keys, tongue in the corner of her mouth, while the others watched. The FT-60R's memory had stood empty for two years — wiped or never used, a little blank neighborhood where her grandfather's settings should have been. It felt good to put a name in it.

"Now the repeater shift," said Mr. O. "Minus six hundred kilohertz. When you transmit — someday, with me sitting next to you — your radio whispers on the machine's input frequency, six hundred kilohertz *down*, and the machine shouts it back out on the seven-six-oh. Listen one place, talk another. That's the dance. And one more thing: a tone, one hundred hertz, called a PL. Your radio hums it under your voice, so faint you can't hear it. The machine only opens its ears for a signal humming the right note. It keeps static and taxi radios from waking it up at three in the morning."

"A secret handshake," Bea said.

Mr. O looked at her with the frank delight he reserved for good questions. "A secret handshake," he said, "that every single person in town knows. Exactly."

Jun saved the channel and labeled it, four letters at a time, W7MR. Then she looked up. "You said we're not buying an antenna."

"We are not," said Mr. O. "Saturday morning, nine o'clock, we're driving out Larkspur Road to see a man about one. Wear boots. Bring gloves if you have them. And do not —" he pointed around the room, taking in all four of them "— under any circumstances, mention the catalog."

"Is he going to hate us?" Theo asked.

"He will pretend to," said Mr. O. "The pretense is traditional. It's how you know it's working."

---

Saturday came up gray and cold-edged, the kind of October morning that smelled like wet leaves and somebody's distant woodsmoke, and Mr. O's station wagon — the color of weak tea, rattling like a drawer of forks — carried them out of town, Jun's FT-60R riding shotgun in her lap.

Larkspur Road ran north out of Maple Run past the feed store and the closed-up pumpkin stands, through fields gone to stubble, the ditches full of milkweed gone to fluff. Jun watched it go by, salvage log open on her knee, not writing, just looking. At the end of the road, where the pavement gave up to gravel, there was a mailbox on a crooked post with WEBB lettered on it in black paint, and beyond it a lane running up through a meadow to a white farmhouse and a red barn.

And masts. Jun counted them as they came up the lane, because she could not possibly have helped it. Six.

One was a serious tower of crisscrossed steel by the barn, with a big antenna on top like the skeleton of a metal bird. One was a tall, thin vertical pipe with guy wires running off it in three directions. One was an honest-to-goodness telephone pole with pulleys at the top and wires sloping away from it into the oak trees. One was an old farm windmill, its fan long gone, doing duty as a pole for a wire that dipped and rose across the meadow. Two more stood by the house and barn, holding up things Jun had no name for yet. Everything had coax coming off it. Every line of it converged on the barn.

"You can count them later," said a voice.

The man standing beside the lane was maybe seventy, in a flannel shirt nearly the color of the sky, holding a mug in both hands like a small heater. He had a white beard that had lost an argument with a pair of scissors and the deeply suspicious expression of a man watching four teenagers get out of a car on his property. At his feet, an old brown mutt with one ear folded wrong sat up, woofed once for the record, and trotted forward to inspect the visitors.

"Si," said Mr. O. "You look terrible. Healthy, I mean."

"Okafor." The man did not smile, quite. "You're early."

"You said nine. It's nine."

"Then you're on time, which is the same as early." The dog had finished with Theo's cuffs and moved on. It sniffed Bea's sneaker, looked up at her for a long moment, and then sat down *on* her foot, having reviewed the applicants and made its selection. "That's Resistor," Si said. "He doesn't like people. Make a note."

"Noted," said Bea, not moving her foot.

Si Webb looked over the four of them — Jun with her salvage log, Priya with her graph-paper notebook already out, Theo taking in the tower with his mouth slightly open, Bea with a dog on her shoe — and grunted, which Jun would learn was a complete sentence.

"Coffee's in the barn," he said, and turned and walked off, which they would also learn was an invitation.

---

The barn was not a barn anymore. The big doors stood open to the cold; inside, where stalls and hay had once been, a workshop went up two stories into the gloom of the rafters — and it was full. It was full the way Second Wind's back room was full, the way the storeroom of 114-B was full, but bigger, older, and organized by a logic Jun could almost smell: coils of coaxial cable hung from pegs like black wreaths; coffee cans on shelves were labeled in faded marker — SCREWS GOOD, SCREWS ASK, HOSE CLAMPS, INSULATORS, DO NOT THROW OUT; aluminum tubing stood in a corner like a stand of silver bamboo; a workbench ran the whole length of the back wall under a bank of lights, with a vise, a drill press, and a pegboard of tools that each had their outline painted around them. On a desk at the far end, in a pool of lamplight, sat a modern radio — IC-7300, said the badge — with a glowing screen where a waterfall of color slid down like an aurora, voices murmuring out of it to nobody, because they were just on.

Jun stood in the doorway and thought: *junk Nirvana*. She did not say it out loud. She wrote it in the salvage log later.

Si poured coffee from a pot on a hotplate into a mug for Mr. O, who accepted it with the face of a man taking one for the team. Then, to Jun's astonishment, Si poured four more, into four mismatched mugs, and handed them around.

"You're builders now," he said. "Drink up. It's terrible. I've been working on the recipe since nineteen seventy-nine and I stand by it."

It was, in fact, terrible — hot, though, and Jun wrapped her hands around the mug and didn't mind. Theo took one brave sip and looked out at the horizon like a sailor reconsidering the sea. Priya took a sip, made a note in her notebook, and took another, which Jun suspected was data collection. Bea held hers without drinking, with the dog leaning against her leg.

Si set the pot down and looked at Mr. O. "You said they want to hear the hill."

"The repeater," Mr. O said. "Tuesday net. They're hearing it through a keyhole in a brick room on a rubber duck."

"Mm." Si turned to the four of them. "And what do you need?"

"An antenna," Jun said.

"Correct." He walked to the corner of the barn, and they heard him drag something, and he came back pushing a big blue recycle bin with his boot, the kind the county hands out, and set it in the middle of the floor with his foot. It was full — to the top, heaped — with empty soda cans, dozens of them, and every single one Jun could see was the same brand of root beer.

"There's your parts," Si said. "Build me an antenna."

Nobody said anything for a moment. The heater ticked. Resistor sighed like an old man.

"Out of... the recycling?" Theo said.

"Out of *aluminum*," said Si. "The root beer was just the delivery system." He looked around at their faces with deep satisfaction. "Okafor, you didn't tell them anything. Good. I like a fresh audience." He reached into the bin and set one can on the workbench, where it rocked once and stood, ordinary as anything, catching the lamplight. "Two-meter band. One hundred forty-six megahertz, where your repeater lives. Who can tell me how long a wave is at a hundred and forty-six megahertz?"

Priya's hand was already up. Si pointed at her with the coffee mug.

"You need the speed of light," she said. "Radio waves move at light speed, three hundred million meters a second. And the frequency is a hundred and forty-six *million* waves a second. So one wave is — three hundred divided by a hundred forty-six —" her pencil moved — "about two meters. Two point oh five." She looked up with the particular expression of a person who has just watched a card trick resolve into arithmetic. "That's why they call it the two-meter band. The band is named after the length of the wave itself."

"Give the girl a diploma," said Si.

"Now," said Si. "An antenna doesn't have to *be* much. It has to be the right *size* much." He picked up the can and turned it slowly, the way Jun turned lamps in the shop to see how they were wired. "The simplest antenna there is, is a dipole. Two wires, or two rods, or two of anything conductive, end to end, with the feedline hooked in the middle. Each side a quarter of a wave long, so the whole thing makes a half. There's an old rule of thumb — four sixty-eight divided by the frequency in megahertz gives you the whole half-wave in feet. Run it for a hundred and forty-six and you get a hair over three feet — call it thirty-eight and a half inches of antenna, end to end. Which makes each side—" he tapped the can and looked at them.

"Nineteen and a half, minus a little," Priya said. "Nineteen point three inches, if we're being fussy."

"We're being fussy," said Si. "Nineteen point three. Remember that number; it's the whole ballgame." He handed the can to Bea, along with a pair of calipers from the workbench. "Measure it. Height."

Bea took the can in one hand and the calipers in the other, and did it the way she did everything — once, carefully, without narrating. "Four point eight three inches," she said.

"Four point eight three," Si repeated. "Times four is—"

"Nineteen point three two." Priya said it before anyone else had started, and then she stopped, and Jun watched her best friend's face do the thing it did when the universe lined up neatly. "Four cans is a quarter wave. The cans are *already the right size.* The soda companies did the antenna math for us."

"Now you see it," Si said, and he was grinning into his beard, the first crack in the curmudgeon all morning. "Four cans a side. Stack 'em on a stick, hook your coax in the middle, and you've got a dipole for the two-meter band."

"But why does it *work*?" Jun asked. "I mean — it's a can. It held root beer."

Si set his mug down, which they would learn meant a lecture was coming, except it wasn't a lecture, it was three sentences. "Radio waves can't read," he said. "A wave doesn't know that used to be root beer. It knows an aluminum tube, so long, such a diameter. Shape and size — that's all anything is, to a wave. And an antenna the right length is *resonant*, which is a fifty-cent word for a thing you already know. Push a kid on a swing. Push at the right moment, every time, and the swing soars — the pushes add up. Push at the wrong moment and you're fighting the swing and the energy comes back at you. An antenna off-resonance fights your radio the same way. The right length is the right moment." He picked his mug up. "That's the whole theory. The rest is sanding."

"There's *sanding*?" Theo said.

"There's always sanding," said Si, and dragged the broom out of the corner — or what had been one, once, before the bristle end gave out years ago and left a perfectly good wooden stick. "Broom died for the cause a while back. I've been saving the corpse." He looked them over. "Here's what you're working with. Gather it."

He recited, and they scavenged the barn for it, and Jun wrote it in the salvage log with the deep satisfaction of a girl whose entire religion was being confirmed:

- Eight root beer cans, rinsed (Si: "Rinse them again. Sticky RF is still sticky.")
- One wooden broomstick, deceased broom
- A coil of RG-58 coax, about twenty feet, off the pegboard wall
- Two ring lugs, two small bolts with washers and nuts, from the coffee can labeled SCREWS GOOD
- Sandpaper, a tape measure, a marker, zip ties
- The drill press and a hole saw, and — Si handed these out personally, with a look — safety glasses

The hole saw went first. Si stood with folded arms while Jun, heart pounding, centered the saw on the bottom of the first can and drilled a clean hole the size of the broomstick. "Fingers," Si said, once, and that was the entire safety lecture, delivered in the tone of a man who had all ten of his and intended them to keep theirs. It took Bea to find the rhythm — light pressure, let the saw work — and after that they had a production line: Theo feeding, Bea drilling, Jun stacking, Priya tallying eight holes in eight cans like a census.

Then the sanding. "The paint's the problem," Si said. "Pretty red paint is an insulator. So's the oxide on aluminum. Where two cans touch, I want bright metal on bright metal, or they're strangers. Sand the mouth rims. Sand the bottom edge of every can that seats into a mouth."

They sanded. Bea sanded like the cans had personally wronged her — every rim bright silver, checked by eye, checked twice. Jun sanded fast, because Jun always worked fast, and if one can went onto the stack with a dull ring of paint still clinging inside its mouth, well. It looked seated. It felt seated. She would remember that can.

"Now build me two towers," said Si. "Slide 'em on the stick, base first, so the mouth of one swallows the base of the next. Mouth to base, mouth to base, like cans in a pantry. They're made to stack — the can people did that engineering for you too. Four on one side, four on the other."

The cans slid onto the broomstick and nested with a satisfying little metal *tick*, mouth to base, and — not all at once, but one can at a time, the way anything real gets built — the heap of recycling started looking like something. Two towers of four, lying along the stick in opposite directions, bright at the rims where Bea had made them honest.

"Leave the middle loose for now," Si said. "That's the feedpoint. One inch of gap between the two inner cans — not 'about an inch,' Miss Fast-Hands, an inch, you'll set it with the tape when we're ready. That gap is where the signal gets on and off."

Jun slid the two inner cans toward each other and eyeballed the space between their bases. About an inch. Close enough. She would remember that too.

While the others fitted cans, Si cut a length of RG-58 off the coil and stripped one end back — an inch of black jacket, then the braid peeled and twisted into a pigtail, then the white center insulation, then the bright copper center conductor standing out alone. He did it slowly, the way a man does something he's done ten thousand times and knows is worth watching. "Coax," he said. "Center conductor carries your signal up. The braid shields it — keeps the world out, keeps your signal in. Center goes to one side of the dipole. Braid goes to the other. One side pulls while the other pushes. That's the whole conversation."

Jun soldered the ring lugs on — center first, then braid — and Si watched over her shoulder without touching anything. "You've held an iron before."

"Since I was eleven," Jun said. "Turkey-baster stand and everything."

"Hold it like that and you'll go far," Si said, and from him it landed like a medal.

Then came the bolting, and here is where it went wrong, in the way things go wrong when the podcast host of the group chooses that exact moment to ask a question. Theo, who had been narrating the build into his phone in a hushed documentary voice — "*day one of the Cantenna Chronicles*" — asked Jun whether the lugs went on the inner cans, the ones at the gap, and Jun said "the inner cans, yes," and bolted the center lug to the base of the inner can on the left, and bolted the braid lug, absently, mid-sentence, to the *second* can out on the right. The cans were identical. The stick was symmetrical. Nobody saw it, because there was nothing to see — a lug on a can, same as a lug on a can.

"Cantenna is taken," Priya told Theo, not looking up from her measurements. "It's a real thing. It's a dummy load in a can."

"The Root Beer Special, then."

"Don't name it till it hears," said Si.

Last came the choke. Si had Jun coil the coax, right where it came off the feedpoint, into four neat loops the size of a cereal bowl, and zip-tie the loops together so they held their shape. "Why am I doing this?" she asked.

"Because the outside of that braid can pick up current and become part of the antenna if you let it," Si said. "Then your feedline's radiating and your measurements lie to you and the antenna behaves like a dog with two masters. Four loops choke it off. Keeps the antenna the antenna and the feedline the feedline." He stepped back and looked at the thing on the workbench — broomstick, eight cans, bright lugs, coiled coax — with the expression of a man regarding a decent birdhouse. "That's it. That's a two-meter dipole, unless it isn't. The analyzer tells us which."

"It looks," Theo said, tilting his head, "exactly like eight root beer cans on a broomstick."

"Everything looks like what it's made of," said Si. "Even people. Doesn't matter. The wave can't read."

---

Si's antenna analyzer was a battered beige box the size of a sandwich, with two needles behind a scratched window and a thumbwheel, and it lived on the workbench in a nest of adapters like a bad-tempered pet. He clamped the broomstick horizontally in the vise, walked everybody three steps back, and plugged the coax in.

"This box puts out a whisper of a signal and sweeps it across the band, and measures how much of it the antenna swallows and how much it spits back," he said. "An antenna that's happy swallows nearly everything — that's an SWR of one to one. Under two to one, your radio will never complain. Above that, power's bouncing back down the coax at you, fighting the swing at the wrong moment." He thumbed the wheel. "Let's see what you built."

The needles swung. The little box hummed through the band, and the dip — the place where the SWR needle dipped down toward happy — wasn't there. Not at 146. The needle hung up around four to one at the repeater frequency, and when Si swept lower, hunting, it sagged reluctantly toward three-to-one somewhere down around 138 and thought better of it.

"Well," said Si. "Congratulations. You have built an antenna. It is an antenna for some *other* band, and a sick one." He folded his arms. "What do we do?"

"Take it apart," Theo said, with the air of a man volunteering to supervise.

"Diagnose *first*," said Si. "What should each side of a dipole look like, electrically?"

"Identical," Priya said. "Mirror images. Nineteen point three inches each."

"So measure."

Priya ran the tape along the left stack: four cans, mouth to base, inner base to outer mouth — nineteen point three, right on the number. She ran it along the right stack, and her eyebrows went up. "Seventeen and a half. There's slack in the joints — the cans aren't seated the same." She pressed the stack gently with one finger, and midway down it, one can gave a tiny metallic rattle. "And this one isn't *touching*. It's just hanging there."

Bea reached past her and flicked the rattling can with a fingernail. It sang, dull and loose. "Paint," she said, with quiet certainty. "Inside the mouth. It never got sanded, so it never seated."

Jun closed her eyes. The fast sanding. The can that had *looked* seated. "That one's mine," she said.

"Keep going," said Si, unbothered, like a doctor who had found the first symptom and expected a second. He took a multimeter off the pegboard, set it to beep, and handed it to her. "Center pin of the coax to every can on the left. Braid to every can on the right. Make it beep."

Left side: beep, beep, beep, beep — all four cans, one happy circuit. Right side: the meter beeped on the can the lug was bolted to, beeped on the next one out — and Jun stopped, and looked at the lug, and looked at the gap at the center of the boom where the lug was *supposed* to be.

"Oh, no," she said. "The braid is on the second can out. Not the inner can. I bolted it on while Theo was—" she glanced at Theo — "while I was talking."

"While *I* was talking," Theo said gallantly. "I take full responsibility for being interesting."

"So the right side was fed from the wrong place," Si said, "through a can joint that wasn't a joint, with the whole stack the wrong length. Three mistakes, and every one of them yours to fix, which is the *good* news, because mistakes you made are mistakes you understand." He unclamped the broomstick from the vise and set it back on the bench. "Well?"

They fixed it. Bea pulled the lazy can and sanded the paint out of its mouth until the aluminum shone, and seated it with a twist and a *tick* you could feel through the stick. Jun unbolted the braid lug and moved it to the inner can where it belonged, and re-soldered the joint for luck, and checked it twice with the meter. Priya laid the tape along both stacks and called numbers while Jun slid cans — nineteen point three, nineteen point three, mirror images — and then set the gap between the two inner cans with the tape measure held right against the stick, one inch, not about an inch. *One inch.*

"Again," said Si.

Back in the vise. Coax on the analyzer. Everybody back three steps, and this time Jun found she was holding her breath like it was a rocket launch. Si thumbed the wheel and the little box hummed up the band, and the needle swung — and dipped, way down, right in the middle of the two-meter band, a deep clean V of a dip with its point at 146 and change.

"One point nine," Si read. He slid the right stack a quarter inch on the stick, and watched the needle. "One point eight to one. There's your number, and it is *fine*. Under two, the radio's happy, and happy is all we ask." He switched the box off and looked at them. "Write it down. First try: four to one, resonant nowhere near home. Second try: one point eight. Nobody buys that improvement. You *build* it."

Priya was already writing in her notebook, and Jun wrote it in the salvage log too, because some numbers deserve to exist in two places: *Entry 221. Pop-can dipole, 2 meters. SWR 1.8:1 at 146 MHz. Cost: one dead broom, eight cans, zero dollars.*

"Does it *hear*, though?" Jun asked. "The analyzer says it should. What does the hill say?"

Si looked at her for a moment, and then at Mr. O, and something passed between the two men that Jun couldn't read and would think about all week. "Bring your little radio," Si said.

They trooped out into the meadow with the afternoon going gray-gold around the edges, Resistor trotting ahead with the importance of a dog leading an expedition. Si had Jun hold the broomstick upright, cans to the sky and cans to the ground, while Mr. O produced from his jacket a small adapter — "Si mailed it to the school," he said, "with a note that said *stop breaking your duck*, which I chose to read as encouragement" — and in a minute the RG-58 from the feedpoint was screwed, through the adapter, into the FT-60R's antenna socket where the rubber duck usually lived.

Jun turned it on. The repeater was quiet for a moment, and then it wasn't — somebody keyed up, and a voice came out of the little speaker so clean that she actually flinched. No swimming, no surfacing, no hash. A man she had never met was saying something unremarkable about firewood, and he might as well have been standing in the meadow with them.

"That's the hill," Si said. "Twenty miles, on eight cans and a dead broom. The difference between a rubber duck and a resonant antenna, at the frequency the antenna was built for — that's not a small difference, that's the *whole* difference. That's the hobby."

Theo was recording again, murmuring into his phone. Bea was watching Jun's face rather than the antenna, and gave her one small nod, as if confirming a measurement. Priya said, very quietly, "Okay," which from Priya was a conversion.

"Tuesday," said Si. "Seven o'clock, the net. You don't listen on that duck anymore — you take *this* up somewhere high, and you'll hear the whole porch talking." He took the broomstick from Jun and looked at it the way Mr. Hackett had looked at the Kenwood, the way men of a certain age looked at a thing that used to be junk and wasn't anymore. "And then, if Okafor here supervises and I hold the license, maybe we let it *talk* once. A hello. Third party, legal as church."

Jun's stomach did a small backflip. "On the air? Us?"

"One of you," Si said. "Somebody who soldered a lug to the wrong can and lived." He turned for the barn, and then stopped, the way people do when they've been saving something. "Okafor tells me you dragged a Kenwood 520 out of a dumpster, Alvarez. Estate radio, green dial, tubes."

"Yes sir," Jun said. "It powers up. It's just — deaf."

"Deaf is fixable. Deaf is usually *dirt*." Si shifted the broomstick under his arm. "When the weather turns, I'll come to that storeroom of yours. If it can be waked up, we'll wake it — that's a winter's project and a half, and you'll know a radio when we're done." Then he was walking, and Resistor peeled away from Bea's leg to follow, looking back at her once — the application approved.

"Wait," Jun called. "The cans. Do you want your—"

"Cans are yours. The bin is sacred." The barn door slid shut behind him, and that was the end of the audience.

In the station wagon, with the antenna laid along their laps like a ceremonial lance and the heater going, nobody said much for the first mile. Then Theo said, "The Root Beer Special," and Priya said, "He said don't name it till it hears," and Jun said, "It *heard*," and that was that.

---

Tuesday came in cold and clear, and by 6:45 the sky over Westridge High was the deep, even gray of a sidewalk after rain, with the first few stars finding their places. Mr. Abernathy, the custodian, met them at the roof-access door with a key ring the size of a fist and the air of a man granting passage to a tomb.

"Walk pads only," he said, clanging the door open. "Stay off the edge gravel. You go up in pairs, you come down in pairs, and you don't touch the vents, the conduits, or anything that's humming. I'll come lock up behind you at eight." He looked at the broomstick in Jun's hands, the cans glinting under the stairwell light. "That for the play?"

"Yes," said Theo, at the same moment Jun said, "It's an antenna."

Mr. Abernathy considered this. "Huh," he said, and let them up.

Si was already on the roof — he'd driven over in a pickup with an antenna on every corner and plates that read W7JUNK — standing at the parapet with Mr. O like two gargoyles in jackets. The Root Beer Special went up in the middle of the roof walk, broomstick sunk into a five-gallon bucket Theo had liberated from the theater department and filled with long-jump-pit sand, and it stood vertical and gleaming and faintly ridiculous: four cans to the sky, four to the ground, the little coil of the choke hanging at its middle like a medal.

Jun connected the coax to the FT-60R through the adapter. "Rules," said Si, before anyone touched anything else. "Five watts from a handheld won't hurt anybody, but nobody hugs an antenna while it's transmitting. Jun holds the radio. I hold the license — my call, my responsibility, my thumb near the button. The rest of you listen, and for once in your lives you'll hear a whole town you didn't know was talking."

Seven o'clock arrived. First, as a baseline, Jun screwed the rubber duck back on and held the radio up. The net control's voice came through the way it always had — there, gone, there, buried in hissing fits, a man shouting politely from inside a washing machine. Then she swapped to the coax from the cans, and turned the volume up.

*"—calling the Maple Run Tuesday net, this is Dale, K7NDT, Kilo Seven November Delta Tango, net control. This net meets every Tuesday at nineteen hundred local on the W7MR repeater. All stations welcome—"*

Clear as a bell. Not *better* — *clear*, full and steady and hissless, the way a voice sounds across a dinner table. "Full quieting," Mr. O murmured, which he explained was repeater talk for *so strong the static gives up entirely*. Around Jun, in the darkening cold, the others went still. Theo wasn't recording. Even Theo.

The net unspooled. A man checked in mobile from the highway, phonetics crisp — *Whiskey Seven...* — heading home from a shift. A woman at the fairgrounds reported the weekend's storm damage was all cleared from the repeater site. Dale the net control worked through a dozen calls with the unhurried rhythm of a man dealing cards he'd dealt a thousand times, paused for the swap-and-shop — somebody had a rotator, somebody else wanted a rotator, a marriage of convenience arranged in thirty seconds over a hilltop — and then the repeater beeped its little courtesy beep, and Dale said the words Si had plainly been waiting for:

*"Any late check-ins? Any visitors or stations with traffic for the net? Come on in."*

Si looked at Jun. Jun's heart was going like the hole saw. Si lifted the radio from her hands — gently, a loan, not a taking — keyed it, and spoke in a voice that was somehow exactly his own and also brand-new.

"K7NDT, this is W7JUNK — Whiskey Seven Juliett Uniform November Kilo — Si Webb, checking in from the Westridge High roof, with one piece of third-party traffic."

The repeater beeped, and Dale came back, and you could hear the grin in it. *"W7JUNK, well, I'll be — the ghost of Larkspur Road. Good to hear you on the machine, Si. Go ahead with your traffic."*

"Dale, I've got a young lady up here named Jun," Si said. "She and her club built the two-meter dipole I'm talking to you on — eight root beer cans and a broomstick, SWR one point eight, built with their own hands Saturday. She's hearing you full quieting on it, which I expect you can tell from her end. She's never spoken on the air in her life, and she'd like to say hello. Control operator present, and I'll keep it short." He released the button and held the radio out to Jun. She took it with both hands, and he put his thumb near the transmit key. "Just your name," he said quietly. "Phonetics if you can. The rest says itself."

The cold roof. The gray sky. Three faces watching — Priya, Theo, Bea — and Mr. O's hand steady on her shoulder. Si's thumb came down on the button, and the little red light glowed, and Jun Alvarez leaned toward her grandfather's radio and said the first words of her life into the sky.

"Hi — um. Hello, net." Her voice cracked, and she didn't care, or cared and went on anyway, which is the same thing but braver. "My name is Jun — Juliett Uniform November. We built the antenna on Saturday. It used to be root beer cans, and a broom that died. This is my first time ever talking on a radio." She ran out of words, and added, honestly, "It's really weird that you can hear me."

Si let the button up. The repeater beeped. And the hill answered.

*"Jun, welcome to the air, welcome to the net — K7NDT, and I have to tell you, in twenty years of net control that is the finest first transmission I have ever heard. Eight root beer cans! You're among friends here. Come back any Tuesday. Seventy-three."* The beep, and then another station, quickly recognized: *"Welcome, Jun — W7JUNK's crew is always trouble, but it's the good kind. 73."* And the net rolled on to the next check-in, the way a river rolls past a stone thrown into it, and the stone was hers, and it was in the river forever now.

Si keyed once more. "K7NDT, W7JUNK — obliged, Dale. The kid's hooked; you've done your damage. Seventy-three, clear." The beep came back, warm as a porch light, and a minute later the repeater spelled its own name in Morse again, dit-dah-dah, dah-dah-dit-dit-dit, patient as ever, saying *mine, mine* to nobody in particular.

Jun stood on the roof of her school in the cold and could not stop smiling. Her hands were shaking. Her name had gone up eight soda cans and come back off a hilltop twenty miles away, and somewhere in the static of the universe there was a record of it now — the river had it. She thought about the radio in her hands, her abuelo's radio, his thumb where hers was, decades of his words carried out of a little room in Tucson. She wondered whose voices it had held before it held hers.

"Oh," she said, to nobody, and it came out very small. "I get it now."

---

They came down off the roof in pairs, as instructed, the antenna riding Theo's shoulder like a standard at the head of a very small parade. In the parking lot, while the others loaded into Mr. O's wagon, Jun lingered by Si's pickup as he stowed the bucket of sand in the bed.

Overhead the sky had gone its deep October gray-black, the last of the dusk draining out of the west, the stars shaking out one by one above the practice-field lights. Si caught her looking.

"That repeater's a machine on a hill," he said, slamming the tailgate. "Good trick. But it's a *local* trick — twenty miles, line of sight, a robot doing the heavy lifting." He leaned on the truck and looked up, the sodium lights catching his beard. "Wait till you hear what the *sky* does. For that, you need a wire."

Then he was gone, W7JUNK grumbling away into the dark, and Jun stood a moment longer in the cold with her abuelo's radio warm in her jacket pocket, looking up at the gray sky and the first stars, thinking about the Kenwood on her desk and its five green bands waiting for winter.

*A wire,* she thought.
