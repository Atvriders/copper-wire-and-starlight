## Chapter 7: The J-Pole and the Storm

The Tuesday night net came up out of the FT-60R like a voice from the bottom of a laundry hamper, and June Alvarez stood on a chair with one arm out the storeroom window, holding the coax coupling above her head, because it helped — a little — for reasons nobody in Room 114-B wanted to examine too closely.

It was the first Tuesday of March, and the net was doing roll call. Thirty-odd check-ins, the regulars, the man with the perpetual cold, the woman who ran the swap-and-shop segment with an iron gavel — all of them arriving through the little speaker wrapped in static, like gifts in too much tissue paper. Outside, somewhere over the practice field in the dark, the coax ran up the wall to the roof, where the pop-can dipole had spent the winter the way a broomstick spends the winter: outdoors, uncomplaining, and slowly coming apart.

"Say again the last one," Theo said from his sprawl across two chairs. "That was either a call sign or somebody opening a bag of chips."

"It was W7JUNK," Jun said. She knew Si's gravel anywhere, even drowned. "Checking in from Larkspur Road. He's fine. The repeater hears him great. *We're* the ones who can't hear."

"Put the coax down, Jun," Priya said. She was at the whiteboard, but she wasn't working a problem — she was updating a graph, which Jun had learned was worse. Priya's graphs were where fun went to be measured. "You're just proving my point with modern dance." She capped the marker and stood back. "Standing-wave ratio of the pop-can dipole, measured every Saturday since October. One point eight when we built it. One point nine at Christmas. Two point four through February." The line bent upward like a ski jump. "Three point one as of last weekend. It is no longer an antenna. It is a wind chime that receives."

Jun climbed down off the chair. "It's corroding, isn't it. At the can joints."

"Aluminum oxidizes," Mr. O said from the operating desk, where he was grading lab reports under the gooseneck lamp. He didn't look up. "And aluminum oxide is an insulator — thin, stubborn, and very good at its job. Your can-to-can connections were mechanical pressure to begin with. Winter has been doing chemistry on them since November."

"So we take it down," Theo said, sitting up. "We clean the cans, we re-solder, we—"

"We stop patching a broomstick," said a voice from the doorway, "and build a grown-up antenna."

Si Webb stood in the door of Room 114-B with ten feet of copper pipe resting on one shoulder, held at the balance point like a hunter's rifle. He wore his canvas jacket and the expression of a man who had been invited somewhere and intended to make everyone pay for it. Behind Jun, Mr. O set down his red pen with the innocence of a man denying everything.

"You called him," Jun said.

"I made a phone call," said Mr. O. "Phones are not yet banned by the school board."

"Brought the dog," Si said. "He's in the truck with the windows cracked, sulking. He wanted to see the Tesla coil." He swung the pipe off his shoulder and stood it on its end in the middle of the room, where it rang faintly against the linoleum, a low bronze note. The copper was the color of a new penny where it had been cut and old brown pennies everywhere else, and it was, Jun thought, the single most beautiful object ever carried through that door, including the radio.

"Half-inch rigid copper," Si said. "Plumber's stock. A man owed me a favor, and you four are the favor, which he will never understand." He let go of the pipe and it stood there, barely swaying. "J-pole. That's the prescription. Two meters, vertical, all-weather, no radials, and you can build it in an afternoon with a torch and the truth. It's been the right answer since before you were born, and it'll be the right answer after I'm a silent key, which is the closest thing to immortality plumbing gets."

---

They cleared the long desk, and Priya took the whiteboard with the grim joy of a woman who had been waiting all winter for a real problem.

"Constants," she said. "Give me the constants."

"Long leg first," Si said, dropping into a lab stool. "Seven hundred five, divided by the frequency in megahertz. Answer comes out in feet."

"Which frequency?" Priya asked. "The band is four megahertz wide."

"The repeater," Mr. O said. "W7MR/R, one forty-six point seven six. That's the machine this antenna will live on."

Priya wrote it out. *705 ÷ 146.76.* Her pencil moved. "Four point eight oh four feet." A pause. "Fifty-seven point six inches."

"Mark fifty-seven and a half," Si said.

Priya turned around. "That's rounding in the wrong direction."

"That's rounding to the tape measure," Si said. "The last eighth of an inch is what the analyzer's for. You'll meet the analyzer Saturday and you'll see what I mean."

"The stub," Si went on, "is a quarter-wave. Two thirty-four over the frequency."

"One point five nine feet," Priya said, and then, before anyone could speak: "Nineteen inches." She looked at the two numbers on the board, and Jun watched her best friend's eyes go narrow and bright. "Wait. Two thirty-four. That's the dipole constant. Half of four sixty-eight."

"Same quarter-wave," Si said. "A quarter-wave is a quarter-wave, whether it's wire, soda cans, or pipe."

"And the long leg is *not* a half-wave," Priya said, working it out with the eraser end of the marker tapping her palm. "A full wavelength at one forty-six is about eighty inches. Fifty-seven and a half is barely seventy percent of that — a three-quarter wave with the usual shortening. The plans all call this an end-fed half-wave, and the pipe is three-quarters of a wavelength long. Somebody is lying."

"Nobody's lying," Si said. "The pipe is three-quarters. The *antenna* is a half. The bottom quarter-wave of the long leg works with the stub — the two of them carry the same current in opposite directions, so they cancel, mostly, and don't radiate. They're the gearbox. The top half-wave of the long leg is the part that talks. Builders named it for the part that talks." He shrugged. "People name trucks for the engine. Same idea."

"Spacing between the two pipes," Si said, holding up a finger and thumb, "an inch and three-quarters, center to center, and it's not fussy. Feed point — where the coax taps on — starts two, three inches above the crossbar, and you slide it till the analyzer smiles. Then you solder it. Then you coil four turns of the coax, five inches across, right at the feedpoint, so the feedline doesn't decide it's part of the antenna. You've done that trick three times already."

"The yagi choke," Bea said. It was the first sound she'd made all evening, from her seat in the second row, hands folded. She'd been watching the pipe the whole time, the way she watched everything — like a judge who already knew the verdict and was checking the arithmetic.

"The yagi choke," Si agreed, and pointed at her with approval. "Four turns this time, not seven. Different band, different recipe."

Jun raised her hand halfway, then put it down, because she was tired of being the one who asked and the answer was already forming in her head. "Why a vertical at all? The dipoles all lie down."

"Ask the room," Mr. O said, from behind his lab reports.

"Because repeaters stand up," Theo said. "Car whips stand up. The rubber duck on your HT stands up. Two-meter FM is a standing-up hobby." He spread his hands. "If your antenna lies down horizontal and the other guy's stands up, the signals arrive cross-eyed. You lose them in the mismatch. Vertical talks to vertical."

"And a plain vertical," Priya added, "needs a ground plane. A quarter-wave whip is only half an antenna — the other half is the car roof, or radials fanning out like skirt ribs." She looked at the pipe, and Jun could see the shape assembling behind her eyes. "But the J brings its own other half. The stub *is* the matching section. That's why it can go up on a bare roof with nothing under it but a stick."

"Now she's got it," Si said to the ceiling.

"The roof," Mr. O said, and finally looked up from his grading. "Which is the part of this plan that involves the school district. I have spoken to Ms. Delgado. She is prepared to allow a permanent antenna on the flat roof, in writing, with conditions." He counted them on his fingers. "One: Mr. Abernathy is present for every minute anyone is on the roof. Two: nothing is screwed, bolted, nailed, or glued to the roof itself. Three: the antenna is disconnected in threatening weather, every time, no exceptions, and she used the word *threatening*, which I note is doing a lot of work in that sentence." He took off his reading glasses. "I told her those were already our rules. She said she knew; she'd been reading our logbook. I did not ask how."

"She knows everything eleven minutes early," Jun said. "It's her whole thing."

The list went on the whiteboard, because a build wanted a list:

- 10 ft of 1/2-inch copper pipe (Si's plumber debt — free)
- one 1/2-inch copper tee, one elbow, two end caps, one short nipple (hardware store, $5.80 with tax — the year's big spend)
- solder, flux, emery cloth (Si's barn)
- pipe cutter, propane torch (Si's barn)
- RG-58 coax, one long run, roof to windowsill (Si's barn spool)
- hose clamps, two, for the mast (Si's barn coffee can of hose clamps)
- a mount. Source: unknown. Status: Jun's problem.

"A mount," Theo read. "What kind of mount?"

"Tripod," Si said. "The kind they used to sell for TV antennas, back when every roof in America had a skeleton on it. Three legs, a mast socket, made to bolt down — except you'll bolt nothing, you'll pile blocks on the feet, because Mr. Abernathy loves that roof like a child." He picked his jacket off the door hook. "Don't buy one new. The country's full of them. They're in alleys and estate sales and the backs of garages, galvanized, ugly, outliving their owners. Find one."

Jun was already writing in her salvage log, her pencil going fast. *Entry 287: TV roof tripod, galvanized, three legs. Source: TBD. Condition: TBD. Potential: hold up the sky.*

"Saturday," Si said, from the doorway. "My barn, eight o'clock. Bring the fittings, bring the math, and bring your patience, because copper's honest. It can smell a shortcut, and it will tell on you."

He was gone before anyone could thank him, which was exactly the way he preferred it.

---

The Webb barn at eight on a March Saturday was forty degrees of sawdust, cold iron, and mouse, and Jun loved it the way other people loved churches.

The kerosene heater was going tick-tick in the corner, throwing a dented orange circle of warmth that reached the workbench and no farther. Resistor met them at the truck, shook himself all over like a rug being beaten, and escorted them to the barn door with the solemnity of a very old bellhop. On the bench: the pipe cutter, the torch, a coil of solder thick as a pencil lead, and a can of flux with a brush welded to its lid by ancient drips. Si poured four mugs of coffee that tasted like it had been drained out of a tractor, set a fifth on the sill for himself, and pointed at the pipe.

"Bench is yours," he said, and sat down on an upturned bucket, which was where he stayed. Si had a genius for sitting. He could sit at you until you figured it out.

Priya ran the tape. Bea held the pencil. "Fifty-seven and a half," Priya said, and Bea made the mark, small and exact, and then Priya ran the tape a second time from the other end of the pipe, because measuring twice was the whole religion. "Still fifty-seven and a half. Cut."

The pipe cutter was a little C-clamp of a thing with a wheel in its jaw, and Jun worked it the way Si had shown the club back in February — three turns, tighten the screw a quarter, three more turns — walking the wheel around the mark until the copper gave up with a soft crystalline *tick* and the long leg came off in her hands. The cut end flared inward, ragged as a biscuit edge.

"Ream it," Si said from his bucket.

She reamed it with the little blade folded into the cutter's body, then worked the outside over with emery cloth until the end shone. The nineteen-inch stub came off the same way, and then the fittings: a tee for the bottom of the stub, an elbow for the bottom of the long leg, and between them a thumb-length nipple that would hold the two pipes parallel, an inch and three-quarters apart, center to center. Si produced the spacer for that — a hardwood block planed to exactly an inch and three-quarters, with J-POLE written on it in pencil so old the graphite had gone silver. He'd made it, he said, for a lookout tower on a mountain with no name worth remembering. The block smelled like cedar.

They dry-fit the whole thing standing up against the bench: long leg, elbow, nipple, tee, stub, and an end cap on top of each pipe. It looked like a letter from an alphabet for giants. It looked, Jun thought, like the letter J the way a treble clef looks like music — exactly, and with flair.

"Now solder," Si said. "Clean, flux, heat, flow. In that order. Who's first?"

Jun was first, because she had soldered a hundred circuit boards and said so, and because she was sixteen and had not yet learned that confidence is a thing copper eats.

She gave the elbow a cursory wipe with the emery cloth, skipped the flux because the joint looked clean, and put the torch to the solder, holding the blue flame on the wire and poking the joint hopefully. The solder balled up, trembled, and rolled off the copper like rain off a waxed truck, leaving a gray tear on the bench. She tried again. The second ball joined the first. The copper sat there, brown and smug, exactly as joined as it had been in the hardware store.

"Interesting," Priya said.

"It's not wetting," Jun said. "The solder's bad."

"The solder's fine," Si said. He did not get up. "What'd you skip?"

Jun looked at the joint. Looked at the emery cloth. Looked at the flux can with its fossilized brush. "Everything," she admitted. "I skipped everything."

"Copper's got a skin," Si said, still on the bucket. "Tarnish. You can't see half of it, but the solder can feel it, and solder will not marry a stranger. Solder wants bright metal, clean flux, and the right heat, and it wants them in that order, every time, since the Romans. It's not a material. It's a contract."

So she did it the way the contract said. She shined the inside of the elbow and the last inch of the long leg with emery cloth until both were pink-bright as new pennies. She painted flux on with the stiff brush, a thin shining coat. She assembled the joint, put the torch flame on the *fitting* — the heavy part, the part with the appetite — and waited, watching the flux go from white to clear, and only then touched the solder to the seam opposite the flame.

The joint drank it. That was the only way to describe it — the solder went liquid and ran around the seam in one smooth silver motion, wicked in by the heat, and when she pulled the wire away there was a bright fillet all the way around, smooth as poured glass.

"Oh," she said.

"There it is," said Si. "You don't solder copper. You invite it."

They did the rest in rotation, because everyone wanted an invitation. Theo soldered the tee to the stub with his headphones around his neck, narrating under his breath into his phone for a *Marsh Hour* segment he was already calling *The Copper Cactus* — "and that's the name of the antenna, folks, you're hearing it here first" — and nobody had the energy to stop him, and the name stuck before the joint cooled. Priya soldered the nipple into both fittings with the block clamped between the pipes to hold the spacing, frowning at the flame like it had talked back. Bea did the end caps, small and neat, her two silver fillets the best of the day, and set them on the tops of the pipes like hats.

"Why cap the stub?" she asked.

"Wasps," Si said. "Mud daubers. They find an open pipe in April, and by July your antenna's full of real estate and detuned by insects. Cap everything. Copper weatherproofs itself — it goes brown, then one year it goes green, like the old courthouse roof, and the green is armor. This thing will outlive your grandchildren's opinions."

When the last joint had cooled they stood it up against the bench, all five feet of it, and Jun walked a slow circle. The solder lines caught the heater light. Six months ago an antenna had been a concept; since October it had been soda cans, an extension cord, a tape measure, two Slinkies, and a loop disguised as a plant stand. This was the first one that looked like it had a job.

"Choke," Si said, and tossed Jun the end of the RG-58. "Four turns, five inches across, right where the feed taps on. You've met the idea."

They wound it around an oatmeal canister from the barn kitchen — five inches across, close enough to the recipe that nobody filed a complaint — four neat turns, zip-tied in a coil like a wreath, a foot below where the coax would tap the pipes. The free end of the coax, Si had already prepped: the jacket stripped back six inches, the braid twisted into a pigtail, the center conductor tinned, each pigtail ending in a small copper lug with a hole in it.

"Those clamp to the pipes," Si said, "and they slide, because tomorrow you tune. Not today. Today the solder's young and the sun's leaving."

"Tomorrow?" Theo said.

"Bench'll be here," said Si, pouring the dregs of his coffee onto the floor, where Resistor inspected them and declined. "Antenna keeps. Bring your analyzer manners."

---

Sunday was colder, with a flat gray sky like a lid, and the Copper Cactus went up on the wooden sawhorse in the middle of the barn aisle, lashed upright with twine, well clear of the tool racks and the truck.

"Metal near an antenna is a liar," Si said, setting his analyzer on an upturned crate. "It talks back. Keep the world away from it while you measure, or you're tuning the world." The analyzer was the size of a paperback, with a screen, a knob, and a coax socket. He plugged the Cactus in, lugs first.

The lugs clamped one to each pipe, side by side, and they were, at Si's instruction, starting high — four inches above the crossbar. Theo had lobbied for higher still, on the grounds that more was more. Si had let the four stand, because four was wrong in an instructive direction.

"Read it," he said.

Priya crouched at the crate. "SWR at one forty-six point seven six..." She turned the knob slowly, watching the screen. "Three point oh to one."

"Three," Jun said. "That's wind-chime territory. That's pop-can-in-February numbers."

"That's a mismatched antenna," Si said. "Nothing's broken. Nothing's even wrong yet. So — what are we sliding, and why does sliding fix it?" He looked around the barn aisle the way Mr. O looked around a classroom, and waited.

It was Priya who got it, of course, but she got there by talking, the way she did her best thinking. "The stub is a quarter-wavelength of transmission line, shorted at the bottom. That's all it is — two parallel pipes, shorted together at the crossbar. And a shorted quarter-wave line is a transformer." Her eyes went up the stub, following her own arithmetic. "At the bottom, at the short, the impedance is zero — a dead short. At the top, where the stub ends and the radiator begins, it's enormous — thousands of ohms. So somewhere in between, it passes through fifty ohms, which is what the coax wants."

"Every value," Si said. "It passes through every value there is, between nothing and everything. Your job is to find where it keeps the fifty."

"So we're not tuning the antenna," Jun said. "We're asking the antenna where it wants to be plugged in."

Si pointed at her with the hand that held the coffee. "Write that on the barn wall," he said. "Don't actually write it. Delgado's not in charge here, but *I* am."

They lowered the lugs half an inch at a time, Bea moving them together, side by side, with her jeweler's care, and Priya read the numbers out like a croupier. Three and a half inches: two point two. Three inches: one point seven. Two and three-quarters: one point five, and Jun felt the room lean in, all four of them watching a paperback-sized box like it was about to speak. Two and a half: Priya turned the knob back and forth across the repeater frequency, and then smiled the smile she pretended not to have.

"One point two to one," she said. "At one forty-six point seven six exactly." She swept the knob up and down the band. "Under one point five from one forty-four to one forty-eight. The whole two-meter band. It's flat. It's a *good* antenna, it's not even on anything."

"Write the height down," Si said. "Two and a half inches above the crossbar. Every J-pole keeps its fifty ohms in a slightly different pocket. You found yours." He handed Jun the torch. "Solder the lugs. You know the contract now."

She did. Bright metal, thin flux, heat on the heavy part, and the lugs went solid to the pipes at two and a half inches, permanent as a signature. Theo wrapped the joint in tape with the tenderness of a man wrapping a microphone cable that had once saved his life, and Bea coiled the choke neat against the mast line, and they stood back, all five of them, six counting Resistor, who had slept through the tuning but woken for the standing back.

There it was. Five feet of copper and solder and arithmetic, four turns of coax at its throat, standing in a barn aisle at the end of Larkspur Road with its whole career ahead of it.

"Next stop," Mr. O said, who had arrived halfway through the numbers and watched the rest with his hands in his pockets, "is the roof."

---

The tripod had been waiting for Jun at Second Wind Thrift, which was where all roads led eventually.

It came in with the Holloway estate cleanout on Delaney Street — a galvanized three-legged TV-antenna tripod, the kind that used to hold up the great aluminum skeletons, when every roof in America wore one. Her mom priced it at six dollars and then slid it across the counter at Jun with the weary generosity of a woman raising a magpie. "For the school," Marisol said. "A donation. If the school asks, it cost forty." Jun logged it that night: *Entry 291. Rooftop tripod, galvanized, feet for lag screws we will not be using. Source: Holloway estate, via Mom. Condition: will outlive us all. Potential: see Entry 287.*

Mr. Abernathy met them at the roof hatch at three-thirty on Wednesday with an extension ladder, a canvas tool roll, and the air of a man who had been waiting twenty years for somebody to ask him about the roof. He had opinions about the roof. The roof was thirty-pound felt over decking, it did not like being walked on in cold weather, and it especially did not like teenagers, present company conditionally excepted. He had read Ms. Delgado's letter — one paragraph, three conditions, a signature like a paper cutter — and he recited the middle condition from memory.

"Nothing screwed to my roof," he said.

"Nothing screwed to your roof," Mr. O agreed. "Ballast only. Blocks on the feet."

"And the other thing," Mr. Abernathy said. "Before anybody climbs. Where are the wires?"

That was the rule, stated as action, the way Si had taught them in December and Mr. O had made them recite in January: before an antenna goes up, you find every power line in the county, and then you put twice the antenna's length between it and the nearest one, so that if the whole works falls over in the worst wind of the year, it cannot reach the wires even falling. The school's service drop came down the east wall to a transformer on a pole by the gym, and the four of them walked the parapet and sighted it, and then did the arithmetic out loud, because Mr. Abernathy wanted it out loud.

"Antenna's five feet," Priya said. "Mast above the tripod, four. Tripod legs, two. Call the whole works eleven feet from the gravel to the top cap. Twice that is twenty-two."

"We're putting it on the west corner," Mr. O said. "Pace it."

Theo paced it, heel to toe, theatrical, a surveyor in a school hoodie. "Thirty-one," he announced. "Thirty-one feet to the drop, give or take my shoes."

"Give or take your shoes," Mr. Abernathy repeated, and the corner of his mouth moved. "West corner's accepted."

So: Jun held the ladder's foot and Bea held the rails, because nobody climbed alone and nobody carried a load up a ladder — that was the rule, and the rule had a rhythm, and the rhythm was: one climbs, one spots, one hauls the rope. The tripod went up in pieces on Abernathy's line, then four cinder blocks, then the rubber stall mat he'd cut from a maintenance-shop scrap to go under the feet, because felt roofs remember every insult. The mast was a length of galvanized fence top-rail from Si's rack. The Copper Cactus went up last, wrapped in a moving blanket like a cello, and Jun hauled the line hand over hand while Theo walked it out from the wall, and she felt the exact moment the weight came off the ground and became her responsibility.

They clamped the J-pole to the mast with the two hose clamps, plumbed it with Abernathy's torpedo level until the bubble sat dead center, cinched everything down, and piled the blocks on the tripod feet like they were building a tiny fort. Then the coax — the new long run of RG-58 — down over the parapet on the same route the old coax had worn, in through the windowsill feedthrough in 114-B, where for forty years every wire this club had ever loved had come in out of the weather.

The pop-can dipole came down the same afternoon. It was the right thing and it felt like a funeral anyway. The broomstick had gone gray, and two cans had loosened and turned in the wind until they'd worn bright flats on themselves, and Jun carried it down the ladder one-handed while Bea steadied it from below, like an honor guard. In the storeroom, Bea wiped each can with a rag, collapsed the stacks, and packed the whole thing — cans, stick, coax, choke — into a five-gallon bucket from the maintenance shop, which she labeled in her small exact hand: *GO-KIT — DO NOT THROW AWAY.*

"You'll see it again," Mr. O said, watching her press the lid on. "Antennas like that don't retire. They go into the reserve."

Then, before anyone was allowed near the radio, came the drill.

Mr. O screwed the coax onto the back of the little station radio, looked around at them, and unscrewed it again. "That," he said, "is the whole discipline. First thunder you hear — not the first lightning you *see*, the first thunder you *hear* — the coax comes off the radio. Not the power switch. The coax. The antenna is disconnected and stays disconnected until the storm is past, and nobody transmits on anything, and we do not negotiate with the sky about it. The antenna works for us; we do not die for the antenna." He handed the coupling to Jun. "Your turn. Do it. Again. Again." They each did it three times — Jun, Priya, Theo, Bea — until the motion stopped being a thought and started being a hand. "It's not a ritual," Mr. O said, "until your hands know it."

"What about Si?" Theo asked. "His farm's got six masts. He's a lightning rod with a mortgage."

"Si's station is grounded to the center of the earth," Mr. O said, "rods, panels, the whole religion, and even Si disconnects and goes quiet when the cell is overhead. You've heard him do it. When thunder gets close, the kids observe and log, and the grown station — the grounded one — does any talking that needs doing. That's the arrangement. It has been the arrangement since Ben Franklin flew a kite and got lucky."

Only then did he screw the coax back on, pick up Jun's FT-60R and its adapter — five watts from a handheld, which was to RF what a desk lamp was to fire — and thumb the PTT with the repeater's offset and tone long since programmed in.

"W7JUNK, W7JUNK, this is N8OWL, November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, radio check on the new machine."

The reply came back in less than a second, and it did not come from the bottom of a laundry hamper. It came from the middle of the room, clear as a man at the next desk.

"N8OWL, W7JUNK. Full quieting, David. Armchair copy. I take it the pipe's standing."

"The pipe is standing, Silas. The kids built it. First transmission on it, and you're the witness."

"Then the record should show it hears as good as it talks," Si said, "and that the coffee at the barn remains terrible. W7JUNK clear. Seventy-three."

"Seventy-three," Mr. O said, and set the radio down, and for a moment nobody spoke — they just looked at the windowsill, where the coax came in out of the March wind, carrying a whole river of sky on a wire thinner than a pencil.

Outside, over the practice field, the sky had gone the color of tin. The forecast for Friday had a kink in it. They all knew; they'd all checked; it was March, and March was like that.

---

Friday arrived wearing the wrong sky.

By three o'clock the light over Westridge High had gone the color of a bruise, green-gray and flat, and the flag on the pole was snapping from the southwest in gusts that came like shoves. The kink in the forecast had become a squall line — a solid wall of weather on the radar, fifty miles long, walking east with the whole county in front of it. Mr. O's tie was Orion, for the last of the winter sky. Under the circumstances it looked less like a constellation and more like a man with his club raised.

The club was in 114-B because there was nowhere else on earth they would have been — Mr. O had decreed that tonight they were an audience, not a cast — and at ten past three, the repeater changed its voice.

"This is W7FCN, Whiskey Seven Foxtrot Charlie November, activating the Fairview County Skywarn net. The National Weather Service has issued a severe thunderstorm warning for Fairview County. This is now a directed net — check-ins from trained spotters only, and all other stations please stand by. Reports wanted: hail of any size, damaging wind, flooding, and any rotation, wall cloud, or funnel. This is W7FCN, net control."

And just like that, the machine woke up.

They listened on the Copper Cactus, on the little station radio, and the difference from Tuesday night was not subtle — it was the difference between a screen door and a picture window. Stations checked in by twos and threes, call sign, name, location, and the net control sorted them the way a librarian sorts returns, never hurried, never lost. Reports came from the west county first: pea-size hail at the county line. Dime-size on the state road. A gust that took a barn door, reported with the flat precision of a man describing a measurement. Every report got a read-back, a time, and a thank-you, and somewhere two counties north, net control said, each one was being relayed to the Weather Service — a voice on a repeater becoming a data point on a meteorologist's screen.

Bea wrote down every line, in her fair exact hand: time, call, place, report. Priya watched her do it and then said, very quietly, "It's a distributed system. With human nodes." From Priya, this was a cathedral of a compliment.

"W7JUNK," came a voice they knew down to the gravel, "Whiskey Seven Juliett Uniform November Kilo. Silas. Larkspur Road, grounded station. Dime-size hail, ended. Wind southwest, hard enough to take the temper out of my barn door. No damage. No rotation. Back to listening."

"That's us next," Jun said. Her eyes were on the window. The bruise-light had deepened, and she could see the rain coming across the practice field like a curtain being drawn down its track.

All four phones buzzed at once — the warning alert with the siren built in. Theo looked at his screen and then at the radio, and said the thing all of them were thinking: "The phone knows there's a warning. The net knows what the storm is *doing*." He said it without a single flourish, which for Theo was a kind of reverence. "That man is running a board," he added, nodding at the radio. "That's not chatter. That's a broadcast operation."

Then, at three-forty-one by Bea's log, the net stopped being about the west county.

"Net control, N7SPQ, November Seven Sierra Papa Quebec. Priority report." The voice was calm and not calm at once, the way a voice gets when the person is making it behave. "Spotter on the county road, two miles south of the water tower off Delaney. I have a funnel cloud, three-quarters of the way to the ground. No ground contact. Repeat, no ground contact. Moving northeast at thirty."

The room did not gasp. It did something quieter than gasping — it went still, because the water tower off Delaney was ten blocks from Second Wind Thrift, and their side of town had just become a sentence on the radio.

"N7SPQ, W7FCN. Solid copy. Funnel cloud, no ground contact, two miles south of the Delaney water tower, moving northeast at thirty. Relaying to the Weather Service now. All stations, this is a tornado situation for the central part of the county — report any lowering, any rotation, any debris. Stand by."

Jun's thumb was already moving. *Mom. Basement. Now.* The reply came back in under a minute, three words and a photograph of the shop's basement stairs, which was her mother's whole heart in one picture: *Already here.*

And then thunder — the first real thunder, close enough to feel in the floor tile — and Bea's hand was on the coax before the sound finished arriving. Off the radio, unscrewed, laid down. Nobody had told her. Her hands knew it.

"Hallway," Mr. O said, mildly, as if suggesting tea. "Away from the window. Bring the logbook."

They sat in the interior hallway on the floor, backs against lockers, while the town sirens wound up outside and the rain hit the building like gravel off a truck. Jun's FT-60R, on its own rubber duck, no rooftop anything, still pulled the net out of the static in fragments — the lightning crashes came through it like handfuls of sand thrown at a drum — and they listened there on the floor, in the dim safety light, while Bea kept logging by the glow of the little screen. *3:47 — N7SPQ: funnel lifted, no contact, repeat no contact. 3:49 — NCS: rotation weakening south of town. 3:52 — wind report, downtown, limbs down.*

Twenty minutes, it took. The rain went from gravel to marbles to rain again. The sirens wound down. Somewhere east of them the line went on about its business, dropping its reports on other towns, other nets, other people on hallway floors.

They came back into 114-B at four-fifteen, into a room full of rinsed gold light, and Mr. O screwed the coax back on in time to hear net control stand the net down to normal operation and thank the spotters by name — a long roll of call signs, read slow, like the credits of a film. W7JUNK came last.

"Clear here on Larkspur," Si said. "Everything still nailed down that was nailed down. Obliged to the net. This is what the machine is *for*, folks — every Tuesday night of it was practice for tonight. W7JUNK, clear. Seventy-three."

Theo let out a breath like a man leaving a theater. Priya was reading Bea's log over her shoulder, two pages of fair handwriting, and she said, "If the internet had died tonight, this notebook would still know exactly what happened." Jun said nothing. She was looking at the windowsill, where the coax came in out of the clean-washed light, and she was thinking that all winter she had loved this hobby for the far away — Japan at sunset, Texas at lunch — and it had taken a funnel cloud on her mother's street to show her the *near*. The repeater on the hill wasn't a toy that reached other states. It was the town, talking to itself, with its eyes open.

---

Mr. Abernathy walked them up to the roof at four-thirty, because the rule said after the storm you check, and because he wanted to see his roof more than he wanted to see the antenna. The gravel was washed dark and shining. The flag was still cracking, clean now, in a west wind that smelled like rain standing in fields. And the Copper Cactus stood in the middle of it, dripping, dead vertical, cinder blocks unmoved, with the whole rinsed sky going gold behind it.

"Held," Mr. Abernathy said, in the tone of a man granting the roof's official blessing.

They stood in a row and looked at it. Five feet of pipe and arithmetic, soldered by four kids in a cold barn, and on its first week on the job it had carried the one net that mattered most in the world.

Mr. O put his hands in his pockets. "People will tell you," he said, "that you build antennas for storms. You don't." He looked along the row of them, one by one, teacher to his marrow. "You don't build antennas *for* storms. You build them *before* storms. Today is why. There will be other Fridays."

Back in 114-B, Jun opened the salvage log and wrote the last entry of the week, pencil going slow, the radio murmuring the ordinary evening chatter on the shelf above her.

*Entry 296. One copper J-pole, 57½ + 19, soldered by us. Source: Si's plumber debt, Holloway tripod, $5.80 of fittings. Condition: standing. Potential: proven. It is not an antenna for talking to the sky. It is the town's ears, and we built it with our hands.*

Underneath it, Mr. O read over her shoulder and then reached past her and wrote, in his red grading pen, a single line on the storeroom calendar, on the fourth Tuesday of March: *after-school operating begins — N8OWL on the air from 114-B, supervised, 3:30.* He had been hoarding the permission slip from Ms. Delgado for a week, waiting, because he was a man who understood timing.

"Word will get around," he said pleasantly, recapping the pen. "It always does."
