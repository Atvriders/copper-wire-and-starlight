## Chapter 10: The Satellite Chase

"The dot," said Mr. O, "is doing seventeen thousand miles an hour. Who wants to talk to it?"

It was the first Monday of May, Room 114-B had its window open for the first time since October, and the dot in question was a whiteboard-marker speck the size of a pea, parked above a lopsided blue circle labeled EARTH. Mr. O capped the marker and let the speck hang there, the way he let all his best demonstrations hang.

"The International Space Station," he said. "Two hundred fifty miles up. That is closer to this room than Spokane is. It goes around the whole planet every ninety minutes — sixteen sunrises a day — and it has a ham radio on board. A good one. The astronauts use it to talk to schoolkids. When they're not using it, it spends its days retransmitting little packets of radio for anybody on Earth who asks nicely." He wrote on the board: *NA1SS.* "Its call sign. November Alfa One Sierra Sierra. It is, in every way that matters, a ham station with the best antenna height in the world."

"Or out of it," Priya said.

"Or out of it," Mr. O agreed.

Theo Marsh had covered space exactly once on the *Marsh Hour* — a forty-minute episode about the billionaire rocket companies, which he had titled *The Gated Community with Better PR* and which had gotten him briefly uninvited from the aerospace club's bake sale. His settled position was that space was a hobby for people who owned islands. He raised his hand halfway and didn't wait to be called on, which Mr. O had long since stopped fighting.

"You're telling me the space station has a *radio station*," Theo said. "And we can hear it. From here."

"From the parking lot, if you like," said Mr. O. "The downlink — that's the station's mouth, the frequency it talks on — is 145.800 megahertz. Two meters. The same band as our repeater. The same band as a certain antenna somebody built in December out of a tape measure." He looked around at them, four students in a room full of forty-year-old oscilloscopes, and delivered it the way he delivered voltage. "People hear the space station every day with exactly what this club already owns. Most people just never tune in."

Jun sat up. "The fox-hunt yagi."

"The fox-hunt yagi," said Mr. O. "Seven dB of gain, give or take, and you aim it with your arms. No tower, no ladder, no rotor. The safest antenna project you'll do all year, and the target is *in space*."

"Wait," Priya said. Her pencil was already moving — the tell, the sound of Priya Nair losing an argument on purpose. "If it's two hundred fifty miles up and moving that fast, we can't hear it for long. The window is however long it's above our horizon."

"About ten minutes," said Mr. O, "horizon to horizon, on a good pass. Which brings us to this." He held up his phone. On it, a pass-predictor app showed a tidy list of dates, times, and little arcs drawn over a map. "You don't chase a satellite. You look up when it's coming, and then you go stand outside and keep the appointment. Wednesday, nine twelve p.m., it comes up over the southwest — but that's a low pass, twenty-four degrees above the horizon at best. A drive-by. Saturday, May sixteenth, eight fifty-two p.m.: sixty-eight degrees of elevation, nearly overhead, ten full minutes. A parade."

"Can we talk back?" Bea asked.

It was the first thing she'd said all meeting, and it reorganized the room, the way her questions did. Mr. O nodded at her once.

"Two answers," he said. "Anybody may listen. No license, no permission, no form to sign — that's the law's gift to the curious. Transmitting is different. Transmitting is me: one call sign on the air, N8OWL, my license, my responsibility, and the four of you as my crew. Those are the rules, they are not negotiable, and they are also —" he allowed himself half a smile "— sufficient. The station's voice uplink — its ear — is 144.490. For packet radio you send on 145.825, and it repeats your packet back down on 145.800 for the whole continent to hear. Write those down. If you remember one thing today, remember which end is the mouth."

Theo was writing nothing down. Theo was seeing a vision. The vision had a crowd in it.

"Saturday the sixteenth," he said. "Eight fifty-two. People need to *know* about this. This is a morning-announcements event. This is a *Marsh Hour* live special — *Westridge Hears Space* — I can have a crowd in that parking lot like it's a home game—"

"You could," Jun said carefully, "hear it yourself first. *Then* invite the crowd."

"When have I ever needed a rehearsal?" Theo said.

"Wednesday," said Bea.

"What happened Wednesday?"

"Spring concert," said Bea. "You introduced the jazz band as the jazz *choir*."

"That was one time, and the program said—" Theo stopped, because everyone was smiling, including, faintly, Bea. "Fine. Noted. Wednesday is a soft launch. Small crowd. Saturday we go big."

"Define *small*," Jun said.

"Define *crowd*," said Theo.

Mr. O picked up the handset of the storeroom's one extension and dialed a number he knew by heart. "Before you book the stadium," he said, "you should hear from a man who has been doing this since the satellites were the size of beach balls."

Si Webb answered on the third ring, and Mr. O put him on speaker, and the storeroom filled with the sound of a barn — a dog barking once and thinking better of it, coffee being poured, a voice like a gravel driveway.

"Webb."

"Silas. I'm putting you on speaker with four kids who want to talk to the space station on a tape measure."

A pause, while Si decided not to pretend he hated this. "Done it with less," he said. "Listen here. A satellite is a bus that's never late and never stops. You don't chase it. You make an appointment and you keep it." A slurp of coffee. "Point like you mean it. Retune when the girl tells you. And when you hear it — and you will hear it — remember you're standing in a parking lot talking to a machine in orbit with a stick from the hardware store, because that part doesn't wear off. Forty years and it never wore off."

"Yes sir," Theo said, and meant it, which surprised him.

"Seventy-three, kids." The barn noise cut off.

"Seventy-three," the club chorused at the dead phone, ragged as a middle-school band, and Mr. O hung up and looked at the whiteboard speck one more time.

"Wednesday we listen," he said. "Quietly. And before Wednesday, we work — because the yagi in question has been living behind the Tesla coil since December, and I give it even odds somebody used it to point at something."

Everyone looked at Theo.

"It was *one* pencil," Theo said. "It was behind the radiator."

---

The yagi came out from behind the Tesla coil wearing a winter's worth of dust and one unmistakable crease.

"Oh no," Jun said.

It had been beautiful in December — three elements of one-inch steel measuring tape, bright as sword blades, hose-clamped to a cross made from a PVC boom and two tees. It had found a hidden transmitter in Fairview County Park in forty-one minutes, in light snow, and Theo had spent the whole hunt holding it like a documentary host, which had not, strictly, helped, but had felt right. Now the reflector — the long back element — had a kink near one end with a white stress-crease across the steel, one hose clamp had bloomed orange with rust, and the whole thing leaned on Priya's shoulder with the air of a retired athlete.

"The radiator incident," Theo said. "For the record, it was my lucky pencil."

Priya was already at the whiteboard, writing from memory, because of course Priya had the numbers in her head. "We re-true it before we trust it. Reflector, forty-one and three-eighths inches. Driven element, two pieces of seventeen and three-quarters, one-inch gap for the hairpin. Director, thirty-five and an eighth. Spacings: reflector to driven, eight inches; driven to director, twelve and a half." She capped the marker. "The tape doesn't know it's been retired for five months. The numbers don't retire."

They worked it over on the long desk under the window. Jun produced the repair parts from her backpack the way other people produced pens: a thrift-store tape measure from Second Wind's bin three, a dollar fifty, its spring long dead but its blade straight and bright. "One inch wide," she said, laying it alongside the kinked reflector. "Same as the original. Mom says hi."

They cut the new reflector to forty-one and three-eighths — measuring twice, Priya watching the tape the whole time like a customs inspector — and snapped the fresh blade into the hose clamps with that particular ring of steel tape that sounded like a fencing match starting. New clamp from Mr. O's coffee can of clamps. Spacings re-checked against the whiteboard, ruler held at arm's length. Then Mr. O brought the little antenna analyzer down from its shelf, clipped it to the coax, and swept the band.

Two point six to one.

"Not catastrophic," Mr. O said. "Not good. The log says one-five in December."

"One-five," Jun confirmed, from the salvage log.

"Then something is lying," Priya said, and they went hunting for the lie.

This was the part Theo had spent nine months learning not to dread — the part where the answer was always in the room somewhere, patient as a splinter, waiting to be found by hands instead of hope. Element lengths: true, all three, to the sixteenth. The choke: seven turns of coax coiled behind the reflector, wound there in December so the feedline wouldn't poison their fox-hunt bearings — still snug, still doing its quiet job. Connector: tight. They stood around the desk like doctors around an X-ray, and it was Bea who took the driven element off the boom and turned it in her small hands like a jeweler with a doubtful stone.

The driven element wasn't one piece but two — two blades of seventeen and three-quarter inches, their inner ends bent toward each other to make the little hairpin of the match — and where the gap between them should have stood at an honest inch, it had been squeezed nearly shut, a dime's width, like a book slammed on a finger.

"It got squashed," Bea said. "In storage."

"December's hairpin, May's buzzcut," said Mr. O, which was as close to a joke about impedance matching as the laws of physics permitted. "The match lives in that gap. Close the gap and you change what the radio sees."

They coaxed the hairpin open again, a millimeter at a time, Bea's fingertips against Priya's ruler, until the gap stood at its inch. Back on the boom. Analyzer on. The number settled at one point five to one at 145.800 and stayed there, smug and steady.

"Even odds," Theo said. "You gave even odds it was my fault."

"The pencil was you," said Mr. O. "The gap was entropy. Entropy doesn't need help."

Then there was the cable.

Listening was settled — Jun's FT-60R on the yagi's coax, the club's ears. But if they were going to *send* a packet on Saturday, somebody had to get the sound out of a computer and into a radio, and that was a Theo problem, because audio was the one corner of radio Theo had owned outright since before any of this started. He had built every cable the *Marsh Hour* had ever used. He had opinions about solder joints the way other people had opinions about pitchers.

The parts list, as he recited it to the salvage log while Jun wrote:

- One dead gaming headset (lost-and-found bin, unclaimed since October), for its four-conductor plug and six feet of good shielded wire.
- One sacrificed aux cable, three-and-a-half millimeters both ends, for the laptop side.
- One length of hookup wire, red, because the PTT line deserved ceremony.
- Heat-shrink tubing, borrowed from Mr. O's drawer with full disclosure and a signed receipt, because Mr. O ran a drawer, not a charity.

The plan: the laptop ran a free program called Dire Wolf that turned its sound card into a packet modem — software doing the job of a hardware box the club did not own — and Theo's cable would carry the little burst of packet noise from the laptop's headphone jack into the microphone pin of Mr. O's handheld, with the red wire to key the transmitter. The radio would say the packet. The space station, if Si's bus schedule held, would say it back.

He built it at the storeroom desk in one long Tuesday afternoon, and it was, he told the room, some of his cleanest work. He was right about the solder. He was wrong about the plug.

First test: Dire Wolf loaded, packet queued — a test beacon, N8OWL, nothing fancy — radio on, volume up, VOX armed. Theo hit send. The laptop played its little braap of noise. The radio's transmit light came on, proud and red. Across the room, Jun held her FT-60R up to the laptop's microphone — the world's worst acoustic coupler, she called it — and waited for the decode.

The FT-60R's speaker gave them a click. A single, lonely click, like a tongue against teeth. No braap. No packet. The decode window sat empty and judgmental.

"Volume," Theo said, and turned everything up, and ran it again. Click.

"VOX delay," Priya suggested. "The radio wakes up late and clips the front of the packet off."

"Dire Wolf has a setting for that. It's already long." Theo ran it a third time, jaw set, and got a third click, and the room went quiet in the specific way it went quiet when Theo Marsh was about to make it someone's fault.

It was Mr. O who handed him the continuity meter. "The plug," he said, mildly. "Where did the plug come from?"

"The headset. It was a *nice* headset."

"A dead nice headset," said Mr. O. "Rings on a four-conductor plug are not a standard, they are a *suggestion*. Manufacturers move them. Measure your work, Mr. Marsh."

Theo measured his work. Tip, ring, ring, sleeve — and there it was, plain as a confession: the salvaged plug had its rings in an order no diagram had promised, and the packet audio, six feet of good shielded wire, some of his cleanest work, was arriving on the radio's PTT pin. And the red wire, the ceremonial one, was delivering the keying signal to the microphone input, which ignored it completely.

"The plug lied," Theo said.

"The plug was honest," said Priya. "Your assumption was wrong. There's a lesson in that, but I don't want to rush you."

He cut the plug off, re-pinned it by the meter instead of by faith, re-soldered, re-shrunk the heat-shrink. Second build, same afternoon, and this time when Dire Wolf played its braap the radio said it loud and true, and across the room the FT-60R braaped it back, and the laptop's decode window filled up with it: N8OWL, TEST. The world's worst acoustic coupler, working.

"The Marsh Interface, Mark Two," Theo announced, holding the cable up. "The Mark One has been removed from service."

"You named a cable," Priya said.

"You laminated a vocabulary quiz once."

"It was *twice*-sided."

Priya had one more question, asked at the ceiling. "Why the red wire? The radio has VOX. It's literally called voice-*operated*."

"VOX is a *guess*," Theo said, with the serenity of a man who had been wrong that day and lived. "It hears the packet and *then* decides to transmit, and deciding takes time, and the front of a packet is where its name is. Clip the front and the station hears a stranger clearing his throat. The red wire doesn't guess. The red wire *knows*."

"Behold," Jun said. "Humility, day one."

Jun wrote it all in the log anyway, because Jun wrote everything in the log: *cable, laptop to HT, built twice. Second one's the charm. Six dollars total if you count the tape measures and nothing else, because everything else was trash.* She read that last part back. "Six dollars," she said. "The whole station-hearing, space-talking rig. Yagi and cable. Six bucks."

"Don't put that in the log," Theo said. "Put it in the *announcements*."

Wednesday came up mild and violet, and "small crowd," in the Marsh dialect, turned out to mean thirty-one kids, two camp chairs, a blanket somebody spread on the asphalt like a picnic, and one sophomore with a telescope — the wrong instrument entirely, and Theo loved him for it on sight. Mr. O leaned against his car at the edge of the lot with his arms folded, watching the sky and, Theo suspected, enjoying himself enormously.

Theo had promoted it exactly once on the *Marsh Hour* — "a modest demonstration of the club's growing capabilities" — and once more on the morning announcements, because the announcements kid owed him a favor from the winter formal. He had brought the good recorder. He had brought the studio headphones, the big ones with the coiled cord, because history deserved monitoring. He had the FT-60R clipped to the yagi's coax and the pass app open on Priya's phone, and at 9:10 he stood in the middle of the west parking lot with the floodlights humming behind him and thirty-one people watching him hold a tape measure at the sky.

"Two minutes," Priya said.

"Ladies and gentlemen," Theo said, to the recorder, in the baritone, "tonight, Westridge High makes an appointment with—"

"Stop narrating," Priya said. "Listen."

9:12. The app said the station was up, four degrees over the southwest, coming on fast. Theo swept the yagi along the horizon, flat and level, the way you swept for a fox — left, right, hunting the bearing — and the speaker gave him static, and the crowd gave him a polite, attentive murmur, and the static gave him nothing back.

"Seven degrees," Priya said. "It is *there*."

Theo swung wider. Static. Two streets over, a car alarm chirped, and half the crowd turned toward it, because a chirp was at least a sound.

The telescope sophomore raised his hand. "How far away is it? Like, right now?"

"Fifteen hundred miles," said Priya, reading the app, "and closing at five miles a second. When it sets, it'll be over Canada. It's not *far*. It's just *up*."

"So it's, like. A really fast delivery route."

"It's a bus," Theo said, quoting Si, and it almost sounded like confidence.

Bea touched his elbow and tilted her free hand upward, palm flat, like a waiter offering something — *up* — and he raised the yagi's nose, still holding it fox-flat, and swept again, and the static surfed and fell and said nothing at all.

"Nine degrees. Theo—" Priya stepped in close and looked at the FT-60R's glowing display, and her voice went flat with regret on his behalf. "Theo. You're on 144.490."

"I programmed—"

"That's the uplink. You're listening to its *ear*."

The crowd heard none of this, but the crowd felt something, the way crowds do, and the murmur changed key. Theo thumbed the radio to 145.800 with the sweat starting at his hairline, and that was when the coiled cord of the studio headphones, which had been hanging from his neck in loops, caught the end of the director — thirty-five and an eighth inches of spring steel — and the yagi went one way and the FT-60R went the other, and Bea caught the radio by its wrist strap with the fast, small movement of someone catching a falling glass, and for eleven whole seconds the Westridge High Amateur Radio Club's historic first satellite pass was a man untangling himself from his own equipment in front of thirty-one people.

"Do it again!" somebody called, kindly, and to his credit, Theo did not look up.

"Twenty-four," Priya said, gently now. "As high as it gets tonight."

Theo got the yagi up. He aimed where Jun pointed, over the gym, following the little arc on her phone. And for half a second — half a second, no more — the speaker tore a sound out of the sky: a braap, a packet, the digipeater aboard the station chattering to somebody far away, ragged and broken and *there*, and then it was gone, swallowed, and no twisting of the volume knob and no pleading brought it back.

9:22, the app said the pass was over, and the sky did not argue.

The crowd did what crowds do when nothing happens at nine-thirty on a school night: it forgave him, audibly, and went home. "Cool try, Marsh." "Maybe next time text the space station first." The sophomore with the telescope patted the yagi like a horse. The sousaphone player from the jazz band — there was a sousaphone player, of course there was, they traveled in pairs with bad news — played one low note, not even unkindly, and Theo would maintain to his dying day that he had not arranged that.

He sat down on the curb next to Jun and put the studio headphones around his neck and didn't say anything, which was so unlike him that Jun checked his forehead with the back of her hand.

"It was right there," Theo said finally. "Half a second. It was *right there*."

"Half a second is a start," Jun said. "Entry two-fourteen was a dead radio in a dumpster. You should see where it is now."

"It's in a storeroom," Theo said. "I have eyes, Jun."

"It's *alive* in a storeroom." She let that sit, the way Mr. O let demonstrations sit, and then she bumped his shoulder with hers. "You know what the worst part of tonight was, for me? The half second. Before that, space was a poster. Now it's a place that braaps at us. You're allowed to be wrecked. You're not allowed to quit, because of the checklist, which Priya has been writing in her head since the cord thing happened."

Theo looked out across the emptying lot. The floodlights buzzed. Somebody had left the picnic blanket, and the telescope kid was showing the sousaphone player something through the eyepiece that was definitely a streetlight.

"I told thirty-one people to come watch me catch a star," Theo said. "I sold tickets to a no-show."

"You sold tickets to a *rehearsal*," said Mr. O, arriving at the curb with his hands in his pockets and his face, as always, giving nothing away. "Every performer has one. The difference between a professional and an amateur, Mr. Marsh, is that the professional schedules the rehearsal *before* the show." He sat down on the curb too, which none of them had ever seen him do, and looked up at the ordinary sky. "My first pass, I was nineteen, and I pointed the wrong end of the antenna at the satellite for six minutes — reflector forward, like a man steering by the rearview mirror. The signal came and went while I argued with a man twice my age about which end was the front. It remains the most educational argument of my life. I think about it every time I am certain of anything."

"Who won the argument?" Jun asked.

"The satellite," said Mr. O. "By going overhead on schedule, indifferent to both of us. Space doesn't do encores."

"Yes it does," said Priya.

They looked at her. She was holding her phone up, and on it the pass app drew its tidy little arcs, one after another, forever.

"Saturday. May sixteenth. Eight fifty-two p.m. Sixty-eight degrees of elevation — three times higher than tonight, which is four times the signal, which I will show you on the whiteboard because I *enjoy* it. Ten minutes, nearly overhead." She slid the phone into her pocket. "Tonight we did everything wrong that a human being can do with a tape measure, and we still heard half a second of it. So. Tomorrow, four o'clock, 114-B. We are making a checklist, and then we are going to rehearse keeping an appointment." She looked at Theo, and for Priya it was almost tender. "Bring the cable. Bring the Mark Two."

Thursday, four o'clock, Room 114-B. Priya stood at the whiteboard with the marker uncapped and the expression she usually saved for incorrect textbooks, and she wrote in block letters across the top:

PASS CHECKLIST (REV 1)

"Rule one," she said. "Nobody performs. This item is not aimed at anyone."

"It's aimed at Theo," Jun said.

"It's a general principle," Priya said, "that happens to be load-bearing for one specific person. Moving on."

The list went up item by item, argued and voted on — Mr. O insisted a checklist was a contract, and contracts had terms. *Radios: TWO, both charged, both checked the morning of.* *Memories: five, programmed in advance.* *Yagi: elements VERTICAL.* *Cord discipline.* *One person on the screen, everybody else on the sky.* And at the top, in Priya's neatest handwriting, because she said it was the item that made all the other items matter: *LISTEN ON THE DOWNLINK. 145.800. THE MOUTH, NOT THE EAR.*

"About the memories," Mr. O said. "Priya. The monkey."

"Oh," said Priya, and her whole face changed. "The monkey."

Everybody at Westridge knew the monkey — the shrieking stuffed monkey Mr. O threw across the physics room on a string, pitch rising as it came at you and falling as it fled, the Doppler effect installed permanently in a thousand skulls. Theo had seen it freshman year from the third row, and even he had laughed.

"Same physics," Mr. O said. "Bigger monkey. The station comes at you fast — five miles every second — and it bunches its own radio waves up ahead of it. So at the start of the pass, its signal arrives at us a little *high*: about three and a half kilohertz above 145.800. Directly overhead, it's honest. As it leaves, the waves stretch out behind it, and the signal arrives a little *low* — three and a half kilohertz under. The station never moves off its frequency. Your receiver is the thing that has to travel."

"So we start high and tune *down* through the pass," Priya said, and she was at the board doing it before anyone could answer, five neat lines: *145.8035 — 145.8015 — 145.8000 — 145.7985 — 145.7965.* "Five memories. Programmed tonight, not in the parking lot, because nobody does math on a curb at nine o'clock. I call the mark, Theo clicks down one. Nobody fumbles with a knob in the dark. Nobody *hunts*."

"Nobody hunts," Theo agreed, and wrote it in the log himself, which Jun noted with one raised eyebrow.

"And the vertical thing," Priya went on, "is not a superstition, it's Wednesday's other murder." She held the yagi up flat, fox-hunt style. "The station's antennas are vertical whips. Ours match them or they don't. Hold the elements flat while theirs stand vertical — cross-polarized — and you throw away about twenty dB of signal."

"English," Theo said.

"Twenty dB is a factor of a hundred. We threw away ninety-nine percent of space."

There was a silence in which Theo did the math on half a second of braap, multiplied by a hundred, and looked genuinely ill. "Saturday," he said. "Vertical. Somebody write it twice."

"It's written twice," said Priya.

Jobs, then, assigned by acclamation. Bea aimed the yagi — she raised one finger when Mr. O asked who had the steadiest hands, and the vote was unanimous and took no time at all. Jun ran the app and called the bearings, because Jun could read a compass off a screen the way other people read street signs. Priya kept the clock and the checklist and called the tuning marks. Theo ran the radio — memories, volume, and, Saturday, the cable — and Mr. O stood at the back with his own handheld and his license and his face, the control operator, the key.

Friday after school they rehearsed in the empty lot with Priya's stopwatch and an imaginary satellite — Bea tracking an invisible dot from horizon to zenith to horizon, Jun calling degrees, Theo clicking memories on the marks, nobody saying anything they didn't have to. They ran it a third time because Bea looked at the stopwatch, then at the sky, and simply started over.

Halfway home afterward, Jun fell into step beside Theo. "You're being weird," she said. "You haven't narrated anything in two days."

"I'm rehearsing," Theo said.

Theo did not promote Saturday. He recorded no promo, called in no favors, told no one outside the group chat, and when the announcements kid asked him on Friday morning if the space thing was happening again, Theo said, "If it works, you can hear about it after," and walked away feeling the strange, light-headed calm of a man who had just handed back a microphone.

---

Saturday evening came on slow and gold, the way May evenings did when feeling generous, and by 8:40 the west parking lot held five students, one teacher, one laptop on the trunk of Mr. O's car, and no sousaphone at all. The swallows were working the floodlights for insects. Venus hung over the gym like somebody had nailed it there. Theo's studio headphones were home on their hook like a retired prizefighter, and the earbud Priya had issued him — cord routed down his sleeve, checklist item four — sat in his left ear and said static, soft and patient.

At 8:41, a car pulled in at the far end of the lot, and Ms. Delgado got out with her clipboard, said nothing to anyone, and stood at the edge of the asphalt with her hands folded, watching the southwest sky.

Priya checked her watch. "Eleven minutes early," she murmured. "Right on schedule."

"Checklist," said Mr. O.

Priya read it, and they answered it, item by item, like a litany. "Radios." "Two, charged, checked twice." "Memories." "Five, programmed, verified: 145.8035 down to 145.7965." "Polarization." Bea raised the yagi overhead, elements standing vertical against the violet sky. "Cord." Theo lifted his sleeve to show the routed wire. "History will note the sacrifice," Priya said. "Uplink radio." Mr. O held up his handheld, Theo's cable running from its side to the laptop on the trunk, where Dire Wolf sat with their packet already typed and waiting. "Downlink frequency." "145.800," said Theo. "The mouth." "And the appointment?"

"Eight fifty-two," said Jun, eyes on the app. "Southwest. Sixty-eight degrees. Ten minutes."

"Then we wait," said Mr. O, "like professionals."


---

At 8:52 exactly, with the sky going from violet to the color of deep water, Jun said, "Southwest, four degrees, coming up over the gym," and Bea raised the yagi to meet it, and the static in Theo's earbud tore open into a braap.

A packet. Loud, whole, unhurried — the digipeater doing its day job for somebody two time zones away. Not a ragged half-second this time: a full sentence in a language of braaps, and behind it something that was not a braap at all.

"Memory two," Priya said, calm as a metronome. Theo clicked down. The voice sharpened.

"*—the thing nobody tells you is how quiet it is up here. The station hums, the fans never stop, but the* world *is quiet. You float up to the Cupola at the end of the day and the whole planet is just* there*, turning, no sound at all—*"

The parking lot made no noise.

"School contact," Mr. O murmured. "A class somewhere is asking questions. We can only hear his half — the school is below our horizon. The station is not."

The astronaut's half came down out of the dark in pieces, clear as church bells at the top of the pass, fraying at the edges as it fell. A kid had asked about sleeping. "*Strapped to the wall like a mummy, and your arms float up in front of you — first night, I kept waking up because my own hands were* floating.*" A kid had asked, audibly in the astronaut's tone, the question every kid asked, and the answer was about the aurora: "*Green curtains, except you're not under them looking up. You're* above *them, looking down. Nobody tells you that. The pictures are all from below. The view from on top is—*" and the word that came out of the sky was just "*—mine*," and then a laugh, human and ordinary and two hundred fifty miles straight up.

Bea held the yagi like it was a violin. Jun called degrees in a whisper. Priya called the marks, and Theo clicked the memories down one by one — 145.8000, 145.7985 — and the voice stayed locked in, and Theo stood in the Maple Run parking lot with a cheap earbud in his ear and listened to a person talk from *work*, from the tin can where he lived, on an antenna that had cost six dollars and once found a radio in an ammo can in the snow.

"*—sixteen sunrises a day up here, and I have tried to watch every one. Tell them down there the view is worth the math. November Alfa One Sierra Sierra, clear.*"

The school's thank-yous came from below their horizon, unheard, and the frequency hung open a moment. Into the gap slid the braaps, patient as traffic: the digipeater, back at work.

"Mid-pass," Priya said softly. "Sixty-eight degrees. The window. Mr. O — *now*."

Mr. O was already at the trunk. "One call sign on the air," he said, half to himself, the litany of the thing. "Mine. Your words. My key." His thumb went down on the PTT, and Dire Wolf sent their packet up the cable Theo had built twice, and the little radio braaped their hello at 145.825 with everything it had — five watts and a clear sky.

One heartbeat. Two.

The FT-60R on the yagi braaped back.

Their own packet. Their *exact* packet — Theo knew its shape by now the way you know a song — repeated back down on 145.800 by a machine doing seventeen thousand miles an hour over the state line, caught by Bea's steady hands and six dollars of measuring tape. The digipeater had heard them and answered, and the whole continent was in the footprint to hear it.

"That's us," Jun whispered. "That's literally, actually *us*."

And Priya, who had been waiting with her phone already loaded, made a small, undignified yelp that no one in Room 114-B had ever heard from her, and held the screen out with both hands. "A-P-R-S dot F-I," she said. "All over the footprint there are volunteer receivers — iGates — they listen to the downlink and gate whatever the station repeats onto the internet. Look. *Look.*"

On the screen, on a little map of the West, was a line of stations that had heard their packet, gated live, seconds old: Spokane. Boise. A town outside Salt Lake City. And at the top of the list, plain as print: **N8OWL via ARISS** — their words, their hello, *WESTRIDGE HIGH ARC SAYS HELLO FROM A SIX DOLLAR TAPE MEASURE*, timestamped less than a minute ago, signed with Mr. O's call and carried there by a machine in orbit.

"Scroll down," Jun started — and Priya did, past their own pass, down into the digipeater's other list, the global one: every station heard via ARISS on earlier passes, all over the world, all day. That was where Jun grabbed Priya's wrist, because near the bottom of that list, between a station in Oregon and one in Manitoba, logged hours ago on a pass over Australia, sat a call sign they knew. "VK2KDP. *Priya.* We know him. January. Ten meters, the gray line—"

"The Australian," Bea said, and there was something in her voice like recognition across a crowded room. "He talks to the machine too."

At 9:02 the last braap frayed and folded under the static like a letter going into an envelope, and the app said the pass was over, and this time the sky had to agree.

Nobody moved. The swallows worked the floodlights. Venus hung where it was nailed.

Theo's phone buzzed; he answered it on speaker without thinking, and Si Webb's voice came out, pleased with itself.

"Heard him from the barn," Si said. "Clear as church. And I saw your packet on the map before my coffee cooled. Six-dollar fishpole did fine." A pause, and the sound of a dog settling. "Appointment kept. Seventy-three, kids."

"Seventy-three," they said, ragged as ever, and the line went quiet, and the parking lot was just a parking lot again — except that none of it was.

Theo had called space a gated community with better PR, on the air, in the baritone, for forty minutes. He stood there now with his sleeve pushed up and Priya's checklist warm in his pocket and the shape of their own packet still ringing in his skull, and he did the math the way she had taught him to, out loud so it counted.

"The gate's a tape measure and a checklist," he said. "And it opens from the inside."

"That's disgustingly poetic," Priya said. "Put it in the episode."


---

They were coiling the coax by flashlight when Ms. Delgado crossed the asphalt, clipboard under her arm, silver glasses catching the floodlights. She had watched all ten minutes without a word, from eleven minutes early to ninety seconds after, and now she stopped in front of Theo and looked at the yagi, the laptop on the trunk, the five of them standing in their own quiet.

"Mr. Marsh," she said. "Was that the club's public demonstration?"

Theo considered the question more carefully than any question in his life.

"No, ma'am," he said. "That was a rehearsal. The demonstration's in June. Everybody's invited."

"Mm." She made a note on the clipboard. "The board agrees with you," she said. "As of Thursday afternoon. The club's public-demonstration requirement will be satisfied by your Field Day operation — Fairview County Park, June twenty-seventh and twenty-eighth, open to the public. Mr. Okafor's application was approved. I intended to tell you all on Monday." She clicked her pen, and something happened to her face that was in a smile's family. "My father listened to shortwave," she said, to nobody in particular. "He would have liked tonight."

She walked back to her car. The door closed gently, the car slid away, and Jun said, "She knew on *Thursday*," in the tone of a woman filing something away for a future religion.

"She knows everything eleven minutes early," Priya said. "The vote was Thursday. That's not eleven minutes, that's two *days*, which means she drove out here—" She stopped, and did the math again, slower, and for once said nothing at all.

---

Monday, Room 114-B, the window open to the practice field and the smell of cut grass. Priya turned the whiteboard calendar to June with a ceremony she pretended not to feel, and wrote in her small, furious hand:

FIELD DAY — 40 DAYS

Under it, in parentheses, because she was Priya: *(t-minus five weeks and five days; yes, I will be counting)*.

Theo recorded the *Marsh Hour* that night at the storeroom desk, window open, the band murmuring under his voice like surf under a pier. He kept the Wednesday pass in the final cut — the uplink, the cord, the sousaphone, all of it — because you don't edit out the part where you aimed at the wrong sky. That was the part that was true. And he ended the episode with the invitation, in the baritone, but steadier than the baritone had ever been:

"Field Day. Fairview County Park, June twenty-seventh and twenty-eighth. We'll have antennas up all weekend — the ones we built, all of them, the cans and the wires and the tape measure — and you can come hear what they hear. Bring a chair. We'll bring the sky."

He stopped the recorder and sat in the hum of the room, and watched Bea, who said nothing at all, take the laminated pass checklist — REV 2, with *WORKS* penciled at the bottom in Jun's handwriting — and tack it to the corkboard, straight and level, in the bare inch of cork between a QSL card from Iceland, 1963, and a postcard from a ship at sea.

Forty years of proof that a contact really happened. Now forty days to make some more.
