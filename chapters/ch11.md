## Chapter 11: Field Day

By four o'clock Friday afternoon, the west end of Fairview County Park looked like a yard sale at the end of the world, and Jun Alvarez had never been prouder of anything in her life.

The picnic shelter — Ms. Delgado had reserved it in March, for the last full weekend of June, back when Field Day was an act of faith rather than a plan — held two folding tables, one for each radio. On the first, the Kenwood TS-520S sat on a thrift-store blanket like a battleship in dry dock, its dented corner gleaming. On the second, Si's IC-7300, modern and faintly smug about it, with a screen like a tiny television. Beneath the tables: two marine batteries, a toolbox, a snarl of coax, and Resistor, who had inspected the entire site and chosen, as his post, the one patch of shade that everybody would trip over for the next thirty hours.

The antennas waited in bundles along the benches, and Jun walked the row the way a general reviews troops, because she had personally dragged half this army out of the trash.

The extension-cord dipole: two sixteen-point-six-foot legs of garage-sale cord, orange as traffic cones, that had carried lawnmower current in a previous life. The EFHW: sixty-five and a half feet of magnet wire unspooled from a dead transformer, its 49:1 unun sealed in a sandwich box that still smelled faintly of Bea's Babcia's pierogi. The ten-meter wire, sixteen and a half feet of nothing, the antenna you could lose in a laundry basket. The J-pole — fifty-seven and a half inches of half-inch copper pipe and its nineteen-inch stub — riding in a gunny sack like a trombone. The pop-can dipole, four root-beer cans a side on its broomstick, going on nine months old. And the tape-measure yagi, leaning against a bench like abstract sculpture, its steel elements catching the late sun.

"Two transmitters, emergency power, club call," Mr. O said, arriving with a clipboard in one hand and a folding chair in the other. "That makes us class Two Alpha. N8OWL on the air for twenty-four hours, starting tomorrow." He looked along the benches. "Or starting never, if we don't get the wires up before dark."

They got the wires up.

Mr. O made them do it properly, because proper was the whole point. He paced off the distance to the service-road power line himself, heel to toe, and made Jun pace it after him. "Twice the length of the longest antenna, then some more," he said. "The line's dead until it isn't. That's the rule, and it doesn't care that we're in a hurry."

"Sixty-five feet of EFHW, a hundred and thirty of pace," Priya said, counting. "We're at a hundred and sixty."

"Then we're fine," Mr. O agreed. "Spotters. Every throw."

Jun threw the first line herself — a water bottle full of sand on mason twine — up and over the big cottonwood limb, while Theo spotted with his hands up like a crossing guard, watching the road line, watching the throw. *She winds up, the crowd holds its breath—*

"Hush," said Bea, who was holding the other end of the dipole, and Theo hushed.

The extension-cord dipole went up as an inverted-V, apex at twenty-five feet on the cottonwood, ends drooping to ten, the eight-turn choke balun snug at the feedpoint. The EFHW sloped from a second limb to the shelter's back beam, its six-and-a-half-foot counterpoise dangling out of the unun box like a tail. The J-pole went onto its tripod at the edge of the gravel, staked and guyed. The pop-can dipole got lashed to a shelter post at twelve feet, where it would handle the talk-in station on the repeater, one-point-eight-to-one same as ever, like it was proud of itself.

Priya hung the ten-meter wire last, and she hung it with a compass in her teeth and a great-circle map folded into her pocket.

"Why is that one crooked?" Theo asked. "All the others go that way."

"Because Japan is *that* way." Priya took the compass out of her mouth and pointed northwest. "A dipole hears best off its broadside, perpendicular to the wire. The great-circle path to Osaka from here is northwest, so the wire runs northeast to southwest. I did the math in February, in case you thought I was doing it for my health."

"You did the math in February for a thing that happens in June?"

"I did the math in February," Priya said, "for a thing that happens at *dawn*, in June, when you will all be too tired to do math. You're welcome in advance."

The failure announced itself at 5:40, in the voice of Si's antenna analyzer, which was supposed to say one-point-six and said three-point-one instead.

Jun felt it in her stomach first. The extension-cord dipole had been one-point-six that morning, checked and coiled in the storeroom. Now the analyzer's little screen said 3.1:1, and then 2.7, and then 3.4, wandering like a dog off its leash, which was the one thing SWR was never supposed to do.

"Frequency's the same. Antenna's the same." Priya was already frowning at the numbers, recording them anyway, because she recorded everything. "It shouldn't *move*. SWR doesn't have a mood."

"What changed?" Si said, hands in his pockets, looking at Jun instead of the analyzer. Si never turned a screwdriver for them. He asked the question that made you see the screwdriver.

"Nothing changed," Jun said. "We hung it exactly—" She stopped. She looked up at the feedpoint, and then down, along the run of coax. At school, the feedline dropped away from the dipole straight down the wall. Here, because Jun had wanted the shelter to look *tidy* for the public, she'd dressed the coax along the shelter's steel support post and down the downspout, taped in four places, like cable TV.

Priya followed her eyes. Then she walked over and put her hand flat on the coax.

The analyzer swung half a point.

"There it is," Priya said.

"There what is?" Theo said.

"The feedline is part of the antenna right now." Priya took her hand off, put it back, made the number wander on command, like the world's most boring parlor trick. "The braid. RF's riding on the *outside* of the coax — common-mode current, same as Mr. Pruitt's speakers. At school it dropped away clean, so there was nothing to couple to. Here it's taped to ten feet of steel post. We built a longer antenna without asking it."

"The choke's supposed to stop that," Jun said, and then answered herself before anyone else could. "The choke stops it at the feedpoint. It can't stop what the feedline picks up *after*." She was already pulling tape. "It was fine at school because it was ugly at school. I made it pretty and broke it."

"Neatness," said Mr. O, who had materialized with the toolbox, "counts in penmanship."

They re-ran the coax straight off the feedpoint, away from the post, a long lazy diagonal through the air to the table, ugly as sin. Jun re-dressed the choke — eight turns, six inches across, snug against the balun — and Priya put her hand on the coax again and waved it around like a wand, and the analyzer did not care at all.

One-point-six. Steady as a heartbeat.

"RF don't read your intentions," Si said, and picked up his coffee. "It reads the geometry."

County Tech arrived at six, in a diesel pickup towing a trailer, and the trailer was carrying a tower.

Jun watched it back in on the far side of the meadow with the complicated feelings of a person whose entire army was made of trash. The tower was folded flat on the trailer like a sleeping giraffe, and when they got it upright — with a *motor*, a little whining electric winch — it cranked up and up to forty feet, with a tribander beam on a rotator, so it could *turn*. The County Tech crew spilled out in matching navy polos that said COUNTY TECH RADIO across the back, set up a generator, and had their station lit up inside of an hour, efficient as a pit crew.

Their captain came over at seven to say hello, because it was that kind of rivalry. She was a tall junior named Dana, with a clipboard even more official than Mr. O's, and she looked along the benches of junk with an expression Jun couldn't quite read.

"You're the can people," Dana said. "The repeater thing, from the school roof. Our advisor showed us the picture."

"Pop-can dipole," Jun said. "Four cans a side. It's the talk-in station tomorrow."

"Uh-huh," Dana said. "And the extension cord is—"

"Twenty meters."

"And the *tape measure*?"

"Satellites," Jun said. "And fox hunts. There's a hidden transmitter involved, if your crew wants to lose to a bunch of underclassmen tomorrow."

Dana looked at her for a second, and then grinned, and Jun decided the expression from before had been respect with a good poker face. "We have a two-thousand-dollar beam," Dana said, "and I am genuinely worried. That's Field Day." She stuck out her hand. "Good luck, can people. Try to keep up."

It was full dark when Si called everybody over and pointed west, at a sky that looked, to Jun, like absolutely nothing — stars, a little haze, the lights of Maple Run.

"Cells in the forecast," Si said. "After midnight. Some of them rowdy." He let that sit, the way he let everything sit. "Don't take my word for it. Take the weather radio's."

Jun fished the little crank radio out of the salvage bin — entry 231, *crank weather radio, $2, Second Wind bin, works fine* — and set it on the table, where it murmured the automated forecast in a flat robot voice: *scattered thunderstorms, mainly after midnight, some possibly severe.*

"So we dress for it now," Mr. O said. "Every wire lashed, every tripod staked, everything that can blow away tied to something that can't. We lash it now, or we chase it at midnight in our socks."

They lashed it now. Jun went down the row with twine and tape, doubling knots, and the last thing she saw before she crawled into her sleeping bag in the shelter was the whole junkyard spread under the starlight — extension cord and magnet wire, copper pipe and soda cans, one tape measure — all of it humming very slightly in the night breeze, all of it waiting.

---

Saturday morning arrived with Mr. Pruitt, who pulled into the gravel lot at seven carrying a donut box and a folding solar panel.

"One hundred watts of free electricity," he announced, "from my RV days, which are behind me, like my hairline. The panel comes home Sunday or I send my speakers after you."

"How are the speakers?" Mr. O asked.

"Quiet as church, thanks to you people." Pruitt set the panel facing the morning sun and squinted at the wires. "This is the junk I've been hearing about? On my repeater?"

"Your repeater?"

"I listen," Pruitt said, with dignity, and went off to find better coffee, which did not exist, because Si had brought his percolator.

Priya got the solar panel wired through its charge controller into the battery bank while Jun ran the talk-in station. The talk-in was the pop-can dipole's job: all day, hams and curious civilians would be hunting for the west end of Fairview County Park, and the club's invitation had gone out on W7MR/R all week — one forty-six point seven six, PL one hundred. Jun clipped her abuelo's FT-60R to the shelter post coax, sat down with a legal pad, and had been there twenty minutes when the little radio crackled.

*"W7MR talk-in, this is— uh — W7PBJ, mobile, just passing through? I'm in the parking lot? The east lot? There's a swan boat thing."*

Jun looked at Mr. O, who was arranging pencils with unnecessary care three feet away — close enough to be the control operator of every word she said, far enough to make her say them. He nodded once.

"Talk-in, this is N8OWL," Jun said, and her voice only shook a little. "You're in the wrong lot. Go back out to the park road, take the right fork past the paddle boats, and follow it to the west end. Look for the shelter with the orange wires. We have donuts." She paused. "Ignore the crank-up tower — that's County Tech. We're the one next to them with the—" she considered "—character."

*"Copy that. Character and donuts. W7PBJ, on my way."*

"Seventy-three," Jun said, and set the radio down with the particular happiness of a person who has just given directions over the sky.

By nine the public had started to trickle — dog walkers, a dad with three kids who was clearly here because Priya's flyer at the library had said BUILD AN ANTENNA OUT OF TRASH, FREE. Priya had made an information table with her own hands: poster board diagrams of the ionosphere, the 468-over-frequency formula written out in longhand, and a checklist taped to the post that said FIELD DAY BONUS POINTS and listed, in her handwriting: *public place ✓ · info table ✓ · youth operators ✓✓✓✓ · satellite contact (pending)*.

At ten they found out the batteries were lying to them.

They gave everything a full-dress test before the noon start — the TS-520S's tubes glowing that warm-attic glow Jun would know anywhere. Mr. O sat in the operator's chair and called a short test on twenty meters; a station in Oregon, doing the same thing, came back.

He got as far as "November Eight Oscar Whiskey—" and the Kenwood's relay *chattered* — a horrible little machine-gun sound — and the dial light dipped, and the Oregon station came back with the report nobody wants:

*"You might want to check your audio, friend. You sound fuzzy. Real fuzzy."*

Mr. O unkeyed. The radio sat there, perfectly innocent, dial glowing green. "Well," he said. "That's new."

Priya had the multimeter out before anyone finished wincing. She put the probes on the battery posts. "Twelve point six at the battery. Mr. O, key it again for one second."

He keyed. The relay chattered.

"Ten point four," Priya said, "at the *rig*. And twelve point two at the battery." She sat back on her heels. "We're losing two volts between the battery and the radio. The power is fine. The *road* is bad."

Jun looked at the road. The road was the jumper cables she'd grabbed from the club bin — long ones, thin ones, with alligator clips the size of her thumb, because they were the first thing she'd found. "Twelve-gauge," she said. "Maybe. And ten feet long."

"Twenty amps on transmit," Priya said, sketching the circuit in the air. "Volts equals current times resistance. Twenty amps through a long skinny wire, and the wire's resistance eats volts like— it's Ohm's law *mugging* us. The rig starves, the relay can't hold, the audio goes fuzzy. It's not the batteries. It's the plumbing."

"The panel's fine too," Pruitt said from the batteries, hurt. "In case anyone was going to say anything."

"Nobody was," Priya said. "The panel is the only innocent party here."

Si had wandered over during the diagnosis and was standing with his hands in his pockets, looking at the jumper cables the way you look at a dog that has dug up the garden. "Big river," he said. "Little pipe."

"Short fat pipe," Priya said. "Which we don't—"

"Truck," said Si, and walked off, and came back three minutes later with a set of battery cables from his toolbox — heavy gauge, short, with real bolt lugs, because of course Silas Webb had Field Day jumper cables ready to go in June. He didn't install them. He set them on the table next to Jun, raised one eyebrow, and went back to his coffee.

Jun installed them. Bolt lugs, wrench tight, both batteries paralleled right at the table, so the run to the rigs was four feet of fat copper instead of ten feet of skinny. Priya re-checked: twelve point six at the battery, twelve point one at the rig, key down, relay holding like a bulldog.

"Oregon's gone," Mr. O said, tuning. "But—" he found a clear spot and gave a short call, and Utah answered in thirty seconds: *"Clean audio now, N8OWL. Whatever you did, keep doing it."*

"Plumbing," Priya said, and wrote it in the log's margin, because it was a fix and fixes got logged: *1030 — replaced power leads, 4 ft heavy cable. Note: ohms are real.*

At eleven forty-five, Mr. O gathered them around the shelter post, the way Jun imagined a coach might, if coaches wore constellation ties.

"Rules," he said. "There are two, and they are the same two as always. One: I am the control operator of every transmission, and so is Mr. Webb — every watt that leaves this site leaves under a license, and the license is standing right here, so if neither of us is in this shelter, nobody keys anything. Two: the log. If it isn't in the log, it didn't happen. We ID with the club call every ten minutes — November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima — we give real signal reports, and we say seventy-three, because we are polite people visiting other people's hobby." He looked around at them. "The exchange is our class: Two Alpha. Field Day is not a race. It's a twenty-four-hour demonstration that when the lights go out, we don't. The points are just how you know it's working."

"And the rest of the rules?" Theo said.

"The rest of the rules," said Mr. O, "is lunch."

At noon — eighteen hundred zulu, which was the only clock that mattered this weekend — the laptop's logging clock flipped, and somewhere beyond the cottonwoods a generator note changed at the County Tech site, and up and down the band, ten thousand hams leaned into their microphones at once. You could hear it happen. Twenty meters went from conversation to avalanche in the space of a breath.

Theo was in the chair, because the chair had been built for him. He pressed the mic and said, in the baritone God had clearly issued for exactly this purpose:

"CQ Field Day, CQ Field Day, this is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, N8OWL, calling CQ Field Day and listening."

The band answered before he'd finished letting go of the switch.

---

For four hours, Theo Marsh was exactly as good as he had always believed he was.

He ran twenty meters like the *Marsh Hour* with an antenna — quick, warm, funny, relentless. "W6 in Fresno, you're five-nine, Two Alpha, thanks for the call — QRZ Field Day, November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima." Priya logged beside him, pencil moving in her small furious handwriting, and the pages filled: a W1 in Maine, a station in Ohio, two in Texas. Theo IDed every ten minutes without being asked, gave real reports, remembered names.

The public came in waves all afternoon, and the waves had a current: they drifted from County Tech's gleaming tower, past Priya's poster board, and stopped — stopped dead, every time — at the pop-can dipole.

"That's *trash*," said the dad from the library flyer, in the tone of a man whose worldview had just shifted. "That's actual trash."

"Resonant trash," Jun said. "Four cans a side, a quarter wave tall on two meters. The can doesn't care it used to hold root beer. Nineteen point three inches of it is nineteen point three inches."

Ms. Delgado arrived at three, and she did not come alone. She came with the chair of the school board — a woman with silver hair, a visor, and the air of someone who scheduled her Saturdays in fifteen-minute blocks. Jun watched them cross the meadow, Delgado pointing, the chair nodding.

"Mr. Okafor," said the chair, shaking hands like a firm deadline. "Ms. Delgado tells me this is the club's required public demonstration."

"This is it," said Mr. O. "Twenty-four hours, two transmitters, emergency power, every antenna on this site built by the students out of—" he considered the benches "—the discarded bounty of Maple Run."

"How much did it cost?" said the chair, which was clearly the question she asked everything.

"The antennas?" Jun said, before she could stop herself. "The soda cans were a deposit. The extension cord was four dollars. The wire in the end-fed was free, because it was already garbage." She thought. "The tape measure was the expensive one. Six dollars."

The chair looked at her for a long moment. Then she looked at the County Tech tower, turning majestically in the breeze three hundred feet away, and then back at the broomstick. "I'll want the tour," she said.

She got the tour — Priya on the ionosphere, dry and precise, two paragraphs and not one word more; Theo at full throttle, working two stations while somehow making it look like a parlor trick. And at four o'clock, she got the fox hunt.

Bea had hidden the fox an hour earlier — the battered ammo can with a battery HT inside, set to beacon its little Morse ID every thirty seconds, same as it had done in the snow in December. Now she handed the yagi to the flyer's oldest kid, a girl of maybe eleven, and pointed at the HT in her own hand.

"It beeps," the girl said, holding the radio like a walkie-talkie, which, fair.

"It says its name in Morse," Bea said. "Everything with a call sign has to say its name. That's the law. The fox is obeying the law."

"Why is this piece longer than the others?" the girl asked, tapping the rearmost element.

"That's the reflector," Bea said. "Forty-one and three-eighths inches. It bounces the signal forward, toward the short one. The radio's louder when the antenna points at the fox." She showed her the S-meter on the HT's little screen, and the way the needle swung as the yagi swept. "Stronger means warmer."

The three kids took off across the meadow with the yagi held out in front of them like a metal detector, sweeping, stopping, arguing about bearings, trailing the dad and then the chair and then — Jun noticed, and elbowed Priya — Ms. Delgado, at a dignified five-yard remove, pretending she was just out for a walk. The kids overshot twice, triangulated, and converged on the paddle-boat shed in nineteen minutes, where the ammo can sat behind a garbage can, beeping its name to no one.

"FOX," the eleven-year-old read off the paper Bea had given her, dots and dashes decoded with her tongue between her teeth. "It just says FOX. Over and over."

"So you know when you've won," Bea said.

"That's the saddest thing I've ever heard," the girl said happily, and asked if she could hide it next.

The ISS pass came at 6:40, and Priya had been advertising it since lunch, which for Priya meant she had written the time on the whiteboard and underlined it. A respectable crowd gathered — the club, the fox hunters and their dad, Ms. Delgado, the board chair, and Dana and two of her crew, who had no satellite pass and had come to see the six-dollar tape measure work.

Priya put the yagi in the eleven-year-old's hands again. "It's going to come up in the southwest. It talks on one forty-five point eight hundred — but it won't *start* there. Hold the antenna on it, and I'll do the radio."

"Why won't it start there?" the girl asked.

"Doppler," Priya said, as the timer hit zero and she spun the dial three and a half kilohertz high. "It's moving at seventeen thousand miles an hour, and its signal stretches and squeezes like a train horn. It starts high and slides down through the whole pass. Ten minutes, start to finish. We just slide with it."

The first burst came through the speaker thirty seconds later — a harsh chirp of packet radio, then another, and on the laptop, the decode drew call signs out of the noise like shapes surfacing in a pond. And there, threading through them: NA1SS.

"That's the space station," Theo told the crowd, with a showman's timing. "That's a machine in orbit, with people on it, heard on an antenna made out of a tape measure and a garden hose clamp." He paused. "Six dollars."

"Seven," said the board chair, "if you count the clamp," and everybody looked at her, and she was smiling, slightly, with one corner of her mouth.

Priya crossed *satellite contact* off the bonus list. Then she wrote the time, because the log was the log.

By seven o'clock, Theo Marsh had ninety-one contacts in the log, a voice like a late-night DJ, a pileup building on his frequency that he could hear stacking up behind every transmission — and, although nobody at the shelter could have told you exactly when it started, the beginnings of a problem.

The problem was that the pileup was not an audience, and Theo treated it like one.

It built the way pileups build — a big signal answers, somebody tells a friend, and by seven-thirty there were twenty stations calling at once, layered over each other like a cafeteria at lunch. And Theo, who had spent three years learning to hold a room, started doing what you do with a room. He played it. He took the loud ones first because they were easy. He made jokes. He clipped calls short — "got the W6, thanks, next" — without confirming what he'd logged, because dead air was the one sin of the *Marsh Hour*, and the patter had to keep moving.

Priya touched his arm. "You're skipping the weak ones. They keep calling. And I think you logged that Ohio station twice."

"I've got it," Theo said.

"You've got it twice," Priya said. "That's my point."

"I *said* I've got it."

It came out sharp — sharper than he meant, sharp enough that the little silence afterward had a shape. And then the band did the thing it does when you start believing your own signal report: a patient voice came through the pileup, calm as a fence post, and said, *"N8OWL, take a breath, son. One at a time. You got my prefix wrong twice. It's Whiskey, not Kilo."*

Theo pressed the mic to answer and nothing came out. He froze — the great Theo Marsh, dead air, eight full seconds of it, the sin itself — and then he set the microphone down on the log book, very gently, stood up, and walked out of the shelter toward the lake.

Nobody followed. That was the club's instinct, and a good one. Mr. O slid into the chair, picked up the mic, and worked three stations the slow way — full calls, confirmed twice, one at a time, no patter at all — and the pileup, robbed of its DJ, settled down and behaved like a line of people again.

Si found Theo at the water's edge, skipping nothing, hands in his pockets, watching the paddle boats. Si didn't sit down. He looked at the lake for a while, and then he said:

"Your voice got you the pileup. Your ears are the only thing that can run it."

"I was *entertaining* them," Theo said, to the lake.

"You were feeding them your voice and calling it theirs." Si picked up a flat stone, looked at it, put it down unskipped. "A pileup ain't a show. It's thirty people who each want one thing — to be heard, by you, once. Loudest man on the frequency is just another caller. Calmest man is the one running the net." He let that sit. "Radio don't care how you sound, son. It cares how you *listen*."

He walked back. Theo stayed another ten minutes. Then he came back to the shelter, and the first thing he did was turn to Priya and say, so everyone could hear it: "You were right. I logged him twice and I bit your head off. Log that too — *operator error, corrected.*"

"Logged," Priya said, and slid the log book back to him, and that was the whole reconciliation, because Priya's forgiveness had always come in the form of stationery.

---

Dinner was Babcia's pierogi out of a cooler — she had sent forty, which was Babcia's idea of a light snack — and Marisol's empanadas, delivered with a kiss on the top of Jun's head and a whispered *your grandfather would be insufferable about all of this*, which was the highest blessing the Alvarez family issued. The sun went down. And at eight-thirty, Bea Kowalczyk sat down at the IC-7300, plugged in the J-38 straight key that had lived in her jacket pocket since April, and called CQ on forty meters, with Si beside her logging and licensed, and changed the whole temperature of the night.

Jun had heard Bea send before. She had not heard her send *at Field Day*, with a band full of serious CW operators hunting contacts. Her dits and dahs came off the old Navy key even as stitching, unhurried, exactly Bea — and the band noticed. The first answer came back at her speed. Then two at once. Then a small, polite pileup of people who could tell, the way CW people can tell inside of three letters, that the fist on the other end belonged to someone who *meant it*.

She didn't perform. That was the thing Theo would say later, watching from the back of the shelter with his arms crossed, quietly taking the lesson. She just listened — head tipped, utterly still, copying calls in her head while the rest of them scribbled — and answered each one clean: *ur 599 2A*, thanks, seventy-three, next.

Dana drifted over from the County Tech site at nine and stood watching with her crew. "How fast is she going?" she whispered.

"Fast enough to be right," Si said, and did not look up from the log.

One of Dana's crew, a senior, found Bea on the band after her next CQ and came back fast — twenty-some words a minute, a friendly little challenge. Bea's eyebrow went up, once. Her hand didn't change. She answered him at his own speed, gave him his report, wished him seventy-three, and moved on, and the senior sat down in a camp chair and just *listened*, the way you listen to someone do a thing you thought you were good at.

Her first hour, when Priya totaled it: forty-one contacts.

"Mine was thirty-eight," Theo said.

"Mm," said Priya.

"On voice," Theo said. "With *jokes*." He looked at Bea, who was rubbing her wrist and pretending not to know anyone was talking about her. "She doesn't even perform."

"That's why it works," said Mr. O.

At ten, Theo took the chair again — the SSB chair, the microphone, the band still loud — and Jun watched him sit for a moment before keying, doing some interior rearrangement. Then he pressed the switch.

"November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima — I'll take the station ending in Kilo. Only the station ending in Kilo, please."

And he ran it that way for an hour — by suffixes, one at a time, full calls confirmed, the weak ones first when he could hear them, patient as a man calling roll. The patter was gone. The contacts came *faster*. Priya logged without a single correction, and at eleven, when he signed the chair over to the graveyard rotation, Theo's last hour had beaten his best hour by nine, and he didn't say anything about it at all, which was how you knew the lesson had landed.

---

The cell announced itself at 11:40, the way Si had said it would — a wind shift first, cool and abrupt, and then the little crank radio on the table woke up with its alarm tone and the flat robot voice: *severe thunderstorm warning, damaging winds, until twelve forty-five.*

They had drilled this in March, on a school roof, with the J-pole. Nobody said anything dramatic. Mr. O simply stood up and said, "That's it. Wires stay, everything comes off the table," and the club moved.

Coax unplugged, every run, and the free ends carried twenty feet from the shelter and laid on the ground. Rigs off. Batteries disconnected. HTs off and in the bin — ears down, no exceptions, because the rule did not care that Field Day points were ticking away. Jun had the laptop and log under a trash bag in ninety seconds. Priya pulled the ten-meter wire down to half height and lashed it. Bea pocketed the J-38 — the only thing anyone saved on instinct.

Across the meadow, the County Tech site had gone from contest to fire drill — flashlight beams crossing, shouted commands, and over it all the electric whine of the tower winch, cranking forty feet of aluminum back down in a hurry, the big beam sinking out of the lightning-lit sky like a slow-motion fall. Dana's voice carried: *"Leave the coffee, MOVE."*

Then the first thunder rolled over the park, close enough to feel in the teeth, and they were out of time to watch. They sheltered in the cinderblock restroom building — four kids, two adults, one dog, and Pruitt, who had refused to go home. The rain hit like gravel on a tin roof. Lightning turned the little building's doorway into a camera flash, over and over, and Jun counted seconds under her breath between flash and bang — four, then two, then four again — while Resistor leaned against Si's leg and the weather radio murmured its flat litany of counties.

"Antennas are replaceable," Si said, to nobody, into the dark. "People aren't. That's the whole rule."

"Logged," said Priya, in the dark, and somebody laughed, and the cell spent twenty minutes being furious and then moved off east, trailing its lightning behind it like a cloak.

At one o'clock the rain was a drizzle and the warning had expired, and they walked back out into a washed world. The cottonwoods had shrugged and gone back to sleep. And the junk — the whole glorious junkyard — was *still up*: extension cord dripping but flying, EFHW intact, the J-pole's tripod unmoved on its stakes, the pop-can dipole beaded with rain and one-point-eight-to-one, probably forever. Low and lashed, the way Si had insisted on Friday, back when the sky had looked like nothing at all.

They reconnected the way they'd disconnected — deliberately, checking each SWR before a watt went anywhere — and were back on the air by 1:30. The graveyard shift belonged to forty meters and to a rotation Priya had drawn on the whiteboard, and the hours passed the way they pass at Field Day: rain-washed band, voices far off and calm, the percolator going around. Jun took the two-to-three-thirty slot on the Kenwood with Mr. O dozing upright in the control-operator chair, working a slow, patient string of night owls — nine contacts, none of them exciting, all of them hers. At four, Bea and the J-38 took over. At four-forty, Priya shook Jun awake with two words.

"It's time."

The whiteboard said it, in Priya's hand, calculated in February for exactly this: *SUNRISE 5:26 — TERMINATOR — D DEAD, F2 ALIVE — GO NORTHWEST.*

"The D layer's been dead all night," Priya said, walking her to the ten-meter wire. "It eats low bands by day and it's *gone* now. But the F2 is still charged, all along the sunrise line — and the line runs from here, northwest, to Japan, where it's sunset. For about an hour, the path is open and empty. It's the best propagation there is, and it happens while everyone sane is asleep."

The ten-meter wire went up to full height in five minutes, and the TS-520S warmed its tubes, and Jun sat in front of twenty-eight point four megahertz and listened to absolutely nothing.

"Patience," Mr. O said, from the control-operator chair, fully awake now, the way he'd been awake in January.

At 5:38, the static moved. It didn't build — it *lifted*, like a curtain, exactly the way it had at Si's farm in the winter — and out of it came a thin, wavering CQ, and Jun's hands knew the call's rhythm before her head did.

"Mr. O," she said. "That's—"

"Answer him," said Mr. O.

She pressed the mic. "JA1YMK, this is November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima, N8OWL, Field Day, over."

The pause was the width of the Pacific. Then: *"N8OWL! Maple Run school club! I remember — January, the young station. Very good morning to you — very good evening here. You are five-nine, Two Alpha?"*

"Five-nine, Two Alpha," Jun said, and her voice did not shake at all. "Good morning, Takeshi-san."

Then the curtain opened all the way, and Japan came through the ten-meter wire like a tide — JA after JA, stacked and eager, an entire country having its evening coffee on the other end of sixteen and a half feet of scrap. Jun worked the first dozen, steady, full calls, real reports. Then Theo slid in beside her — "the morning show," he murmured, but he ran it her way, by suffixes, calm — and between them and Bea's key they put thirty-eight JAs in the log in fifty minutes, until the sun cleared the cottonwoods, the D layer woke up hungry, and the curtain came back down.

---

Sunup found the whole club around the shelter table in the smell of Si's percolator coffee, which remained, defiantly, the worst coffee in three counties, and which everybody drank anyway, because it was Field Day and the coffee was part of the points.

Priya closed the laptop, checked her math twice, and announced: "Three hundred and twelve. Three duplicates removed. Three hundred and twelve contacts, on trash, through a storm."

Across the meadow, County Tech's tower was going back up in the morning light, two hours of cranking for two hours of storm. Jun never once heard them complain, which she respected — and never once wished she'd traded, which she respected more.

Ms. Delgado and the board chair arrived at seven-thirty — the chair with a box of donuts, which she set down without comment, the way you set down a flag after planting it. She looked at the log book. She looked at Bea, asleep in a camp chair with her hand still resting on the J-38, and at Theo, asleep sitting up, and at the wires dripping in the new light.

"Is it always like this?" she asked.

Mr. O considered. "Define 'like this.'"

"Children building radios out of garbage," the chair said, "and talking to Japan before breakfast."

"Then yes," said Mr. O. "On the good days, it is exactly like this."

She nodded, once, the nod of a person whose due diligence had just come back with a recommendation. "The board's summer session meets in July," she said, to Ms. Delgado, and took a donut for the road, which Jun filed away as a good sign.

In the truck, packing up, they heard it on Si's radio — the same line of storms, the cells that had washed over the park at midnight and kept going. Two counties over, one of them had put a tornado on the ground. Nobody was making jokes about it. The reporter's voice had the particular carefulness of damage still being counted.

Si turned the volume down and looked at the sky for a long moment — scrubbed blue, innocent as a postcard.

"Weather's not done," he said. "It never is. That's why we build before."

Jun loaded the last coil of coax and thought about the J-pole on the school roof, lashed and waiting, and about the whole patient invisible conversation humming along above everything, all the time. She would write it in the salvage log that night, entry two hundred and forty-something: *one Field Day, 312 contacts, one storm ridden out, one morning the sky opened.* But she already knew how the entry ended, because it ended the way every entry ended now.

Condition: better than it looked. Potential: everything.
