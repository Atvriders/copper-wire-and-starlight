## Appendix B: Glossary for Young Builders

Radio has the oldest, weirdest vocabulary of any hobby going — a hundred years of hams coining shorthand because dits were precious and airtime was shared. Every term here is defined in the story where it lands; this appendix is the pocket version, for the night you're up a ladder and somebody yells a word at you.

The math is real. The junk is real. The words are free.

---

- **Antenna.** Anything that turns the electrical wiggle in a wire into a radio wave, or catches one out of the air. A coat hanger counts. A soda can counts. Intent counts most.

- **Dipole.** A wire cut to a half wavelength and fed in the middle, so it has two equal legs. Total length in feet: 468 over the frequency in megahertz, 234 over per leg. Twenty meters at 14.100 MHz comes out to 33.2 feet — 16.6 a side.

- **Feedline / coax.** The cable carrying signal between radio and antenna. Coax — coaxial — wraps the signal wire in a braided shield so the RF stays in its lane. The club runs RG-58: cheap, bendy, forgiving.

- **Velocity factor.** Radio waves slow down inside cable — in RG-58, 0.66 of free-space speed, about two-thirds. Forget that when cutting cable to a calculated length and your math will lie to you exactly once.

- **Balun.** *Balanced-to-unbalanced*: a small transformer marrying a balanced antenna — a dipole's two equal legs — to unbalanced coax, so the current goes into the antenna instead of sightseeing along the cable's outside.

- **Unun.** The balun's cousin, *unbalanced-to-unbalanced*. Bea's end-fed wire uses a 49:1 unun — two turns to fourteen on an FT240-43 core — to bring the impedance at the end of a half-wave wire down to what a radio can stomach.

- **Choke balun.** Not a transformer — just coax coiled into loops at the feedpoint: eight turns, six inches across, on the club's HF dipoles. It chokes RF off the outside of the cable. Ugly, free, works.

- **SWR.** Standing-wave ratio: how much of your power leaves the antenna versus how much bounces back into the radio like a returned sandwich. 1:1 is perfect; under 2:1 is fine; 1.6:1 is worth celebrating.

- **Resonance.** The frequency where the antenna's length and the wave's length agree, and the antenna drinks your power instead of arguing. Cut right, it's the dip where SWR falls. Cut wrong, you learn to trim.

- **Wavelength.** The physical length of one complete wave. At 146 MHz the wave is two meters long; a quarter wave is 19.3 inches, four root-beer cans a side. The wire doesn't care what it used to hold.

- **Frequency.** How many waves pass per second, counted in hertz. 146.760 million is the Maple Run repeater; 14.100 million is where the twenty-meter dipole sings.

- **Bands.** Neighborhoods of the spectrum, named by wavelength: 80, 40, 20, 15, 10 meters. 40 is a night owl; 10 sleeps through quiet solar years, then, near a cycle peak, throws the best party on the planet.

- **HF.** High frequency, 3–30 MHz — the shortwave bands that bounce off the sky to other continents. The TS-520S lives here, 80 through 10 meters. So does Argentina at two in the morning.

- **VHF.** Very high frequency, 30–300 MHz: the two-meter band, the repeater, the J-pole, every handheld with a rubber antenna. Mostly line-of-sight — local, polite, dependable.

- **UHF.** Ultra high frequency, 300 MHz–3 GHz — the 70-centimeter band and up. Shorter waves, smaller antennas, same physics.

- **Ionosphere.** Sun-charged layers of the upper atmosphere that bend shortwave signals back to Earth. The D layer absorbs by day and eats the low bands at noon; E rides near 100 km, F2 near 300 km. The sun charges it — and can take it away.

- **Skip / skywave.** The bounce: your signal ricochets off the ionosphere and lands hundreds or thousands of miles away — two thousand miles a bounce, sometimes twice. Stations too close for the landing can't hear you; that's the skip zone.

- **Gray line.** The twilight strip along the sunrise/sunset terminator, where the D layer has gone home but F2 is still charged. For an hour or so, paths along it open wide on 10 and 15 meters — and Osaka is right there.

- **MUF.** Maximum usable frequency — the highest the ionosphere will bounce back on a given path. It climbs with solar activity; near the peak of cycle 25, ten meters stops being a rumor and starts being Japan.

- **Gain.** How strongly an antenna concentrates signal in one direction — cupped hands around a mouth. Gain adds no power; it just sends what you have where you point.

- **dB.** Decibel — the logarithmic unit hams measure everything in. 3 dB doubles the power; 10 dB makes it ten times. The tape yagi's 7 dB over a dipole makes a five-watt handheld sound five times stronger.

- **Directivity.** An antenna's talent for hearing and sending better in some directions than others. A dipole favors its broadside; a yagi favors where it's pointed.

- **Yagi.** The classic rooftop shape: driven element, longer reflector behind, shorter directors ahead, on a boom. Cut from a 25-foot tape measure — reflector 41 3/8 inches, director 35 1/8 — for about 7 dB of gain and a fox behind the sledding hill.

- **Polarization.** The orientation of the wave's electric field, set by the antenna's. Arrive horizontal at a vertical repeater and your signal misses the door; tilt a yagi wrong and a satellite vanishes mid-sentence.

- **Ground plane.** The other half a vertical antenna works against — radials, a car roof, the earth itself. A 19.3-inch whip over a decent ground plane is a real antenna; over nothing, a science project.

- **J-pole.** A half-wave vertical with a quarter-wave matching stub at the bottom — copper pipe. Radiator 57½ inches, stub 19, feed point slid 2–3 inches up the stub until the SWR smiles. Matches itself; wants no ground plane; rides out March storms.

- **Magnetic loop.** A small loop tuned knife-sharp with a variable capacitor. The club's: three feet across, ¾-inch copper pipe, 0.09 of a wavelength on forty meters, capacitor from a dead AM radio. Fifteen kHz of bandwidth, endless retuning — and it works from a balcony disguised as a plant stand.

- **Antenna tuner.** A box of adjustable coils and capacitors that lets your radio *believe* a non-resonant antenna is behaving. It doesn't fix the antenna; it brokers the peace. It's how two attic Slinkies load up on forty meters — barely.

- **Repeater.** A relay on a hilltop that listens on one frequency and re-shouts on another, so a five-watt handheld covers a county. Maple Run's is W7MR/R, output 146.760 MHz.

- **Offset.** The split between a repeater's listening and talking frequencies — 600 kHz on two meters. You transmit on 146.160; W7MR answers on 146.760.

- **PL tone.** A low, steady tone — 100.0 Hz for W7MR — hidden under your voice to open the repeater's squelch. You never hear it; the repeater won't wake without it.

- **Net.** A scheduled on-air meeting with a script and a boss — net control — who takes check-ins in order. Stiff until you hear one run. Then it's choreography.

- **Skywarn.** Volunteer storm-spotting: trained hams radio hail, wind, and funnel reports to the National Weather Service. The discipline is the point — report facts, from a grounded station, or stay off the air.

- **RFI.** Radio-frequency interference: your signal showing up uninvited in powered speakers, turntables, the neighbor's patience. Usually their cheap cables act as antennas; sometimes your coax radiates where it shouldn't. Solvable — politely, which makes fans.

- **Ferrite.** A dark ceramic that eats high-frequency current. Snap-on ferrite chokes clipped around a cable strangle stray RF without touching the wanted signal — the interference detective's toolkit, sold by the bag.

- **Grounding.** Connecting the station to the actual earth — a rod, a short fat wire — so lightning and fault current go somewhere better than through you. Safety first; everything else is commentary.

- **Bonding.** Tying every metal box in the station — rig, tuner, ground bus — to the same ground, so nothing disagrees about where zero is. Grounding is the earth; bonding is the family meeting.

- **CQ.** The universal radio hello — *calling any station*. You send it into the sky, and the sky decides whether tonight somebody answers.

- **Call sign.** Your government-issued radio name — N8OWL, W7JUNK, someday one with you inside it — unique on Earth. Say it every ten minutes and at the end of every contact; the frequency belongs to nobody and everybody.

- **Control operator.** The licensed person responsible for a transmission. Until you pass your exam, one sits beside you while you talk — a legal, traditional third-party contact. Every ham you meet started exactly that way.

- **Phonetics.** The ITU alphabet — November Eight Oscar Whiskey Lima — so your letters survive static. Kilo, Whiskey, Sierra beats "B as in... boy? Bob?" every time.

- **RST.** The signal report: Readability 1–5, Strength 1–9, Tone 1–9 on CW. "59" is top marks, and everybody is always 59 — polite, like waving in traffic. Give the honest number; someone may be trimming an antenna by it.

- **QSL card.** A postcard proving a contact happened: call signs, date, frequency, report. Traded by mail for a century; a corkboard of them is a club's family tree.

- **Q-codes.** Three-letter shorthand from the telegraph era, older than most countries on the map. QTH is your location, QSL is *confirmed*, QRP is low power. They saved dits and outlived the telegraph.

- **DX.** Distance — any faraway contact, and the farther the better. Denver is a thrill; Japan is DX in capital letters.

- **CW.** Continuous wave — Morse code, the oldest digital mode: a carrier keyed on and off in dits and dahs. Your power lands in a whisper of bandwidth, so it gets through when voices can't. Learn it slow and it turns into music.

- **QRP.** The Q-code for "reduce power," grown into a religion: five watts or less, a wire, and stubbornness. Proof the antenna matters more than the amplifier.

- **Field Day.** The last full weekend of June: hams haul radios into parks and run on emergency power for twenty-four hours straight. Part contest, part disaster drill, part campout. Bring bug spray and every adapter you own.

- **Elmer.** A mentor — the ham who lends the analyzer, answers questions, and asks the one question back that makes you see the fix yourself. Si Webb, W7JUNK, is the illustration.

- **Silent key.** A ham who has died; the key goes quiet. The call sign retires into logbooks and QSL cards — and sometimes, like N6RUA, into a granddaughter's hands.

- **73.** "Best regards," from the old landline telegraph code. It's plural already — *seventy-three*, never *seventy-threes* — it ends nearly every contact you'll ever make.

---

That's the pocket dictionary: fifty-odd terms, not a password among them. Learn them the way the club did, one build at a time, and the next time somebody on the air says *QSL, 73*, you'll hear what they mean — *I heard you. Best regards. Your turn.*
